## 7. Oil Sands: How Extraction Differs from Conventional Production

Conventional oil is a liquid at reservoir conditions. It flows through a wellbore under natural pressure or with a pump, requires relatively little surface infrastructure, and costs ~$15–25/bbl to produce. Oil sands bitumen is essentially solid at surface temperatures (too viscous to flow) and requires either excavation or in-situ thermal stimulation before it can be moved. This fundamental difference drives higher cost, energy use, and environmental intensity.

### Method 1: Surface Mining (for deposits shallower than ~75 metres)

- Giant hydraulic shovels load oil sand into trucks (up to 400-tonne capacity)
- Material transported to crushers, then to extraction plants where hot water separates bitumen from sand and clay (gravity/flotation process)
- Raw bitumen is then either upgraded to **Synthetic Crude Oil (SCO)** or diluted with condensate/naphtha to form **dilbit** (diluted bitumen) for pipeline transport
- Only ~3% of the oil sands area (containing ~20% of recoverable oil) is accessible by mining
- Produces large **tailings ponds** (liquid waste — a significant environmental liability)
- ~50% of current bitumen production uses this method at large integrated mines (Suncor, Syncrude, Canadian Natural Resources Horizon)

### Method 2: In-Situ — SAGD (for deposits deeper than ~75 metres)

- **Steam-Assisted Gravity Drainage (SAGD)** is the dominant method
- Two parallel horizontal wells are drilled into the reservoir, one a few metres above the other
- High-pressure steam is continuously injected into the **upper well**, heating the surrounding bitumen and reducing its viscosity until it drains by gravity
- The mobilised bitumen flows into the **lower production well** and is pumped to surface
- ~80% of total oil sands reserves (the deep majority) can only be produced this way
- Much smaller surface footprint than mining; no tailings ponds
- However, **steam generation is energy-intensive** — typically requires burning natural gas — producing significantly more GHGs per barrel than conventional production

### Key Comparisons

| Factor | Conventional Oil | Oil Sands (SAGD) | Oil Sands (Mining) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Resource state | Liquid in reservoir | Solid/viscous | Solid/viscous |
| Water use | Minimal | High (steam) | Very high (hot water extraction) |
| Surface footprint | Small | Moderate | Very large |
| GHG intensity | Baseline | ~20–40% higher/bbl | ~20–30% higher/bbl |
| Operating cost | $15–25/bbl | $25–45/bbl | $20–35/bbl (at scale) |
| Upgrading needed | Usually no | Yes (dilbit or SCO) | Yes (dilbit or SCO) |

## 8. Western Canadian Select (WCS) Price Discount

**WCS** is the benchmark price for Canadian heavy oil — a blend of oil sands bitumen and conventional heavy crude. It trades at a persistent discount to **WTI** (West Texas Intermediate, the North American light crude benchmark) for two structural reasons:

1. **Quality:** WCS is heavy (~20.5° API) and high-sulphur (~3.5% vs WTI's 0.4%). Only refineries equipped with coking and hydrocracking units can process it profitably. This limits the pool of buyers.
2. **Transport cost:** Moving crude from landlocked Alberta to refineries adds ~$10/bbl vs WTI's Gulf Coast delivery point.

The discount *beyond* these fundamentals reflects **pipeline capacity constraints** — when production exceeds takeaway capacity, producers accept lower prices rather than shut in.

| Period | WCS-WTI Differential | Context |
|---|---|---|
| Typical range | $10–20/bbl | Normal market conditions |
| November 2018 (crisis) | ~$50/bbl | Enbridge Line 3 outage + pipeline bottleneck; AB government intervened with production curtailments |
| 2023 (pre-TMX) | ~$18.65/bbl average | Pipeline-constrained; markets pricing in bottleneck |
| 2024 (post-TMX) | ~$14.73/bbl average | TMX expansion provided Pacific egress relief |
| March 2025 | Below $10/bbl | TMX fully ramping; Asian buyers competing |

**TMX impact:** Year 1 of the Trans Mountain Expansion (May 2024–May 2025) generated an estimated **C$13 billion in additional revenue** for Canadian producers compared to the pre-TMX status quo. The narrowed discount is the most direct financial benefit of the C$34 billion expansion project.

**Why this matters to the industry:** Every $1 change in the WCS-WTI differential translates to approximately C$1.8 billion/year in producer revenue. The discount history is why every major new pipeline is so commercially significant.

## 9. Export Markets

Canada exports approximately 4.3–4.5 MMb/d of crude oil (2024–2025). Despite being the world's 4th largest crude producer, the US has historically absorbed virtually all exports — a structural vulnerability now actively being addressed.

### Destination Breakdown (May 2024 – April 2025)

| Market | Share | Volume (approx.) | Route |
|---|---|---|---|
| United States | 93.8% | ~4.1 MMb/d | Enbridge Mainline (Midwest); Keystone (Midwest/PADD 4); TMX maritime (West Coast) |
| China | ~3–4% | ~150–200 kb/d | TMX → Westridge terminal → tanker |
| Singapore/Hong Kong | ~1–2% | ~80–100 kb/d | TMX maritime |
| South Korea / Japan / India | <1% | ~60–80 kb/d combined | TMX maritime |
| Other | <0.5% | Minimal | |

**TMX transformation:** Before the Trans Mountain Expansion (pre-May 2024), non-US exports were virtually zero (~80 kb/d). By early 2025, ~400 kb/d was reaching non-US markets. Key buyers:
- **China:** C$5.9 billion (May 2024–Sep 2025) — 61% of all Indo-Pacific exports
- **Singapore:** C$1.6 billion; **Hong Kong:** C$1.5 billion; **South Korea:** C$411M; **India:** C$158M

**US Midwest remains critical:** Enbridge Mainline delivers 3+ MMb/d to US PADD 2 refineries designed specifically for Canadian heavy crude. These refineries have invested billions in coking capacity and *cannot easily switch feedstocks* — creating mutual dependency. The US imports ~60% of its crude from Canada (~6.6 mb/d total), making Canadian supply essential to US Midwest gasoline prices.

**Strategic shift underway:** Trump's tariff threats (2025) and "51st state" rhetoric accelerated Canadian diversification efforts. Alberta is in active discussions with South Korea, Japan, and European nations. China overtook the US as Canada's largest crude customer by *value* in certain months of early 2025 via TMX, though the US retains overwhelming volume dominance.

## 13. Transportation & Pipelines

Canada's oil export infrastructure is overwhelmingly pipeline-based. The pipeline network is the single most important determinant of Canadian crude pricing, export volumes, and market access.

### Major Existing Pipelines

| Pipeline | Operator | Capacity | Route | Utilisation (2025) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Enbridge Mainline** | Enbridge | ~3.0–3.1 MMb/d | Hardisty/Edmonton AB → US Midwest (PADD 2) + eastern Canada | ~95% |
| **Trans Mountain (TMX)** | Crown/Canada | 890 kb/d | Edmonton AB → Burnaby BC (Pacific) | ~88% |
| **Keystone** | TC Energy | ~620–640 kb/d | Hardisty AB → US Midwest / Cushing OK | ~94–100% |
| **Express Pipeline** | Cenovus/others | ~280 kb/d | Hardisty AB → US Rockies (PADD 4) | High |

**Enbridge Mainline** is the backbone of Canadian oil exports — the world's longest heavy crude pipeline network, moving ~3 MMb/d from Alberta to refineries as far as Chicago and Detroit. It is running near capacity.

**Trans Mountain Expansion** came online May 2024, nearly tripling the original 300 kb/d pipeline to 890 kb/d. Total project cost: ~C$34.2 billion (government-owned). It provides the only tidewater route from Alberta to the Pacific, enabling Asian crude exports for the first time at scale. Not yet full — utilisation at ~88% as Asian marketing continues to develop.

**Keystone** (not Keystone XL, which was cancelled by Biden in 2021) runs at 94–100% utilisation and is essentially full. A significant operational incident occurred April 2025 (oil release in North Dakota), temporarily shutting the line.

### Combined Export Capacity (H1 2025)

Total pipeline exports: **~4.6 MMb/d**, leaving approximately **250 kb/d of spare capacity** — mostly on TMX, with small amounts on Keystone and Express. Canada is close to being pipeline-constrained again at current and projected production growth rates.

## 14. Environment & Regulation

Canadian oil production operates under overlapping federal and provincial regulatory frameworks, and the balance between them is a source of significant ongoing political tension.

### Carbon Pricing

| Mechanism | Status (April 2026) | Applies to |
|---|---|---|
| Consumer fuel charge ("carbon tax") | **Eliminated March 2025** by PM Carney | Was applied to transport fuels, heating — abolished under economic/political pressure |
| Industrial OBPS (Output-Based Pricing System) | **Retained** | Large industrial emitters; oil sands operations pay on emissions above efficiency benchmarks |
| Alberta TIER regulation | Active | Alberta's own equivalent industrial pricing regime |

### Federal Oil & Gas Sector Emissions Cap

The Trudeau/Carney government developed a cap-and-trade system specifically for upstream oil and gas:

- **Scope:** Large upstream operators >365,000 boe/year (covers oil sands, conventional, offshore, gas processing)
- **Phase-in:** 2026–2029 (registration and reporting only)
- **First compliance period:** 2030–2032
- **2030 target:** 106–112 Mt CO2e — **35–38% below 2019 emission levels**
- **Mechanism:** Operators receive tradeable allowances; can buy from others, use offset credits (up to 20%), or contribute to a decarbonisation fund
- **Alberta's position:** Premier Danielle Smith has challenged the federal cap as unconstitutional intrusion on provincial resource jurisdiction; legal challenges ongoing

### Alberta Oil Sands Emissions Cap

Alberta has its own legislated emissions limit: a maximum of **100 Mt/year** on oil sands operations, with a C$30/tonne carbon price applied above efficiency benchmarks. Industry argues the federal cap would constrain production growth beyond what Alberta's own regulation already achieves.

### Impact Assessment (Bill C-69)

The federal *Impact Assessment Act* requires environmental assessments for designated major projects (including pipelines and oil sands expansions). In October 2023, the Supreme Court of Canada found parts of the Act unconstitutional as they intruded on provincial jurisdiction. The government revised the legislation; the core federal assessment powers remain. This law is a key factor in the timeline for any new pipeline or oil sands project receiving regulatory approval.

### Industry Outlook on Regulation

The federal emissions cap — if implemented as drafted — would require significant reductions from a sector that has been growing production. Industry bodies (CAPP) argue that emissions intensity (per barrel) has improved significantly over two decades, and that an absolute cap penalises growth unfairly. The resolution of this tension between production growth and absolute emissions limits is the central regulatory question facing the sector through 2030.

## 15. Donald Trump's Second Term: Impact on Canadian Energy

### The Tariff Timeline

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| Jan 20, 2025 | Trump inaugurated; immediately signals tariff action on Canada |
| Feb 1, 2025 | Executive order: 25% tariffs on most Canadian goods; **10% on Canadian energy** (lower to protect US gasoline prices) |
| Mar–Nov 2025 | On-again/off-again tariffs; Canada retaliates with C$30bn+ counter-tariffs (excluding energy to protect own producers) |
| Feb 20, 2026 | **US Supreme Court rules 6–3** in *Learning Resources, Inc. v. Trump*: IEEPA does not authorise tariffs; all IEEPA-based tariffs struck down |
| Feb 24, 2026 | IEEPA tariffs terminated |
| Feb 24, 2026 | Trump replaces with 10% global surcharge under Section 122 (Trade Act 1974); **energy products exempted** |

**Current status (April 2026):** Canadian energy exports are effectively **tariff-free**. Remaining tariffs affecting Canada are under Section 232 (steel 25%, aluminium 25%, autos 25%) — these were not IEEPA-based and were not struck down by the Court.

### Practical Impact on the Oil Industry

| Issue | Impact |
|---|---|
| Direct tariff cost (energy at 10%) | ~$9–10 billion/year on Canadian energy exports — passed to US refiners, ultimately consumers (~15¢/gallon added to gasoline) |
| US refiner exposure | US Midwest refiners designed for Canadian heavy crude had **no short-term alternative supply** — could not easily source WCS-equivalent elsewhere |
| Investment uncertainty | Materially more damaging than the actual tariff: capital allocation decisions deferred; expansion projects paused pending clarity |
| WCS discount widening | Tariff uncertainty widened the WCS-WTI differential temporarily in early 2025 before TMX marketing offset this |
| Diversification acceleration | Confirmed the strategic logic of TMX; Canadian producers accelerated Asian marketing with urgency rather than opportunism |

### What the Industry Is Doing Differently

1. **Asian market development:** Canadian Natural Resources, Suncor, Cenovus, and Pembina all accelerated Pacific Basin marketing through TMX. This is now a core commercial strategy, not a hedge.
2. **New pipeline advocacy:** The oil industry and Alberta government have moved from passive pipeline support to active lobbying for multiple new corridors — particularly eastward and to additional Pacific tidewater points.
3. **Eastern Canada market:** Proposals to connect Alberta crude to Ontario and Maritime refineries (replacing imported crude with domestic) are gaining traction. Irving's Saint John refinery importing 100% foreign crude while Canadian oil is surplus is increasingly difficult to defend strategically.
4. **LNG Canada linkage:** While LNG Canada (Kitimat, BC — natural gas) is not oil, its first-cargo milestone (2025–2026) demonstrates that Pacific export infrastructure for Canadian hydrocarbons works. It validates the model for future oil investment in coastal export terminals.
5. **US vulnerability acknowledged:** The "single customer" risk to 94% of oil exports — previously treated as an acceptable, stable arrangement — is now treated as a structural risk requiring active mitigation.
