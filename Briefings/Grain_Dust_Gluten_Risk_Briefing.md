# Grain Dust Exposure: Risk Assessment for Employees with Gluten-Related Conditions

*6 May 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

This briefing assesses the risk of harm to an office-based employee working on an industrial grain storage site, where active grain drying and loading/unloading operations take place. The office is located approximately 50 yards from the grain storage sheds. Windows are open in summer; shed doors are open during the day. Wind direction is variable.

Three conditions are assessed separately because they have distinct biological mechanisms and different relationships to airborne grain dust exposure.

| Condition | Exposure Route | Likelihood of Harm (Summer) | Severity if Triggered | Overall Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coeliac Disease | Inhalation → mucociliary clearance → ingestion (indirect) | Low–Moderate | High | **Moderate** |
| Gluten Allergy (IgE-mediated) | Direct inhalation | Moderate–High | Very High | **High** |
| Gluten Intolerance (NCGS) | Ingestion only — no inhalation mechanism | Very Low | Low–Moderate | **Low** |

**Key finding:** The three conditions present materially different risk profiles. Gluten allergy carries the highest airborne risk because it is triggered directly by inhaled grain proteins. Coeliac disease carries a meaningful but lower risk via an indirect inhalation pathway. Gluten intolerance presents negligible airborne risk based on current scientific evidence.

## 1. Site Context

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| Grain types stored | Wheat, barley, and other grains |
| Active operations | Grain drying; vehicle loading/unloading |
| Distance: office to sheds | ~50 yards (~46 metres) |
| Office ventilation | Windows open in summer |
| Shed ventilation | Doors open during the day |
| Prevailing wind direction | Variable / unknown |
| Season of highest risk | Summer (open windows, peak drying season) |

Grain drying and loading/unloading are among the highest-dust-generating operations in agricultural settings. Grain dryers force hot air through bulk grain, exhausting fine particulate-laden air. Vehicle tipping and auger/conveyor transfer during loading/unloading generate significant dust plumes at point of transfer. Both operations produce airborne particles small enough to remain suspended and travel meaningful distances.

## 2. The Science of Each Condition

### 2a. Coeliac Disease

**What it is:** An autoimmune condition in which the ingestion of gluten (a protein composite found in wheat, barley, rye, and related grains) triggers an immune response that damages the lining of the small intestine. UK prevalence is approximately 1 in 100 people.

**Target organ:** The small intestine — not the lungs. Coeliac disease is an intestinal condition. The airway is not the site of autoimmune activation.

**Dose threshold:** The landmark clinical trial by Catassi et al. (2007) established that:
- **50 mg gluten/day** caused significant villous atrophy in the majority of coeliac patients within 3 months
- **10 mg gluten/day** produced variable responses — some patients showed mucosal damage; others did not
- Individual sensitivity varies considerably; some patients react to amounts below 10 mg/day
- For reference, a single slice of standard bread contains approximately 2,000–3,000 mg of gluten

Sources: <a href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17209192/" target="_blank">Catassi C, Fabiani E, Iacono G, et al. A prospective, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial to establish a safe gluten threshold for patients with celiac disease. Am J Clin Nutr. 2007;85(1):160–166.</a>

**Inhalation pathway — how it works:** Grain dust particles inhaled through the nose and mouth are not absorbed through lung tissue. Instead, the respiratory tract's mucociliary escalator — a continuous layer of mucus propelled by hair-like cilia — traps inhaled particles and carries them upward toward the throat, where they are swallowed. Gluten delivered this way reaches the small intestine, where it can trigger the same autoimmune response as ingested gluten. This indirect pathway is the mechanism by which airborne grain dust poses a risk to coeliac disease sufferers.

**Critical evidence — the NEJM 2007 case:** Two farmers with non-responsive coeliac disease (i.e., ongoing intestinal damage despite following a strict gluten-free diet) were found to be inhaling and subsequently swallowing large quantities of grain dust daily while feeding cattle in enclosed spaces. The grain feed contained approximately 30% barley, 11% wheat, and 11% maize. Based on average daily handling of approximately 5 kg of feed, the paper estimates exposure to **greater than 150 grams of gluten-containing dust particles per day**. The actual gluten protein fraction within this dust was far in excess of the safe dietary threshold of under 10 mg/day for coeliac patients. Following implementation of face mask use, both patients showed significant histological improvement; one achieved near-normal intestinal findings.

Sources: <a href="https://www.nejm.org/doi/full/10.1056/NEJMc063112" target="_blank">Kasim S, Moriarty KJ, Liston R. Nonresponsive celiac disease due to inhaled gluten. N Engl J Med. 2007;356(24):2548–2549. DOI: 10.1056/NEJMc063112</a>

**Relevance to this scenario:** The NEJM case involved extreme occupational exposure (enclosed space, direct handling) many orders of magnitude greater than an office worker 50 yards from an open storage shed. However, it establishes that the inhalation pathway is biologically real and clinically significant. The key question for this site is whether grain dust concentrations at the office reach levels sufficient to deliver meaningful gluten doses after mucociliary clearance and swallowing. No published studies directly measure airborne gluten concentrations at 50 yards from active grain drying/loading operations. This is an acknowledged evidence gap. Risk must therefore be assessed qualitatively based on proximity, dust-generating operations, and seasonal ventilation.

**Symptoms in an airborne proximity scenario:**
- **Gastrointestinal (delayed — hours to days):** Abdominal pain, bloating, diarrhoea, constipation — reflecting intestinal inflammation from indirectly ingested gluten
- **Silent damage:** Mucosal damage can occur without any acute symptoms; an employee may not experience noticeable effects while ongoing intestinal harm accumulates over time
- **Ocular and respiratory:** Coeliac disease does **not** cause watery eyes, rhinitis, sneezing, or respiratory symptoms as a direct consequence of the condition. If these symptoms occur near the grain sheds, they indicate a separate IgE-mediated allergy, not coeliac disease
- **Skin:** A minority of coeliac patients have dermatitis herpetiformis — a blistering, intensely itchy skin rash triggered by gluten ingestion — which could be aggravated if gluten is delivered via the mucociliary route in sufficient quantity

### 2b. Gluten Allergy (IgE-Mediated Wheat Allergy / Occupational Grain Asthma)

**What it is:** An immediate-type (Type I) hypersensitivity reaction mediated by immunoglobulin E (IgE) antibodies. This is an allergic mechanism, entirely distinct from the autoimmune mechanism of coeliac disease. The two conditions should not be conflated.

**Critically different from coeliac disease:** Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition targeting the gut; IgE-mediated wheat allergy is an allergic condition that can target the airways, skin, and cardiovascular system. A person can have coeliac disease, wheat allergy, both, or neither.

**Trigger route — inhalation is primary:** Unlike coeliac disease, IgE-mediated wheat allergy can be triggered directly by inhaling grain proteins — ingestion is not required. This is the mechanism behind baker's asthma, one of the most prevalent occupational respiratory diseases in the UK.

**Epidemiology:** Occupational asthma caused by grain dust exposure affects an estimated 5–10% of workers with regular, direct grain handling exposure. Rhinitis (nasal inflammation) affects a higher proportion (~15–20%). In grain storage and agricultural settings, workers exposed to wheat and barley dust are at material risk of developing sensitisation.

**Key allergens:** The principal sensitising proteins in wheat and barley dust are α-amylase/trypsin inhibitors (ATIs), lipid transfer proteins, gliadins (a component of gluten), and peroxidases. Once sensitised, an individual may react to very low airborne concentrations.

**Symptoms in an airborne proximity scenario (onset: minutes to 2 hours after exposure):**

| Symptom | Description | Severity |
|---|---|---|
| Allergic conjunctivitis | Watery, itchy, red eyes | Mild–Moderate |
| Rhinitis | Sneezing, runny or blocked nose, nasal congestion | Mild–Moderate |
| Coughing | Dry or productive cough | Mild–Moderate |
| Wheezing / chest tightness | Airway narrowing; asthma exacerbation in sensitised individuals | Moderate–Severe |
| Urticaria | Raised, itchy hives on the skin | Moderate |
| Angioedema | Swelling of face, lips, or throat | Severe |
| Anaphylaxis | Systemic collapse — drop in blood pressure, loss of consciousness | Life-threatening |

**Timing and pattern:** Symptoms typically begin within 1–120 minutes of exposure. A clinically significant pattern is marked improvement on days away from the site (weekends, holidays) and recurrence on return — this is diagnostically important and should be recorded if it occurs.

Sources: <a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10156020/" target="_blank">Sayed KM, Ali AH. IgE Mediated Wheat Allergy Associated with Resistant and Severe Forms of Allergic Conjunctivitis. Open Respir Med J. 2022.</a>

**Risk at this site:** An employee with confirmed IgE-mediated wheat allergy or occupational grain asthma faces the highest risk of the three conditions considered in this briefing. Grain drying and loading/unloading generate the allergen-carrying airborne particles, and at 50 yards with open windows and variable wind, meaningful concentrations can reach the office, particularly during active operations.

Sources: <a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3810540/" target="_blank">Baur X, Deegen E. Diagnosis and Management of Grain-Induced Asthma. PMC3810540. Allergo J Int. 2014.</a>

### 2c. Gluten Intolerance (Non-Coeliac Gluten Sensitivity, NCGS)

**What it is:** A clinical condition in which symptoms (typically gastrointestinal — bloating, abdominal pain, altered bowel habit — and sometimes extra-intestinal) are triggered by gluten ingestion, in the absence of coeliac disease or IgE-mediated wheat allergy. Diagnosis is made by exclusion.

**Mechanism:** The biological mechanism of NCGS is not fully established. Proposed mechanisms include reactions to amylase-trypsin inhibitors (ATIs), fructans (a type of FODMAP), or a nocebo effect. It does not involve intestinal autoimmunity (unlike coeliac disease) or IgE-mediated sensitisation (unlike wheat allergy).

**Inhalation risk:** There is no published scientific evidence that NCGS can be triggered by inhalation of grain dust or airborne gluten. The condition is understood to be driven by ingestion. Blinded placebo-controlled trials have shown that up to 30% of self-reported NCGS sufferers do not react to gluten when the gluten source is concealed, suggesting a significant nocebo component in some cases.

**Symptoms in an airborne proximity scenario:**
No symptoms are expected from airborne exposure alone. If an employee with NCGS experiences ocular symptoms (watery or itchy eyes) or respiratory symptoms (wheezing, rhinitis) near the grain sheds, these are **not** attributable to NCGS — they indicate a separate IgE-mediated wheat allergy that warrants medical investigation. The gastrointestinal and extra-intestinal symptoms of NCGS (bloating, abdominal pain, altered bowel habit, fatigue, brain fog, headache, joint pain) would only arise from ingestion of meaningful quantities of gluten, which at 50 yards from an open shed is unlikely via the airborne route alone.

**Risk at this site:** Based on current scientific understanding, an office worker with NCGS faces negligible airborne risk from proximity to grain storage operations. Standard workplace hygiene precautions (hand washing before eating, no food consumption in dusty areas) are sufficient.

Sources: <a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6182669/" target="_blank">Catassi C, et al. Recent advances in understanding non-celiac gluten sensitivity. F1000Res. 2018;PMC6182669.</a>

### 2d. Symptom Comparison Summary

The table below summarises expected symptoms by condition for an office worker in this specific scenario (50 yards from active grain drying/loading, open windows, summer).

| Symptom | Coeliac Disease | Gluten Allergy (IgE) | Gluten Intolerance (NCGS) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Watery / itchy eyes** | No | Yes | No |
| **Sneezing / runny nose** | No | Yes | No |
| **Coughing / wheezing** | No | Yes | No |
| **Chest tightness / asthma** | No | Yes | No |
| **Skin hives (urticaria)** | No | Yes | No |
| **Blistering skin rash** | Possible (dermatitis herpetiformis, minority) | No | No |
| **Abdominal pain / bloating** | Yes (delayed) | Possible if ingested | Possible if ingested |
| **Diarrhoea / altered bowel** | Yes (delayed) | Possible if ingested | Possible if ingested |
| **Fatigue / brain fog** | Yes (cumulative) | No | Possible if ingested |

> **Key point for managers and first aiders:** Watery eyes, rhinitis, and respiratory symptoms near the grain sheds are signs of IgE-mediated allergy — not coeliac disease or gluten intolerance. An employee with coeliac disease who develops these symptoms should be assessed for a co-existing wheat allergy, which is a separate and distinct condition.

## 3. Exposure Pathway Analysis

```
Grain dryer exhaust / Loading/unloading tip point
          ↓ (dust cloud generated)
Airborne particulate dispersal (wind-dependent)
          ↓ (~50 yards, concentration attenuates with distance)
Open shed doors → open office windows
          ↓
Inhalation by office occupant
          ↓
Pathway A (Coeliac): Mucociliary escalator → swallowed → small intestine
Pathway B (IgE Allergy): Direct airway contact → IgE reaction → symptoms
Pathway C (NCGS): No established mechanism beyond ingestion
```

**Dust generation intensity by operation:**

| Operation | Relative Dust Generation | Peak Risk Period |
|---|---|---|
| Grain drying (active) | High — continuous exhaust particulate from dryer | Harvest season (Aug–Oct) |
| Loading/unloading | Very high — point-source dust plume during tipping | Throughout storage season |
| Static storage (no handling) | Very low — minimal dust generation | Baseline |

**Distance attenuation:** Airborne particulate concentrations decrease with distance from the source. At 50 yards, concentrations at the office will be substantially lower than at the source — but with active grain drying or loading/unloading, open windows, and unfavourable wind direction, transient elevated concentrations at the office are plausible, particularly during summer.

**Seasonal variation:** Risk is materially higher in summer (open windows and doors, peak grain handling season) than in winter (closed windows, reduced operations).

## 4. Risk Assessment Matrix

| Factor | Coeliac Disease | Gluten Allergy (IgE) | Gluten Intolerance (NCGS) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Exposure route** | Inhalation → mucociliary clearance → swallowing | Direct inhalation of grain proteins | Ingestion only |
| **Dose required for harm** | ≥10 mg/day gluten (variable; some patients <10 mg) | Low — sensitised individuals may react to very low concentrations | Unknown; ingestion-driven |
| **Likelihood — summer, active operations, downwind** | Low–Moderate | Moderate–High | Very Low |
| **Likelihood — winter, closed windows** | Very Low | Low | Negligible |
| **Severity of harm** | High — ongoing small intestinal damage, malabsorption, long-term nutritional consequences | Very High — asthma attack, anaphylaxis (potentially fatal) | Low–Moderate — GI symptoms, fatigue |
| **Risk rating (summer, active ops)** | **Moderate** | **High** | **Low** |
| **Key aggravating factor** | Duration of exposure; proximity to dryer exhaust | Previous sensitisation; degree of airway reactivity | None identified |
| **Key mitigating factor** | Inefficiency of mucociliary-to-swallowing route; distance from source | Distance from source; engineering controls | No airborne mechanism |

> **Note on risk ratings — why gluten allergy rates higher than coeliac disease in this context:**
> This may appear counterintuitive, as coeliac disease is widely regarded as the more serious gluten-related condition. However, these ratings assess **airborne proximity risk specifically**, not the general severity of each condition.
>
> For coeliac disease, the airborne exposure pathway is indirect and inefficient: grain dust must be inhaled, trapped by the mucociliary escalator, carried up to the throat, swallowed, and then deliver sufficient gluten (≥10 mg) to the small intestine to cause damage. Each stage reduces the effective dose. At 50 yards, reaching the damage threshold via this route is possible but uncertain.
>
> For IgE-mediated gluten allergy, grain proteins act directly on sensitised airway tissue the moment they are inhaled — no ingestion is required. The concentration threshold for triggering a reaction in a sensitised individual is also far lower than the coeliac damage dose. In an airborne exposure scenario, these two factors combine to produce a higher likelihood of harm. The severity rating is also higher because anaphylaxis — a possible outcome of an IgE reaction — can be life-threatening within minutes, whereas coeliac mucosal damage from ambient exposure accumulates gradually.
>
> In summary: coeliac disease is the more serious condition when assessing dietary (ingestion) exposure; IgE-mediated gluten allergy presents the greater risk in an airborne occupational proximity scenario.

## 5. Regulatory Position

### COSHH Regulations 2002

Under the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2002/2677/contents/made" target="_blank">Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002</a> (COSHH), grain dust is classified as a substance hazardous to health. Employer duties include:

- **Regulation 6** — Conduct a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risk to health from exposure to grain dust, including for employees in adjacent areas (not only those directly handling grain)
- **Regulation 7** — Prevent or, where prevention is not reasonably practicable, adequately control exposure to grain dust; exposure must be kept as low as reasonably practicable (ALARP)
- **Regulation 11** — Provide health surveillance for employees at risk, particularly those exposed to respiratory sensitisers

### Workplace Exposure Limit (EH40)

The current Workplace Exposure Limit for grain dust under <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/eh40.htm" target="_blank">EH40/2005 Workplace Exposure Limits</a> is:

| Dust fraction | WEL (8-hour TWA) |
|---|---|
| Inhalable | 10 mg/m³ |
| Respirable | 4 mg/m³ |

**Important:** Grain dust carries the **'Sen' (sensitiser) notation** in EH40, meaning it is a recognised respiratory sensitiser. For substances with the Sen notation, the WEL is not a safe exposure level — exposure must be driven as low as reasonably practicable, regardless of whether measurements are below the WEL. An employee with an existing IgE-mediated wheat allergy or established grain dust sensitisation may react at concentrations far below the WEL.

### HSE Grain Dust Guidance

- <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/agriculture/topics/grain-dust.htm" target="_blank">HSE: Grain Dust — Agriculture Topic Page</a>
- <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/eh66.pdf" target="_blank">EH66: Grain Dust — HSE Guidance Note</a>
- <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/indg140.pdf" target="_blank">INDG140: Control of Exposure to Grain Dust — Employee's Guide</a>

HSE guidance does not publish specific exposure limits differentiating bystander (office worker) exposure from primary handler exposure. The COSHH duty to assess and control risk applies to all employees who may be exposed, including those in adjacent buildings.

## 6. Control Measures

Measures are listed in hierarchy of controls order (most to least effective):

### Engineering Controls (most effective)

| Control | Benefit | Applicable conditions |
|---|---|---|
| Dust extraction/suppression at dryer exhaust | Reduces airborne particulate at source | All three conditions |
| Dust suppression at loading/unloading point (e.g. enclosed transfer, water misting) | Reduces dust plume at tipping | All three conditions |
| Office windows on the opposite side of the building to grain sheds | Reduces ingress of airborne dust | All three conditions |
| Positive pressure ventilation in office (air in from clean side) | Prevents dust ingress | All three conditions |

### Administrative Controls

| Control | Benefit | Applicable conditions |
|---|---|---|
| Notify office staff before active grain drying or loading/unloading commences | Allows windows to be closed | All three conditions |
| Schedule high-dust operations to avoid periods when wind is blowing toward the office | Reduces transient peak exposure | All three conditions |
| Prohibition of eating at desks during active grain handling operations | Reduces ingestion route | Coeliac, NCGS |
| Hand washing before eating | Reduces hand-to-mouth gluten ingestion | Coeliac, NCGS |

### Personal Protective Equipment (least preferred; supplement to above)

| Control | Benefit | Applicable conditions |
|---|---|---|
| FFP2 or FFP3 filtering face piece during outdoor movement near sheds | Reduces inhalation of grain dust | Coeliac (face mask proven effective in NEJM case); IgE allergy |
| Antihistamine — pre-agreed protocol with employee's GP | Reduces mild allergic reaction | IgE allergy |
| Epinephrine auto-injector (e.g. EpiPen) on site, with trained first aiders | Emergency treatment for anaphylaxis | IgE allergy |

## 7. Recommendations

### Gluten Allergy (IgE-mediated) — Priority: HIGH

1. **Obtain a medical assessment** from the employee's GP or occupational physician to confirm the nature and severity of their wheat/grain sensitisation, including any history of anaphylaxis
2. **Conduct a spirometry (lung function) baseline** before employment at this site, and annually thereafter (COSHH health surveillance)
3. **Ensure an epinephrine auto-injector is available on site** and that sufficient trained first aiders know how and when to use it
4. **Implement dust suppression engineering controls** at source (dryer exhaust and loading point) as a priority
5. **Establish a notification protocol** — office staff must be warned before grain drying or loading operations commence so windows can be closed
6. **Review suitability of role** — if engineering controls cannot adequately reduce exposure, the role at this location may not be suitable for an individual with a confirmed IgE wheat allergy without further medical clearance

### Coeliac Disease — Priority: MODERATE

1. **Obtain a medical opinion** on the individual's current degree of intestinal sensitivity and whether any occupational gluten exposure is contraindicated
2. **Implement engineering controls** at dust source (as above) — these benefit all conditions
3. **Implement the notification protocol** so windows can be closed during active grain handling operations
4. **Provide an FFP2 or FFP3 mask** for the employee to use if they must be outdoors near the grain sheds during active operations
5. **Enforce hand hygiene and no-eating-at-desk** during active grain handling periods
6. **Review annually** — if the employee's coeliac disease is well-controlled and engineering controls are in place, continued employment on site is likely compatible with maintaining health

### Gluten Intolerance (NCGS) — Priority: LOW

1. Standard good practice workplace hygiene measures (hand washing, no eating in dusty areas) are sufficient
2. No specific engineering controls are required for NCGS from an airborne exposure perspective
3. If the employee reports symptoms, consider whether cross-contamination via hand-to-mouth is occurring and reinforce hygiene measures

## Evidence Gaps and Limitations

This assessment is limited by an acknowledged gap in the scientific literature: **no published studies directly measure airborne gluten concentrations at 50 yards from active grain drying or loading/unloading operations.** The risk ratings for coeliac disease in particular are therefore qualitative assessments based on:
- Known biological mechanisms (mucociliary clearance and swallowing)
- The NEJM 2007 benchmark case (150g/day grain dust in enclosed farm conditions)
- Physical distance attenuation
- Seasonal ventilation conditions

If precise risk quantification is required, airborne grain dust sampling at the office location during active grain handling operations, followed by laboratory analysis for gluten protein content, would provide direct evidence. The HSE can advise on appropriate sampling methodology.
