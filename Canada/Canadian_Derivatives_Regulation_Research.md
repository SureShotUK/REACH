# Canadian Securities Regulation Requirements for UK FCA-Regulated Firm Offering Oil Commodity Swaps in Ontario

**Research Date:** January 21, 2026
**Scope:** Derivatives dealer registration requirements for foreign firm offering swaps to professional clients in Ontario, Canada

---

## Executive Summary

A UK FCA-regulated firm offering oil commodity swaps (derivatives) to professional clients in Ontario faces significant registration requirements under Canadian securities law. The primary regulatory framework consists of:

- **NI 31-103** (Registration Requirements, Exemptions and Ongoing Registrant Obligations)
- **NI 93-101** (Derivatives: Business Conduct) - effective September 28, 2024
- **NI 94-101** (Mandatory Central Counterparty Clearing of Derivatives)
- Ontario Securities Act and Commodity Futures Act

**Key Finding:** Foreign dealers can operate without full Canadian registration by relying on the **International Dealer Exemption** (Section 8.18 of NI 31-103 and Section 39/46 of NI 93-101), but this exemption has strict requirements and limitations. Operating without proper registration or exemption carries severe legal and financial consequences.

---

## 1. DERIVATIVES DEALER REGISTRATION REQUIREMENTS IN CANADA/ONTARIO

### 1.1 What Constitutes "Trading" or "Dealing" in Derivatives

Under NI 31-103, registration as a derivatives dealer is required when a person or company is **"in the business of trading in derivatives"** in Canada. This is determined by the **"business trigger"** test, which considers:

#### Key Factors for Business Trigger:
- **Holding oneself out** as being in the business of trading derivatives
- **Advertising, soliciting, or indicating** willingness to act as a derivatives dealer
- **Frequency and nature** of trading activity (high volume or central to business operations)
- **Acting as a market maker** (regularly providing bid/ask quotes)
- **Direct or indirect solicitation** of trades from counterparties
- **Providing clearing services** for derivatives

**Important:** There is no standalone definition of "trading in derivatives" in NI 31-103, but it is generally understood to encompass any act, advertisement, solicitation, conduct, or negotiation, directly or indirectly, in furtherance of the sale or disposition of a derivative for valuable consideration.

### 1.2 Activities That Trigger Registration

The following activities typically trigger the registration requirement:

1. **Solicitation:** Contacting Canadian clients to solicit derivative trades
2. **Order Execution:** Executing derivatives transactions with Canadian counterparties
3. **Market Making:** Providing continuous bid/ask quotes
4. **Clearing Services:** Acting as intermediary for clearing derivatives (highly regulated under NI 94-101)

### 1.3 Thresholds and Exemptions

#### Proposed Notional Amount Thresholds:
- **C$1 billion** gross notional amount for dealers exclusively in commodity derivatives
- **C$250 million** gross notional amount for other derivatives dealers

**Note:** These are proposed exemptions for dealers with limited activity. However, for foreign dealers, the more practical exemption is the International Dealer Exemption.

## 2. INTERNATIONAL DEALER EXEMPTION (SECTION 8.18 OF NI 31-103 & SECTIONS 39/46 OF NI 93-101)

The International Dealer Exemption allows foreign dealers to operate in Canada without full registration, subject to **mandatory conditions**.

### 2.1 Mandatory Requirements - ALL Must Be Met

#### 1. Home Jurisdiction Registration
- Foreign dealer must be registered and in good standing in its home jurisdiction (UK FCA registration qualifies)
- Must be authorized to perform the same activities in home jurisdiction as intended in Canada

#### 2. Permitted Clients / Eligible Derivatives Parties (EDPs) Only
Trading must be limited to **"Permitted Clients"** (under NI 31-103) or **"Eligible Derivatives Parties" (EDPs)** under NI 93-101:

**Categories include:**
- Canadian financial institutions
- Federal, provincial, or territorial governments
- Registered dealers and advisers
- Pension funds regulated by federal or provincial law
- Individuals with financial assets exceeding **C$5 million** (aggregate realizable value)
- Companies with net assets of at least **C$25 million**
- Commercial hedgers (entities using derivatives to hedge physical commodity business risks)

#### 3. Agent for Service of Process
- Must appoint an agent for service in each province/territory where exemption is relied upon
- File **Form 31-103F2** (Submission to Jurisdiction and Appointment of Agent for Service)
- Under NI 93-101: File **Form 93-101F1**

#### 4. Client Notification and Disclosure
Must provide clear written disclosure to clients stating:
- The dealer is **not registered** in Canada
- Most of the dealer's assets are located **outside of Canada**
- Challenges may exist in **enforcing legal rights** against the dealer
- The dealer complies with securities laws of its home jurisdiction

#### 5. Annual Notification and Fees
- Annually notify securities commissions of reliance on exemption
- Pay required participation fees (e.g., under OSC Rule 13-502 in Ontario)

#### 6. Compliance with Home Jurisdiction Laws
- Must comply with all securities laws of home jurisdiction (UK FCA requirements)

#### 7. Submission to Jurisdiction
- Must submit to jurisdiction of Canadian securities regulators
- Provide regulators with access to books and records

### 2.2 Limitations of the International Dealer Exemption

- **No retail clients:** Cannot solicit or trade with retail clients or any client not meeting the "permitted client" / EDP definition
- **Limited physical presence:** Generally assumes dealer does not have a physical business location in Canada (see Section 6 below)
- **Not full registration:** Dealer is subject to fewer regulatory requirements but also has fewer privileges than fully registered dealer
- **Exemption is mandatory, not optional:** If dealing with Canadian clients, must either obtain full registration OR rely on an available exemption - cannot operate without one or the other

### 2.3 Recent Regulatory Updates (2024-2025)

#### NI 93-101 Derivatives Business Conduct Rule
- **Effective Date:** September 28, 2024
- **Status:** Upgraded from Multilateral Instrument (MI 93-101) to National Instrument (NI 93-101), applying across all Canadian jurisdictions
- **Impact:** Foreign dealers involved in derivatives trading must comply with this rule OR qualify for an exemption under it
- **Transition Period:** Derivatives firms have until September 2025 to collect waivers from relevant individuals and commercial hedgers

#### CIRO Delegation (April 1, 2025)
- Ontario Securities Commission delegated certain registration functions to Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO)
- Significant structural change in Canadian regulatory landscape
- Less direct impact on foreign dealers relying on International Dealer Exemption (since they are not "registered")

## 3. ALTERNATIVE EXEMPTIONS AVAILABLE

### 3.1 Can Foreign Dealer Rely Solely on Permitted Client Exemptions Without Registration?

**Answer: NO - Not Exactly**

A foreign dealer **cannot** simply trade with permitted clients/EDPs and assume this alone exempts them from registration. The dealer must **actively rely on** and **comply with all conditions** of the International Dealer Exemption.

**Key Distinction:**
- Trading with permitted clients/EDPs is a **necessary condition** but **not sufficient** on its own
- The dealer must file required forms, provide disclosures, appoint agent for service, and meet all other conditions
- The exemption must be **actively claimed and maintained**, not passively assumed

### 3.2 Other Available Exemptions

#### End-User Exemption
- Generally applies to entities using derivatives to hedge their own business risks
- **Not applicable** to dealers offering derivatives to third-party clients
- Relevant for clients who are commercial hedgers, not for the dealer itself

#### Commodity Trading Exemptions

**Commodity Dealer Exemption (NI 93-101):**
- Available for derivatives dealers whose business is **solely in commodity derivatives**
- Must not exceed **C$10 billion** notional amount of outstanding commodity derivatives
- Exempts dealer from most provisions of NI 93-101 Derivatives Business Conduct Rule
- **Important:** This exempts from business conduct requirements but does NOT exempt from registration requirements under NI 31-103

**Key Limitation:** This exemption reduces regulatory burden but does not eliminate the need to either register or rely on the International Dealer Exemption.

#### Eligible Commercial Hedgers
- Category of client, not dealer exemption
- Commercial hedgers can waive certain protections under business conduct rules
- Relevant for determining who qualifies as an EDP

### 3.3 Summary: Exemption Strategy

For a UK FCA-regulated firm offering oil commodity swaps to Ontario clients:

**Recommended Path:**
1. Rely on **International Dealer Exemption** (Section 8.18 NI 31-103 & Sections 39/46 NI 93-101)
2. Ensure all clients qualify as **Permitted Clients / Eligible Derivatives Parties**
3. If dealing exclusively in commodity derivatives with notional below C$10 billion, also claim **Commodity Dealer Exemption** from NI 93-101 business conduct requirements
4. Comply with ALL mandatory conditions (forms, disclosures, agent appointment, fees)

**Not Viable:**
- Relying solely on "permitted client" status without meeting exemption requirements
- Operating without any registration or exemption

## 4. COMMODITY DERIVATIVES SPECIFIC TREATMENT

### 4.1 Regulatory Treatment: Oil/Energy vs. Financial Derivatives

#### Key Differences:

**Commodity Derivatives (Including Oil/Energy):**
- Subject to both **Securities Act** (for OTC derivatives) and **Commodity Futures Act** (for exchange-traded futures) in Ontario
- Eligible for **Commodity Dealer Exemption** under NI 93-101 (C$10 billion threshold)
- **Physical commodity market participants** receive special consideration through exemptions
- **Reporting thresholds:** C$250 million aggregate month-end gross notional amount exemption for commodity derivatives reporting (Ontario, Manitoba, Québec)

**Financial Derivatives:**
- Generally lower thresholds and fewer carve-outs
- More stringent clearing requirements under NI 94-101
- Commodity derivatives have **temporary UPI (Unique Product Identifier) exemption** until January 2027

### 4.2 Physical Commodity Market Participants - Carve-Outs

#### Derivatives Data Reporting Exemptions:
1. **Temporary UPI Exemption:** Market participants can continue using existing methods for identifying commodity derivatives in trade reporting until January 2027
2. **Reporting Threshold:** Exemption from reporting commodity derivatives for local counterparties with aggregate month-end gross notional amount up to **C$250 million**
3. **Valuation Data Exemption:** Counterparties that are not derivatives dealers or recognized clearing agencies are exempt from reporting valuation data
4. **Extended Reporting Deadlines:** Non-qualifying reporting counterparties have extended deadlines for trade creation and life-cycle data
5. **Inter-Affiliate Exemption:** Transactions between affiliated entities exempt from reporting in certain circumstances

#### Business Conduct Rule Exemptions:
- **Commodity Dealer Exemption:** Derivatives dealer solely in commodity derivatives exempt from most of NI 93-101 if under C$10 billion notional
- **Eligible Commercial Hedgers:** Can waive certain protections under business conduct rules

### 4.3 NI 94-101 vs. NI 31-103 - Different Pathways

| **Aspect** | **NI 94-101** | **NI 31-103** |
|------------|---------------|---------------|
| **Focus** | **The Transaction** - Clearing and settlement | **The Market Participant** - Registration and conduct |
| **Purpose** | Mitigate systemic risk through mandatory central clearing of standardized OTC derivatives | Govern registration, conduct, and obligations of firms trading or advising on securities/derivatives |
| **Core Question** | **What** derivatives must be centrally cleared? | **Who** needs to register to trade/advise on derivatives? **How** must they conduct business? |
| **Application to Oil/Energy** | Applies if oil/energy derivative is listed as "mandatory clearable derivative" in appendices | Determines if firm dealing in oil/energy derivatives needs derivatives dealer registration |
| **Exemptions** | Based on transaction type, counterparty type, clearing arrangements | Based on dealer characteristics, client types, notional amounts |

**Key Insight:**
- NI 94-101 is about the "plumbing" of the derivatives market (transaction infrastructure)
- NI 31-103 is about the "people and firms" operating in that market (market participants)
- Both must be complied with, but they address different aspects of derivatives regulation

**For Oil Commodity Swaps:**
- Likely NOT subject to mandatory clearing under NI 94-101 (most commodity derivatives are exempt from mandatory clearing)
- DEFINITELY subject to registration requirements under NI 31-103 (unless exemption applies)

## 5. UNREGISTERED DEALING RISKS

### 5.1 Legal Consequences of Dealing Without Registration or Exemption

Operating as an unregistered derivatives dealer in Ontario carries severe consequences under the Ontario Securities Act (OSA).

#### Administrative Penalties (Section 127 OSA)

The OSC has broad powers to impose:
- **Administrative penalties:** Up to **C$1 million per contravention**
- **Disgorgement:** Order to pay back all amounts obtained through non-compliance
- **Trading and acquisition bans:** Prohibition from trading or acquiring securities
- **Removal of exemptions:** Making OSA exemptions unavailable to the person/company
- **Director and officer bans:** Prohibition from serving as director/officer of issuer, registrant, or investment fund manager
- **Reprimands**
- **Payment of costs:** OSC investigation and hearing costs

#### Quasi-Criminal Offences (Provincial Prosecution)

Prosecuted in Ontario Court of Justice:
- **Fines:** Up to **C$5 million**
- **Imprisonment:** Up to **5 years less a day**
- **Both:** Fine AND imprisonment may be imposed

#### Criminal Code of Canada (Federal Prosecution)

For serious securities fraud cases:
- **Maximum penalty:** **14 years imprisonment**
- **Mandatory minimum:** **2 years imprisonment** for frauds exceeding C$1 million

### 5.2 Contract Enforceability

#### Voidability of Contracts

**Key Risk:** Contracts for the sale of securities/derivatives by an unregistered person may be **voidable** by the investor.

**Investor Rights:**
- **Right of rescission:** Investor can return the securities/derivatives and demand full refund
- **Action for damages:** Statutory right to sue for damages for misrepresentation in offering memorandum
- **Disgorgement and distribution:** OSC can order disgorgement of profits and distribution to harmed investors

**Practical Impact:**
- Unregistered dealer may be unable to enforce derivative contracts against Canadian counterparties
- Even if contracts appear valid, courts may refuse to enforce them due to illegality
- Clients can potentially avoid obligations under derivatives transactions by asserting dealer's lack of registration

### 5.3 Penalties and Enforcement Actions

#### OSC Enforcement Powers:
1. **Investigation:** Extensive powers to compel documents, testimony, and information
2. **Interim orders:** Freeze assets, halt trading pending investigation
3. **Permanent orders:** Long-term bans, penalties, disgorgement
4. **Public disclosure:** Enforcement actions are publicly disclosed, damaging reputation

#### CSA Coordination:
- **Canadian Securities Administrators** coordinates enforcement across provinces
- **Public warnings:** CSA issues investor alerts about unregistered dealers
- **Multi-jurisdictional enforcement:** Violations in one province can trigger action in others

#### Recent Trends:
- Increased focus on derivatives regulation post-2024 with NI 93-101 implementation
- Enhanced scrutiny of foreign dealers operating in Canada
- Growing coordination between provincial regulators and international counterparts (including UK FCA)

### 5.4 Practical Consequences Beyond Legal Penalties

- **Reputational damage:** Public enforcement actions harm firm's global reputation
- **Business disruption:** Inability to continue operations in Canada, unwinding of existing positions
- **Client relationships:** Loss of Canadian clients, potential claims from counterparties
- **International impact:** Enforcement actions may be reported to home jurisdiction regulator (FCA), triggering additional scrutiny
- **Insurance and banking:** Difficulty obtaining insurance coverage, banking relationships
- **Future market access:** Difficulty obtaining registration or exemptions in future

## 6. CROSS-BORDER CONSIDERATIONS

### 6.1 Physical Office in Ontario vs. Dealing from UK

The presence or absence of a physical office in Canada is a **critical factor** in determining registration requirements.

#### Scenario 1: Dealing from UK (No Physical Office in Ontario)

**Status:** International Dealer Exemption is **AVAILABLE**

**Requirements:**
- Must rely on International Dealer Exemption (Section 8.18 NI 31-103 / Sections 39/46 NI 93-101)
- Deal exclusively with permitted clients / EDPs
- No physical office or place of business in Canada
- Registered with FCA in UK for similar activities
- File Form 31-103F2 and Form 93-101F1
- Provide client disclosures
- Pay annual participation fees

**Key Advantages:**
- Avoids full Canadian registration process
- Lower compliance burden
- No CIRO membership requirement
- Can conduct business relatively efficiently

#### Scenario 2: Physical Office in Ontario

**Status:** International Dealer Exemption is **NOT AVAILABLE**

**Requirements:**
- **MUST obtain full registration** with Ontario Securities Commission (OSC)
- **MUST become member** of Canadian Investment Regulatory Organization (CIRO)
- Must meet comprehensive registration requirements:
  - Capital requirements
  - Insurance requirements
  - Compliance systems
  - Proficiency standards for employees
  - Books and records
  - Client relationship model (CRM2) compliance
  - Ongoing regulatory reporting

**Key Disadvantages:**
- Full registration process is time-consuming (typically 6-12 months)
- Significant costs (legal, compliance, capital requirements)
- Ongoing regulatory compliance burden equivalent to domestic dealer
- Subject to CIRO oversight and rule compliance
- Must maintain registered individuals (Ultimate Designated Person, Chief Compliance Officer, etc.)

### 6.2 What Constitutes "Physical Office" or "Physical Presence"?

#### Factors Indicating Physical Presence:
- Leased or owned office space in Canada
- Employees or agents working from Canadian location
- Business address or phone number in Canada
- Holding out as having Canadian office
- Regular in-person client meetings at Canadian location

#### Activities That Generally Do NOT Constitute Physical Presence:
- Occasional business travel to Canada for client meetings
- Attending conferences or industry events in Canada
- Having clients located in Canada (but all interactions remote)
- Using third-party service providers located in Canada (if not acting as dealer's agents)

**Gray Areas Requiring Legal Advice:**
- Shared office space or co-working arrangements
- Representative or agent operating in Canada on dealer's behalf
- Regular pattern of temporary presence (e.g., monthly visits)

### 6.3 Foreign Dealer Exemptions - Summary

#### "Foreign Dealer" Status:
Canada recognizes that foreign dealers should be able to serve sophisticated Canadian clients without full local registration, BUT only under strict conditions.

#### Exemption Philosophy:
- **Investor protection:** Permitted clients/EDPs are sophisticated and don't need full regulatory protection
- **Market access:** Facilitate access to global derivatives markets
- **Regulatory coordination:** Rely on home jurisdiction supervision where dealer is based
- **Jurisdiction:** Ensure OSC/CSA can investigate and enforce if problems arise

#### Eligible Foreign Jurisdictions:
For purposes of International Dealer Exemption, head office must be in "specified foreign jurisdiction," including:
- **United Kingdom** ✓
- United States
- European Union member states
- Other jurisdictions recognized by CSA

**UK FCA-regulated firm qualifies** as eligible foreign jurisdiction.

### 6.4 Practical Recommendations for Cross-Border Operations

#### If Operating from UK Only:
1. **Rely on International Dealer Exemption**
2. **Strictly limit clients to Permitted Clients/EDPs** - implement robust client qualification procedures
3. **File all required forms** (31-103F2, 93-101F1) proactively
4. **Provide written disclosures** to all Canadian clients
5. **Appoint agent for service** in Ontario (and any other province where dealing)
6. **Pay annual fees** and maintain exemption status
7. **Avoid establishing physical presence** - conduct all meetings remotely or during temporary visits
8. **Document everything** - maintain clear records of client qualifications, disclosures, exemption reliance

#### If Considering Physical Office in Canada:
1. **Seek full registration** with OSC **before** establishing physical presence
2. **Plan for 6-12 month registration process**
3. **Budget for significant costs** (legal, compliance, capital, insurance)
4. **Engage Canadian legal counsel** experienced in securities registration
5. **Consider CIRO membership requirements**
6. **Evaluate alternative structures** - could Canadian entity be separate legal entity with relationship agreement to UK parent?

#### Red Flags to Avoid:
- Establishing physical office while relying on International Dealer Exemption
- Dealing with retail clients or clients not meeting permitted client/EDP criteria
- Failing to file required forms or provide required disclosures
- Not appointing agent for service or paying participation fees
- Soliciting clients in Canada without proper exemption or registration

## CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

### Key Findings:

1. **Registration IS Required** unless valid exemption applies - no "gray area" for unregistered dealing
2. **International Dealer Exemption IS Available** for UK FCA-regulated firm dealing from UK with qualified clients
3. **Physical Presence ELIMINATES Exemption** - physical office in Ontario requires full registration
4. **Commodity Derivatives Have Some Benefits** but do not eliminate core registration/exemption requirements
5. **Consequences of Non-Compliance Are Severe** - up to C$5M fines, 5 years imprisonment, voidable contracts
6. **Client Qualification Is Critical** - must verify and document all clients meet permitted client/EDP criteria

### Practical Recommendations:

#### Immediate Actions:
1. **Verify current client base** - ensure all Canadian clients meet permitted client/EDP criteria
2. **File exemption forms** (31-103F2, 93-101F1) with OSC immediately if not already filed
3. **Appoint agent for service** in Ontario
4. **Provide written disclosures** to all Canadian clients per exemption requirements
5. **Implement client onboarding procedures** to verify and document permitted client/EDP status

#### Ongoing Compliance:
1. **Annual notifications** to OSC of continued reliance on exemption
2. **Annual participation fees** payment
3. **Monitor client qualifications** - reverify financial thresholds periodically
4. **Update disclosures** if circumstances change
5. **Track regulatory changes** - NI 93-101 implementation ongoing through 2025-2026

#### Strategic Considerations:
1. **Avoid physical presence** in Canada if possible - maintain remote relationship model
2. **If physical presence needed**, plan for full registration process 6-12 months in advance
3. **Consider limiting to commodity derivatives** to benefit from C$10 billion threshold exemption from NI 93-101 business conduct requirements
4. **Engage Canadian legal counsel** for formal legal opinion on exemption availability and compliance
5. **Coordinate with UK FCA** - ensure Canadian activities are within scope of FCA authorization

#### Risk Mitigation:
1. **Document everything** - client qualifications, disclosures provided, forms filed, fees paid
2. **Regular compliance reviews** - periodic audit of exemption compliance
3. **Staff training** - ensure all staff understand limitations of exemption
4. **Avoid retail solicitation** - no marketing to non-permitted clients
5. **Contract provisions** - include appropriate disclaimers about non-registered status in client agreements

### When to Seek Professional Advice:

**Legal Counsel Required:**
- Formal opinion on exemption availability
- Review of client qualification procedures
- Drafting of disclosure documents
- Assessment of physical presence issues
- Full registration application (if establishing Canadian office)

**Compliance Consultation Recommended:**
- Implementation of ongoing compliance program
- Staff training programs
- Regulatory change monitoring
- Interaction with OSC/CIRO
