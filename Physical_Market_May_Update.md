## SCENARIO UPDATE: CEASEFIRE BUT BLOCKADE CONTINUES

Since the previous analysis (22 April), the conflict has entered a new phase that must not be misread as de-escalation on the supply side.

The US-Iran 2-week ceasefire announced 7 April was **extended indefinitely on 21 April** rather than allowed to lapse — Trump cited Iran's "seriously fractured" government and the need for Tehran to submit a "unified proposal." No new end date was set. However:

- The **US naval blockade of Iranian ports remains fully in place**
- **No exchange of fire** between US forces and Iran since 7 April (formally confirmed in Trump's 1 May War Powers Resolution letter to Congress: *"hostilities have terminated... there has been no exchange of fire between United States Forces and Iran since April 7, 2026"*)
- The **Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed** to international commercial shipping — only Iran-linked vessels are transiting
- **Peace talks are stalled** — Iran submitted a new 10-point proposal on 1 May; Trump declared himself "not satisfied," with nuclear enrichment rights the unresolvable sticking point
- **~3,200 vessels** (including ~800 tankers and ~53 LNG/LPG carriers) remain at anchor or idling in the region; the IMO is now preparing a formal humanitarian evacuation plan for approximately 20,000 stranded seafarers

The practical effect on the oil market is unchanged from full-war conditions: the Strait is closed, product supply chains remain severed, and stock drawdown continues daily. The cessation of kinetic activity should not be interpreted as supply relief.

### Why products remain more acute than crude

The analysis from the April 22 document is unchanged and has intensified. Crude-on-crude substitution is managing the crude side (Saudi Red Sea, West African, North Sea grades filling gaps). But **refined product chains are structurally broken**. With 10 additional days of stock drawdown on top of 10 weeks of disruption prior to that, the product market is measurably tighter than at the April 22 assessment date.

ARA gasoil/diesel stocks fell **1% in the latest reporting week** to <a href="https://www.insights-global.com/ara-oil-stocks/" target="_blank">**1.84 million tonnes**</a> (source: Insights Global ARA Stocks) — an **8-month low**, with independent gasoil inventories averaging **10% lower in April than in March**. **Days-of-cover context:** 1.84 million tonnes equates to approximately **13.7 million barrels** (at ~7.45 bbl/tonne). The ARA hub distributes gasoil and diesel across Northwest Europe (UK, Netherlands, Belgium, Scandinavia, and export markets) at a throughput rate of approximately **150,000–200,000 tonnes/day**. These are independently stored spot/traded stocks in the ARA hub tank farms; they do not include strategic petroleum reserves (held separately under IEA obligations), refinery stocks, or direct pipeline supply from UK refineries to retail infrastructure. UK retail diesel at **188.5–189.8p/litre** (confirmed) is **above the April 22 projected ceiling** of 175–185p/litre. This confirms that the April 22 analysis was not pessimistic; current conditions have exceeded its central estimate.

The diesel market remains in **deep backwardation** — the front-month premium over the 6-month forward signals that buyers have limited confidence in near-term relief.

#### Continued constrained regions (diesel)

- **East Africa**: Acute shortage continuing; Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania most exposed
- **Southeast Asia**: Philippines, Myanmar, Cambodia tightening further
- **Europe**: Managing via US Gulf Coast and Indian imports, but both channels near ceiling

ARA jet fuel stocks are confirmed at <a href="https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2815208-ara-jet-fuel-stocks-at-six-year-low-insights-global" target="_blank">**below 600,000 tonnes**</a> (source: Argus Media / Insights Global; <a href="https://www.tradingview.com/news/reuters.com,2026:newsml_L6N41614X:0-ara-jet-fuel-stocks-fall-to-lowest-since-2020/" target="_blank">confirmed Reuters</a>) — the lowest level since **March 2020** (the COVID demand collapse). This represents a **40% drawdown since January 2026**. The Centre for Aviation (CAPA) has explicitly warned that **European airlines face jet fuel shortages before the June 2026 summer peak** if Hormuz remains closed.

**Days-of-cover context:** 600,000 tonnes equates to approximately **4.7 million barrels** (at ~7.83 bbl/tonne for jet fuel). The ARA hub is the primary jet fuel supply and distribution point for Northwest European aviation — serving UK airports (via tanker from Rotterdam/Amsterdam), the Netherlands, Belgium, and export flows to Scandinavia. At a combined NW European aviation throughput rate of approximately **45,000–60,000 tonnes/day** (UK aviation alone consumes approximately 30,000–35,000 tonnes/day; <a href="https://www.caa.co.uk/data-and-analysis/uk-aviation-market/airports/datasets/uk-airport-data/" target="_blank">UK CAA airport data</a>), current ARA jet stocks represent approximately **10–13 days of hub supply** for the NW European markets served — against a pre-crisis normal of approximately 16–22 days at ~950,000–1,000,000 tonnes. For UK aviation specifically, 600,000 tonnes represents roughly **17–20 days of UK-only consumption**, but as the ARA hub serves multiple countries simultaneously this is not a meaningful ceiling. These are hub spot stocks only and do not include strategic aviation fuel reserves held by airlines, airports, or under government agreements.

The jet crack spread's apparent normalisation in forward month futures reflects market participants pricing in an H2 2026 Hormuz resolution — **not current physical availability**. The physical spot market remains acutely constrained; the forward easing is a speculative signal, not a supply signal.

#### UK jet fuel — current position
- **No current physical shortage** at UK airports; airlines are operating on pre-purchased forward contracts and strategic uplift arrangements
- Government has **suspended airport slot rules** ("use it or lose it") to allow carriers to cancel flights without losing take-off/landing rights — a precautionary measure consistent with government preparing for allocation scenarios
- **Airline surcharge positions** as of May 2026:

| Airline | Surcharge position |
|---|---|
| Virgin Atlantic | Up to **£360/ticket** (Economy £50; Premium Economy £180; Upper Class £360) |
| British Airways | Surcharges applied; specific per-sector amount not publicly disclosed |
| EasyJet | **No surcharge** — pledged surcharge-free position for Summer 2026 |

The split between carriers reflects hedging strategy: airlines with longer forward cover (EasyJet, Jet2) are able to hold surcharge-free positions; those more exposed to spot/near-term market (Virgin, BA) are passing through costs immediately.

The risk sharpens when forward contracts expire and must be renewed against spot market conditions. For airlines with Q3 2026 hedge expiry dates, the procurement challenge will become acute by June.

#### Regions at risk for jet shortages next
- **UK airports** — forward contracts provide current buffer; physical allocation risk within 6–8 weeks
- **Southern European airports** (Madrid, Rome, Athens) — dependent on Gulf cargoes via Suez

### 3.3 PETROL / GASOLINE / NAPHTHA

#### Petrol — low direct risk (unchanged)
The UK petrol position remains insulated from Hormuz disruption. Fawley and Humber produce surplus petrol exported to West Africa and the US. UK retail petrol is approximately **~157p/litre** (May 2026) — elevated vs ~130p pre-war due to crude price increases, but not reflecting a product-specific supply crunch.

#### Naphtha — significant downward price revision; demand destruction signal

**A major data revision applies here.** The April 22 analysis reported naphtha at ~$1,000+/tonne. Since then:

| Period | Naphtha (NWE, $/tonne) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| 6 March 2026 | ~$776 | Pre-supply shock baseline |
| Late March 2026 (peak) | ~$1,000+ | Initial supply shock pricing |
| April 2026 | **~$525** | **Demand destruction** |

The ~48% fall from peak is not supply normalisation — it reflects **demand destruction at scale**. Asian petrochemical steam crackers have sharply reduced run rates as the combined effect of feedstock shortages and downstream chemical demand weakness has made production uneconomic. The naphtha market has moved from a supply-shock story to a demand-destruction story.

The downstream implications are significant: reduced cracker run rates will feed through to tightening supply of polyethylene, polypropylene, and other petrochemical derivatives over a 4–8 week lag period.

## 4. TRADE FLOW REROUTING — UPDATE

The alternative supply picture has not materially changed since 22 April, but two developments are worth noting:

**India — export duty cuts (1 May 2026):** India has cut export duties on both diesel and ATF (jet fuel) effective 1 May 2026 in a direct policy response to the European supply crisis:
- Diesel: duty cut from ₹55.5/litre to ₹23/litre
- ATF: duty cut from ₹42/litre to ₹33/litre

This modestly improves the economics of Indian exports to Europe. However, a key constraint remains: **EU buyers are cautious about Jamnagar-processed products** due to the risk of secondary sanctions — Russian crude is processed at Reliance's Jamnagar complex, creating potential EU sanctions exposure. Until EU regulators provide clarity, Indian diesel and jet volumes cannot be fully utilised as Gulf substitutes.

**Saudi Red Sea (Yanbu):** The Petroline/East-West Pipeline continues providing partial crude and LPG bypass. Saudi Arabia has increased Yanbu loadings but product output via this route is constrained by pipeline throughput and Yanbu refinery configuration.

| Source | Products | Incremental Europe capacity | Key constraint | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| US Gulf Coast | Diesel (ULSD), jet, naphtha | ~150–200 kbd diesel (near ceiling) | Terminal and logistics capacity full | Immediate |
| India (Jamnagar/Nayara) | Diesel, gasoil, jet | 200–300 kbd diesel; 50–100 kbd jet | EU sanctions risk (Russian crude input) | 2–6 weeks; duty cut from 1 May helps |
| North Africa (Algeria, Egypt) | Diesel, naphtha | <100 kbd incremental | Limited refinery complexity | 1–2 weeks |
| Russia (via Turkey/India) | Gasoil | Constrained by EU sanctions | Sanction risk + premium | Ongoing but legally risky |
| ARA spot | All products | Competitive; all buyers simultaneously | Scarce; no premium available | Immediate |
| Saudi Red Sea (Yanbu) | Crude, limited products | ~2 mb/d crude; limited products | Pipeline capacity | Immediate crude; weeks for products |

**Bottom line**: The gap analysis from 22 April is unchanged. Available incremental European supply (~400–600 kbd) cannot fully cover the European Gulf product import deficit (~600–800 kbd). The **structural uncoverable gap of 100–300 kbd** remains, and is now compounding after 10+ weeks of drawdown.

Combined with the **Grangemouth closure** (April 2025, ~150,000 b/d), the UK has now lost approximately **280,000 b/d** of domestic refining capacity within 13 months — representing roughly **30–35% of its peak domestic refining throughput**.

- Product must be shipped from ARA, US Gulf Coast, or south English refineries — adding 3–7 days minimum supply pipeline lag
- Terminal and pipeline infrastructure (not just price) is the binding constraint in a shortage scenario
- Any allocation or rationing scenario would see these regions tighten first and with least warning

## 10. KEY CONCLUSIONS (UPDATED 2 MAY 2026)

1. **The ceasefire changes the shooting war, not the supply war.** No exchange of fire since 7 April, but the US naval blockade and Iranian Hormuz closure continue uninterrupted. The product supply chain disruption is functionally identical to full-war conditions. The cessation of kinetic activity should not be used to lower the urgency of supply risk planning.

2. **Jet fuel is at crisis stock levels — the window before airport allocation is closing.** ARA stocks confirmed below 600,000 tonnes (lowest since March 2020 COVID crash) after a 40% drawdown since January. CAPA warns of European summer shortages before June's peak demand. The government's pre-emptive slot-rule relaxation is a supply crisis preparation signal.

3. **UK retail diesel has already exceeded the April 22 ceiling.** Confirmed at 188.5–189.8p/litre — above the 175–185p/litre projected range in the previous analysis. The supply situation has worsened, not stabilised, in the 10 days since.

4. **The Lindsey closure has materially worsened the UK's position.** The permanent closure of Lindsey Oil Refinery (assets acquired 28 April) — added to Grangemouth's April 2025 closure — means the UK has lost ~30–35% of its peak domestic refining capacity in 13 months. The UK is now more dependent on imports across all major products than at any point in the modern era.

5. **Hormuz will not reopen quickly even with a deal.** Baker Hughes and ~80% of Dallas Fed surveyed oil executives project August 2026 at the earliest. Mine-clearance adds 2–4 weeks post-agreement. The 3,200-vessel queue in the Gulf would take 3–5 weeks to clear. Supply relief, when it comes, is **5–9 weeks behind any diplomatic breakthrough** — earliest meaningful UK supply impact: September to October 2026.

6. **India's export duty cuts (1 May) help but cannot be fully utilised.** The duty reductions on diesel and ATF are constructive; India's record export volumes to Europe are a genuine support. But the EU sanctions overhang on Jamnagar-processed products (Russian crude input) remains the binding constraint on European buyers fully utilising Indian supply capacity until regulatory clarity is provided.

7. **Plan for current diesel prices as the floor, not the ceiling; plan for jet allocation as a near-term operational reality.** At 188–190p/litre and rising, diesel costs should be locked in where possible. For aviation operations, the question is no longer whether physical allocation will occur, but **when** — forward contract expiry dates and airport uplift planning should be reviewed urgently against the June–July risk window.
