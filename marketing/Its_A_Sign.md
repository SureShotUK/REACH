# It's A Sign: Advertising Near Roads — Legal Position (England)

*Research note: advertising sign placement adjacent to a 50mph A-road in England. Two specific scenarios examined: (1) on the grass verge beside the road where there is no footpath; (2) on private land owned by the advertiser, set back behind a hedge. Initial use: temporary event signage. Potential subsequent use: permanent commercial advertising.*

---

*Drafted By: Steve Irwin*  
*Date: 29/04/2026*

## 1. Legal Framework

Advertisement control in England operates on two separate legal tracks that both apply simultaneously:

| Track | Legislation | Who administers it |
|---|---|---|
| Advertisement consent (planning) | Town and Country Planning (Control of Advertisements) (England) Regulations 2007 (SI 2007/783) | Local Planning Authority (LPA) (district/borough council) |
| Highway land permission | Highways Act 1980 (ss. 96, 132, 137) | Highway Authority (county council for A-roads) |

Both must be satisfied. A sign lawful under planning rules can still be a criminal offence under the Highways Act if it is on highway land without the highway authority's consent.

**Primary legislation:**
- <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2007/783" target="_blank">Town and Country Planning (Control of Advertisements) (England) Regulations 2007</a>
- <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1980/66/section/132" target="_blank">Highways Act 1980, s.132 — Prohibition of unauthorised marks on highway</a>
- <a href="https://www.gov.uk/guidance/advertisements" target="_blank">GOV.UK Guidance: Advertisements</a>

## 2. Standard Conditions (Apply to Every Advertisement)

All advertisements in England — whether they require consent or not — are subject to the standard conditions set out in Schedule 2 to the 2007 Regulations. In summary:

1. **Landowner permission:** No advertisement may be displayed without the permission of the owner of the site. If the sign is on highway land, the highway authority is the relevant "owner" for this purpose.
2. **No obstruction of official signs:** No advertisement may obscure or hinder the interpretation of official road, rail, waterway, or aircraft signs, or otherwise make hazardous the use of those modes of transport.
3. **Maintenance:** Any advertisement must be kept in a condition that does not impair visual amenity of the site.

## 3. Scenario 1 — The Grass Verge (No Path)

### 3.1 Is the Verge Highway Land?

Almost certainly yes. Grass verges alongside A-roads are generally part of the adopted highway maintainable at public expense. The legal position is nuanced:

- **Freehold subsoil:** Under the *ad medium filum* presumption, adjacent landowners are presumed to own the freehold of the subsoil of the highway to the centre line of the road, in the absence of evidence to the contrary.
- **Surface rights:** The highway authority holds the right to maintain the surface and to control what happens on it. Even if you own the subsoil beneath the verge, this does not give you the right to erect a sign on the surface without the highway authority's consent.
- **In practice:** For the purpose of placing any sign or structure on the verge, the highway authority's consent is required as though they are the landowner of the surface.

### 3.2 What Permissions Are Required

Both of the following must be obtained before erecting any sign on the grass verge:

| Permission | From whom | Legal basis |
|---|---|---|
| Advertisement consent (planning) | Local Planning Authority | 2007 Regulations |
| Highway authority landowner consent | County Council Highways dept | Highways Act 1980 |

### 3.3 The Highways Act Position

Section 132 of the Highways Act 1980 makes it a criminal offence to affix any picture, letter, sign, or advertisement to the surface of a highway, or to any tree, structure, or works in or under the highway, without lawful authority. There is no requirement for prior warning — the sign may be removed immediately. Enforcement consequences include:

- Removal of the sign without notice
- Fixed Penalty Notice(s) per sign
- Storage fees to recover the sign
- Prosecution at magistrates' court: fine up to **£2,500** (level 4 on the standard scale)

### 3.4 Practical Likelihood on a 50mph A-Road

The highway authority (county council) is very unlikely to consent to advertising signs on the verge of a 50mph A-road. Their primary consideration is road safety. Factors working against consent:

- **Speed:** At 50mph, driver reaction time and stopping distances are significant; distracting or cluttered signage on verges is a safety concern.
- **Policy:** Most highway authorities have explicit policies permitting only approved traffic management signs on highway land. General advertising is excluded. Surrey County Council's published guidance, for example, only permits temporary signs directing drivers to events generating 400+ vehicles per day, and only black-on-yellow directional signs — not commercial advertising.
- **Maintenance obligation:** The highway authority is responsible for maintaining the verge and will not want structures interfering with that.

**Conclusion for Scenario 1:** Placing any advertising sign on the grass verge without both permissions is a criminal offence. Even with the correct applications, approval for a 50mph A-road verge is unlikely for commercial advertising. Do not place signs on the verge without prior written consent from both the LPA and the county council highways department.

## 4. Scenario 2 — Private Land Behind a Hedge

### 4.1 Land Ownership Position

You own the land — so the landowner consent element of the standard conditions is satisfied automatically. The Highways Act does not apply because the sign is not on highway land. This is the significantly more straightforward of the two scenarios.

### 4.2 Advertisement Consent Still Required

Owning the land does not exempt you from planning control on advertisements. Any sign visible from a public road requires advertisement consent under the 2007 Regulations, even on private land. The question is whether **deemed consent** applies or whether **express consent** is needed.

---

## 5. Advertisement Consent: Deemed vs. Express

### 5.1 Deemed Consent (No Application Needed)

The 2007 Regulations provide two routes to deemed consent:

**Schedule 1** — No consent required at all (e.g. advertisements on vehicles, incorporated into building fabric). Unlikely to be relevant here.

**Schedule 3** — Deemed consent granted automatically provided all conditions and size limits for the relevant class are met. There are 16 classes (Class 1–16). The most relevant for the two intended uses:

| Use case | Relevant Class | Key conditions |
|---|---|---|
| Temporary event sign | **Class 3** (temporary announcement signs, non-commercial events) | Max 28 days before the event; must be removed within 14 days after; size limits apply; non-illuminated |
| Commercial promotion / business advertising | No Schedule 3 class covers off-premises commercial signs as a general rule | **Express consent required** |

**Important distinction:** Schedule 3 Class 3 applies to *non-commercial events* (charity events, auctions, circuses, etc.). If the temporary sign is for a commercial promotion — even an event held by a business — it will likely fall outside Class 3 and require express consent.

### 5.2 Size and Illumination Limits Under Deemed Consent

Even where deemed consent applies, the standard conditions require:
- No illumination unless the class expressly permits it (Class 3 does not permit illumination)
- Size limits depend on the specific class; for most temporary signs, individual sign area is restricted (typically not exceeding 0.6 m² for residential/small-scale or up to 2 m² for larger commercial premises)
- Height limits apply (generally no part of the sign more than 4.6 m above ground, or 3.6 m in an Area of Special Control)

### 5.3 Areas of Special Control

Many rural areas alongside A-roads are designated as **Areas of Special Control** by the LPA. If your site falls within such an area:
- Additional restrictions apply: smaller permitted sizes, stricter illumination rules
- Hoardings alongside trunk roads in these areas may be prohibited entirely
- Check with your LPA (district/borough council) as a first step

### 5.4 Express Consent (Where Deemed Consent Does Not Apply)

If the sign does not fall within a Schedule 1 or Schedule 3 deemed consent class, or does not comply with the conditions of the applicable class, express consent is required.

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| Application to | Local Planning Authority (district/borough council) |
| Application via | <a href="https://www.planningportal.co.uk/planning/planning-applications/consent-types/advertisement-consent/" target="_blank">Planning Portal — Advertisement Consent</a> |
| Fee | £174 per advertisement (as at April 2025; subject to annual CPI uplift from April each year) |
| Decision period | 8 weeks from valid application |
| Grounds for refusal | Amenity and public safety only — the LPA cannot refuse on other grounds |

The LPA will consider whether the sign would harm visual amenity or pose a public safety risk. A sign set back behind a hedge on private land, not illuminated, and not obscuring sight lines, has a reasonable prospect of consent — but this is not guaranteed, particularly if the LPA considers it a distraction to drivers on a 50mph road.

## 6. The Transition: Temporary Event Sign → Permanent Business Sign

This is a two-stage situation legally:

| Stage | Position |
|---|---|
| **Temporary event sign** | May qualify for Schedule 3 Class 3 deemed consent if the event is non-commercial; 28-day pre-event / 14-day post-event limit strictly applies. If commercial: express consent required even temporarily. |
| **Permanent commercial sign** | Express consent required from the LPA. The temporary consent (if any) does not carry over. A separate application (£174) must be made and approved before the permanent sign goes up. |

Do not assume that removing and re-erecting a temporary sign on a rolling basis avoids the need for express consent. The LPA can serve a discontinuance notice if a sign with deemed consent is causing unacceptable amenity or safety harm.

---

## 7. The Sign's Position Behind the Hedge — Does It Matter?

Practically, a sign partially or fully screened by a hedge:
- Reduces its advertising value (reduced visibility from the road)
- Reduces the highway safety impact (less likely to distract drivers)
- Reduces the amenity impact on the street scene

However, legally:
- If any part of the sign is visible from a public road, it still constitutes a displayed advertisement requiring consent
- "Behind a hedge" does not create an exemption
- If the sign is entirely invisible from any public place, it is not a displayed advertisement for the purposes of the Regulations — but this would also make it largely useless as advertising

## 8. Enforcement Summary

| Offence | Enforcer | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Sign on highway verge without highway authority consent | County council highways | Removal without notice; FPN; fine up to £2,500 |
| Sign without advertisement consent (LPA) | LPA | Enforcement notice requiring removal; prosecution; fine |
| Sign in breach of deemed consent conditions | LPA | Treated as displayed without consent — same enforcement |
| Sign obscuring traffic signs | Police / LPA | Criminal liability; removal |

Unauthorised signs are subject to a 10-year enforcement time limit for advertisement consent purposes — but this is not a route to legitimacy; it is merely the period within which enforcement action can be taken.

---

## 9. Practical Checklist

Before placing any sign in either location:

- [ ] **Identify the land:** Confirm whether the verge is highway land (contact county council highways)
- [ ] **Check Area of Special Control:** Ask the LPA whether the site is within an Area of Special Control
- [ ] **Determine the use:** Is the sign for a non-commercial event (may get deemed consent) or commercial promotion (express consent needed)?
- [ ] **Check deemed consent:** Does the proposed sign comply with all Schedule 3 conditions for the relevant class?
- [ ] **Apply for express consent if needed:** £174 to LPA, allow 8 weeks
- [ ] **Grass verge:** Do NOT proceed without written consent from county council highways — even if advertisement consent is in place
- [ ] **Private land:** May proceed once advertisement consent (deemed or express) is confirmed and standard conditions are met
