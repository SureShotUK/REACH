## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched a coordinated air campaign against Iran, triggering the closure of the Strait of Hormuz by Iranian Revolutionary Guard forces. The Strait carries approximately **20 million barrels per day (mb/d)** of hydrocarbons in normal conditions — roughly 25% of global seaborne oil trade. Since its closure, global oil and product markets have experienced their most severe supply shock since the 1973 Arab embargo.

A nominal 2-week ceasefire was announced on 7 April 2026 and extended indefinitely on 21 April. However, the US naval blockade of Iranian ports remains fully in place and the Strait is still effectively closed to international commercial shipping as of 2 May 2026. Total flows through the Strait have declined further during the ceasefire period, now estimated at approximately **~2 mb/d** — less than 10% of pre-war levels. Peace talks are stalled, with Iran's demand for the right to continue nuclear enrichment remaining a condition the US has explicitly rejected. Baker Hughes and ~80% of oil executives surveyed by the Dallas Federal Reserve expect the Strait to remain closed until **at least August 2026**.

A second major UK refinery closure has been confirmed since the crisis began: **Lindsey Oil Refinery (Immingham) permanently ceased refining operations**, with Phillips 66 completing acquisition of the assets on 28 April 2026. Combined with the Grangemouth closure of April 2025, the UK has lost approximately **30–35% of its peak domestic refining capacity in 13 months**, materially worsening its exposure to import disruption.

## BACKGROUND: THE 2026 IRAN WAR AND HORMUZ CLOSURE

### Chronology

The US-Israel air campaign against Iran began on **28 February 2026** following Iran's refusal to halt uranium enrichment and allow international inspection of its nuclear facilities. The Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) responded by closing the Strait of Hormuz to international commercial shipping on approximately **2 March 2026**. US naval forces subsequently imposed a blockade of Iranian port facilities.

A first nominal ceasefire was announced on **7 April 2026** following mediation by Pakistan and the submission of a 10-point Iranian peace proposal. The ceasefire — which suspended kinetic air operations — was extended indefinitely by President Trump on **21 April 2026**. However, Trump confirmed in a formal War Powers Resolution letter to Congress on 1 May that while there has been "no exchange of fire... since April 7," the US naval blockade of Iranian ports remains in force.

As of **2 May 2026** (Day 64 of the conflict):
- No exchange of fire between US forces and Iran since 7 April
- US naval blockade of Iranian ports: **fully in place**
- Strait of Hormuz: **effectively closed** to international commercial shipping
- Iran's new 10-point peace proposal (1 May): rejected by Trump as "not satisfactory"; nuclear enrichment demands remain the unresolvable sticking point
- Israeli operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon: continuing independently of the US-Iran ceasefire

### Current Hormuz status

Only Iran-linked vessels are currently transiting the Strait. Daily commercial vessel transits have fallen from approximately **138 ships/day** pre-war to **single digits**. Approximately **3,200 vessels** of all types (including ~800 tankers and ~53 LNG/LPG carriers) are at anchor or idling in the region; the International Maritime Organization (IMO) is preparing a formal humanitarian evacuation plan for approximately 20,000 stranded seafarers.

## 1. SCALE OF THE HORMUZ DISRUPTION

### Pre-crisis flows and current status

The Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical energy chokepoint. Before the conflict, it carried:

| Commodity | Pre-war volume | % of global seaborne trade |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil + condensate | ~15.0 mb/d | ~34% of global crude trade |
| Refined products | ~3.3 mb/d | ~8–10% of global products trade |
| LPG | ~1.5 mb/d | ~16% of global LPG trade |
| LNG | — | ~35% of global LNG trade |
| **Total hydrocarbons** | **~20.1 mb/d equivalent** | **~25% of global seaborne oil** |

**Current flows (2 May 2026 estimate):** approximately **~2 mb/d** — less than 10% of pre-war levels.

| Timepoint | Estimated Hormuz flow | Comment |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-war (2025) | ~20.1 mb/d | Baseline |
| Early March 2026 | ~3.8 mb/d | Initial closure; some commercial traffic continuing |
| 22 April 2026 | ~3.8 mb/d | Ceasefire announced but blockade maintained |
| 2 May 2026 | **~2 mb/d** | Further tightening; ceasefire has not relieved blockade |

Crude export collapse from the region: from ~18.7 mb/d to approximately **5.9 mb/d** — a **68% decline**. Refining capacity in the Gulf is simultaneously constrained; an estimated **3–4 mb/d of regional refining throughput** has been shut or curtailed.

### Why products are more acute than crude

Crude-on-crude substitution is relatively elastic — importers can switch to West African, Latin American, or North Sea grades. **Refined products are not interchangeable** in the same way: diesel specifications, cetane ratings, sulphur content and cold flow properties vary; jet fuel requires tight certification of conformance; and alternative supply chains take weeks to months to establish. The net effect is that **product market tightness is more severe and more immediate than the crude tightness** for most Western importers.

### Reopening timeline

Baker Hughes (24 April 2026) stated the Strait may not fully reopen until **H2 2026**. A Dallas Fed survey of oil executives found ~80% expect closure to continue until at least August. An additional complication is mine-clearance: even after any political agreement, sweeping the Strait for Iranian-laid mines could add **2–4 weeks** before commercial transit safely resumes. The vessel queue of ~3,200 ships would then take a further **3–5 weeks** to clear. **Earliest realistic UK supply relief from a Hormuz reopening: September–October 2026.**

## 2. MIDDLE EAST REFINING — WHAT HAS BEEN LOST

The Gulf is home to some of the world's largest and most complex export-oriented refineries. Their combined output — which transited Hormuz to Asia and Europe — has been effectively landlocked since early March 2026.

| Refinery | Country | Capacity (b/d) | Key products | Status (May 2026) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ruwais (ADNOC) | UAE | 922,000 | Diesel, gasoil, jet, naphtha, LPG, fuel oil | Operational; export blocked |
| KNPC complex (Al-Ahmadi / Mina Abdullah / Shuaiba) | Kuwait | 940,000 | Diesel, naphtha, jet, LPG | Operational; export blocked |
| Ras Tanura / Jubail (Saudi Aramco) | Saudi Arabia | 550,000–1.2 mb/d combined | Diesel, naphtha, jet, LPG | Partial Red Sea rerouting only |
| BAPCO | Bahrain | 267,000 | Diesel, naphtha | Operational; export blocked |
| Sohar (OQ) | Oman | 230,000 | Diesel, fuel oil, naphtha | Operational; limited access |
| South Pars condensate refineries | Iran | ~400,000 | Condensate splits, naphtha | Struck/curtailed |

**Key point:** Even where refineries are physically operational (Saudi Arabia, UAE, Oman), the inability of product tankers to load and transit the Strait means output is either stockpiling onshore or being curtailed. As of late April 2026, approximately **800 tankers** and a further ~53 LNG/LPG carriers are at anchor in Gulf waters; the total vessel count idling in the region exceeds **3,200**. The accumulated inventory stockpiling onshore in Gulf terminals now represents months of output — but it cannot reach global markets until the Strait reopens and the vessel queue clears.

## 3. PRODUCT-BY-PRODUCT ANALYSIS

---

### 3.1 DIESEL / GASOIL

#### Global trade flows and current constraints

Middle East diesel exports accounted for approximately **860 kb/d** of global seaborne gasoil/diesel trade before the crisis. These flows have largely ceased. Primary recipients were:
- **Asia** (India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, East Africa): ~500 kb/d
- **Europe** (via Suez): ~200 kb/d, predominantly from Kuwait and UAE
- **East Africa / Red Sea basin**: ~100 kb/d

Diesel is the **most critically short product globally** by volume. Three factors have converged:
1. Gulf export collapse (~860 kb/d lost)
2. Refinery throughput cuts as crude feedstock availability has been disrupted
3. Surge in industrial diesel demand (military logistics, power generation backup across the crisis region)

Distillate crack spreads at NY Harbor reached **$1.42/gallon** — double the five-year average of $0.68/gallon and the highest since the 2022 Ukraine energy crisis.

#### ARA hub diesel stocks and days-of-cover

<a href="https://www.insights-global.com/ara-oil-stocks/" target="_blank">ARA gasoil/diesel stocks</a> (source: Insights Global) fell **1% in the latest reporting week** to **1.84 million tonnes** — an **8-month low**, with independent gasoil inventories averaging **10% lower in April than in March**.

**Days-of-cover context:** 1.84 million tonnes equates to approximately **13.7 million barrels** (at ~7.45 bbl/tonne). The ARA hub distributes gasoil and diesel across Northwest Europe at a throughput rate of approximately **150,000–200,000 tonnes/day**. At this rate, current ARA hub stocks represent approximately **9–12 days of hub distribution capacity** — compared with a pre-crisis normal of approximately 14–19 days at ~2.8 million tonnes. These are independently stored spot/traded stocks in the ARA hub tank farms; they do not include strategic petroleum reserves, refinery stocks, or direct pipeline supply from UK refineries to retail infrastructure.

#### UK retail price
UK retail diesel is currently confirmed at **188.5–189.8p/litre** (27 April–1 May 2026), up from approximately 140p/litre pre-war and above early analyst projections of 175–185p/litre. Further rises are the base case.

#### Constrained regions (diesel)
- **South Asia** (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka): Industrial diesel rationing underway; India has suspended retail price caps in multiple states
- **East Africa**: Acute shortages in Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania
- **Southeast Asia**: Philippines, Myanmar, Cambodia tightening
- **Europe**: Managing via US Gulf Coast and Indian imports but both channels are near their logistical ceilings

The diesel market is in **deep backwardation** — the front-month premium over the 6-month forward signals buyers have limited confidence in near-term relief.

### 3.2 JET FUEL / AVIATION KEROSENE

#### Global trade flows and current constraints

The Gulf was the world's largest net exporter of jet fuel before the crisis. Global jet fuel seaborne trade through Hormuz totalled approximately **600–700 kb/d** pre-conflict. Major exporters were Kuwait (major jet exporter to Europe and Asia via long-term supply agreements), UAE (Ruwais), and Saudi Arabia (Jubail SATORP).

**Jet fuel is the tightest market globally** and the product most likely to drive visible public impacts in the UK. It is geographically concentrated in its supply chain in a way that diesel is not: the US Gulf Coast does not export significant jet volumes to Europe (US domestic aviation demand absorbs most US jet production), and alternative supply chains from India take weeks to establish and certify.

#### ARA hub jet fuel stocks and days-of-cover

ARA jet fuel stocks are confirmed at <a href="https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2815208-ara-jet-fuel-stocks-at-six-year-low-insights-global" target="_blank">**below 600,000 tonnes**</a> (source: Argus Media / Insights Global; <a href="https://www.tradingview.com/news/reuters.com,2026:newsml_L6N41614X:0-ara-jet-fuel-stocks-fall-to-lowest-since-2020/" target="_blank">confirmed Reuters</a>) — the lowest level since **March 2020** (the COVID demand collapse), representing a **40% drawdown since January 2026**.

**Days-of-cover context:** 600,000 tonnes equates to approximately **4.7 million barrels** (at ~7.83 bbl/tonne for jet fuel). The ARA hub is the primary jet fuel supply and distribution point for Northwest European aviation, serving UK airports (via tanker from Rotterdam/Amsterdam), the Netherlands, Belgium, and Scandinavia. At a combined NW European aviation throughput rate of approximately **45,000–60,000 tonnes/day** (UK aviation alone consumes approximately 30,000–35,000 tonnes/day; <a href="https://www.caa.co.uk/data-and-analysis/uk-aviation-market/airports/datasets/uk-airport-data/" target="_blank">UK CAA airport data</a>), current ARA jet stocks represent approximately **10–13 days of hub supply** for the NW European markets served — against a pre-crisis normal of approximately 16–22 days at ~950,000–1,000,000 tonnes. These are hub spot stocks only and do not include strategic aviation fuel reserves held by airlines, airports, or under government agreements.

The Centre for Aviation (CAPA) has explicitly warned that <a href="https://centreforaviation.com/analysis/reports/europes-airlines-face-jun-2026-jet-fuel-shortages-if-strait-of-hormuz-stays-closed-743448" target="_blank">**European airlines face jet fuel shortages before the June 2026 summer peak**</a> if Hormuz remains closed.

#### Jet crack spread and price
- Jet crack spread peaked at approximately **$120/bbl** over Brent during the crisis (vs ~$18–22/bbl pre-war)
- Forward month futures show partial normalisation (market pricing in H2 2026 resolution) — this is a speculative signal, **not a physical supply signal**
- Global Jet-A average: **$8.63/gallon (~$209/barrel)** in April 2026 vs ~$3.00–3.50/gallon pre-war
- Jet A-1 CIF NWE spot peaked at approximately **$1,800/tonne** vs ~$800/tonne pre-war

#### Airline surcharge positions (UK, May 2026)

| Airline | Surcharge position |
|---|---|
| Virgin Atlantic | Up to **£360/ticket** (Economy £50; Premium Economy £180; Upper Class £360) |
| British Airways | Surcharges applied; per-sector quantum not publicly disclosed |
| EasyJet | **No surcharge** — pledged surcharge-free position for Summer 2026 |
| Jet2 | **No surcharge** — eliminated fuel surcharges for Summer 2026 |

The split reflects hedging strategy: airlines with longer forward cover are holding surcharge-free positions; those more exposed to the spot market are passing through costs now. The risk sharpens when forward contracts expire and must be renewed at spot market rates — for airlines with Q3 2026 hedge expiry, the procurement challenge becomes acute by June.

The UK government has <a href="https://www.gov.uk/government/news/jet-fuel-and-travel-plans-what-you-need-to-know" target="_blank">suspended airport slot rules</a> ("use it or lose it"), allowing carriers to cancel flights without losing take-off/landing rights — a precautionary measure consistent with government preparing for allocation scenarios.

### 3.3 PETROL / GASOLINE

#### Low direct risk for UK
The Hormuz closure has a more muted direct effect on petrol than on middle distillates for the UK. The Middle East is not a major petrol exporter to Europe; most Gulf gasoline went to Asia and the US West Coast. Europe's petrol balance is structurally long — several major European refineries are net gasoline exporters.

The UK is a **net gasoline exporter** — Fawley and Humber produce more petrol than UK demand absorbs, with surplus exported to West Africa and the US. UK petrol supply is primarily sourced from domestic refinery output and NW European (ARA) traded volumes. UK retail petrol is currently approximately **~157p/litre** (May 2026), elevated vs ~130p pre-war due to crude price increases but not reflecting a product-specific supply crunch.

**Direct UK petrol shortage risk is unlikely within a 3-month horizon** unless UK refineries experience crude feedstock disruption (not currently acute — UK crudes are North Sea and North American) or naphtha cracker feedstock shortages reduce blendstock availability.

### 3.4 NAPHTHA

#### Demand destruction — a counterintuitive price fall

The naphtha market has experienced an unexpected price collapse that should be understood as a **demand destruction signal, not supply normalisation**:

| Period | Naphtha price (NWE) | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-war (early 2026) | ~$650–700/tonne | Baseline |
| 6 March 2026 | ~$776/tonne | Initial supply shock |
| Late March 2026 (peak) | ~$1,000+/tonne | Supply shock fully priced |
| April 2026 | **~$525/tonne** | **Demand destruction** |

The Gulf exports large volumes of naphtha — primarily condensate splitter output and reformer naphtha — to South Korea, Japan, and India for petrochemical feedstock. This supply has largely ceased. However, Asian petrochemical steam crackers have sharply reduced run rates as the combined effect of feedstock shortages and downstream chemical demand weakness has made production uneconomic. The naphtha market has moved from a supply-shock story to a demand-destruction story.

**The downstream implication is significant:** reduced cracker run rates will feed through to tightening supply of polyethylene, polypropylene, and other petrochemical derivatives over a 4–8 week lag period. The price fall does not indicate the crisis is easing — it indicates the economic damage has propagated into the petrochemical complex.

### 3.5 LPG (PROPANE / BUTANE)

#### Global disruption
Hormuz is the dominant chokepoint for global LPG trade. Pre-crisis volumes: approximately **1.5 mb/d** — around **16% of global LPG seaborne trade**. Key exporters (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, UAE) have been effectively cut off from their main markets.

Most severely exposed:
- **India**: ~60% of domestic LPG consumption from imports, 90% of which normally transited Hormuz. Residential cylinder shortages reported in rural areas; government has suspended export allocations; subsidy bill rising sharply
- **Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka**: All acutely exposed; industrial users switching to alternatives
- **Japan / South Korea**: Managing via strategic stocks and US propane imports but costs have surged

Saudi Arabia exports significant propane via the **Petroline / East-West Pipeline** to Yanbu on the Red Sea — partially bypassing Hormuz. This provides some relief but volumes are materially smaller than Ras Tanura's total LPG export capacity.

#### UK LPG position — insulated

UK LPG supply is dominated by **North Sea production** (propane/butane from CATS, SAGE, and NSMP pipeline systems landing at Teesside). This makes UK LPG supply largely **insulated from Hormuz closure** for:
- Bulk residential propane (off-grid heating)
- Autogas (LPG vehicles)
- Agricultural LPG

Residual risks:
- **Industrial/petrochemical LPG**: Some UK industrial users import propane/butane from the Gulf when North Sea supply is insufficient; these cargoes are disrupted
- **Price**: UK propane/butane prices track international benchmarks; CP (Contract Price) for propane has surged 30–40% above pre-war contract levels globally
- **Aerosol and refrigerant-grade butane**: Specialist grades partly sourced from Gulf

**Verdict:** UK residential and transport LPG is physically secure. Industrial users face a significant price shock but not physical shortage risk in the near term.

### 3.6 FUEL OIL / HSFO

Singapore and the UAE (Fujairah) are the world's two largest bunkering hubs. Fujairah bunker volumes have effectively ceased due to the conflict; Singapore is managing but feedstock supply is constrained. ARA fuel oil inventories fell **20% in March 2026**.

The Middle East exported approximately **500–600 kb/d** of fuel oil into the global bunker market pre-crisis; this supply has largely been removed. The HSFO crack spread (vs Brent) has narrowed from –$15 to –$20/bbl pre-war to approximately **–$6 to –$8/bbl** — reflecting tightening supply.

An additional dynamic: Cape of Good Hope rerouting burns significantly more bunker fuel per voyage, creating demand pull simultaneously with supply disruption. For the UK, direct fuel oil exposure is limited; however, elevated bunker costs feed into shipping freight rates that affect all import costs.

## 4. TRADE FLOW REROUTING — LOGISTICS AND FREIGHT

### Cape of Good Hope routing

With Hormuz effectively closed and the Red Sea also disrupted (by Houthi activity and the broader regional conflict), vessels rerouting from the Gulf to Northwest Europe must take the **Cape of Good Hope** route. This adds:

- **+16–32 extra days** for Aframax/LR-class product tankers vs Suez routing
- Total voyage time NW Europe → Gulf → NW Europe: **~41 days** (vs ~25 days via Suez)
- Extra distance: **3,500–4,000 nautical miles** per voyage
- Additional fuel cost: approximately **$932,905 per Aframax voyage**

The practical effect is that a fleet of 100 LR tankers dedicated to this trade can now make roughly 60% of the voyages per year that they could previously — constituting a **~40% reduction in available freight capacity** for Middle East-to-Europe product trade, even before accounting for the loss of cargo availability from Gulf refineries.

### War risk insurance

War risk insurance premiums on Gulf of Oman / Hormuz transits escalated from a pre-crisis norm of **~0.125–0.25% of vessel value** to **1–5%** at the height of hostilities (mid-March 2026), with some underwriters quoting up to 10% for specific transits. By late April, premiums moderated to approximately **0.8–1%** but remain several times pre-crisis levels. Many P&I clubs and underwriters are declining cover entirely for Hormuz transits regardless of premium. For a VLCC (valued at ~$120m), the current ~1% rate represents an additional $1.2m per voyage versus ~$150,000–300,000 pre-war.

### Tanker market dynamics

| Vessel class | Gulf exposure | Current earnings change |
|---|---|---|
| LR2 (product tanker) | 44% from Gulf | –44% to –45% week-on-week |
| LR1 (product tanker) | 33% from Gulf | –48% to –50% week-on-week |
| MR (medium range) | 9% from Gulf | –25%; averaging ~$46k/day on Atlantic Basin routes |

**Tanker class definitions:**
- **LR2 (Long Range 2):** Large product tankers of 80,000–119,999 DWT (deadweight tonnes), capable of carrying approximately 600,000–750,000 barrels of clean refined products (diesel, jet fuel, naphtha, gasoline) in one cargo. The dominant vessel type for Gulf-to-Europe and Gulf-to-Asia product runs — the Hormuz-to-ARA route is a core LR2 trade. An LR2 carries roughly one day's worth of UK diesel consumption in a single voyage.
- **LR1 (Long Range 1):** Medium-large product tankers of 55,000–79,999 DWT, carrying approximately 400,000–550,000 barrels per cargo. Used extensively on Gulf-to-South Asia, Gulf-to-East Africa, and intra-regional product trades. Also deployed on Atlantic Basin runs (US Gulf to UK/ARA) as an alternative to LR2s.
- **MR (Medium Range):** Product tankers of 25,000–54,999 DWT, carrying approximately 180,000–340,000 barrels. The workhorse of short-to-medium haul product distribution — US Gulf to Europe, intra-European coastal trades, ARA to UK, and West Africa runs. MRs have limited Gulf exposure and are currently the primary vessel type handling the alternative supply flows from the US and India to Europe.

Paradoxically, LR tanker earnings are **down** because vessels cannot get cargoes out of the Gulf, not because demand has fallen. Vessels are trapped, at anchor, or rerouting empty. Meanwhile MR tankers are commanding very strong rates on Atlantic Basin routes (US Gulf to ARA, India to ARA) as demand for alternative supply channels surges.

LR2 Gulf-UK/Continent rates: **$91.11/mt** — significantly elevated versus the pre-war norm of ~$35–45/mt.

## 6. UK-SPECIFIC EXPOSURE

### 6.1 UK refinery landscape (May 2026)

The UK has experienced two major refinery closures since April 2025, materially reducing its domestic production buffer at the worst possible time.

**Currently operational:**

| Refinery | Operator | Capacity (b/d) | Crude slate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fawley (Southampton) | ExxonMobil | ~270,000 | North Sea, North American |
| Humber (Immingham) | Phillips 66 | ~221,000 | North Sea, diverse |
| Pembroke (West Wales) | Valero | ~270,000 | Diverse Atlantic Basin |
| Stanlow (Merseyside) | Essar Energy | ~296,000 | North Sea, Atlantic Basin |

**Recently closed:**

| Refinery | Closure | Capacity lost | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grangemouth (Scotland) | April 2025; converted to import terminal July 2025 | ~200,000 b/d (~20% UK capacity) | Scotland now 100% import-dependent |
| Lindsey (North Lincolnshire) | April 2026 — permanently closed; <a href="https://fueloilnews.co.uk/2026/04/phillips-66-limited-completes-acquisition-of-lindsey-oil-refinery-assets/" target="_blank">assets acquired by Phillips 66 28 April 2026</a> | ~130,000 b/d | Further import dependency |

Combined UK refinery throughput is now approximately **1.0–1.1 mb/d** against UK demand of approximately **1.4–1.5 mb/d** — a structural import deficit of **300,000–400,000 b/d** covered by imports primarily from ARA and direct ocean shipment. The two closures in 13 months represent a loss of approximately **30–35% of the UK's peak domestic refining capacity**.

**Can UK refineries increase output?** Marginally. All four remaining refineries are running at high utilisation given elevated crack spreads. They run North Sea, US, and West African crudes — all unaffected by Hormuz closure — and cannot materially offset the import deficit without additional crude availability. This is not the binding constraint.

**Scotland and Northern Ireland** are the UK's highest-risk regions. With Grangemouth gone, Scotland has no domestic refinery. All Scottish and Northern Irish product must be imported — adding shipping lag and making these regions the first-failure points in any allocation scenario.

### 6.2 UK JET FUEL — CRITICAL EXPOSURE

**Jet fuel is the UK's single greatest physical product vulnerability.**

**UK jet fuel import structure (pre-crisis):**
- **Kuwait**: **38% of total UK jet imports** (~4.1 million tonnes/year) — the UK's single largest jet fuel source
- **Saudi Arabia**: second largest source
- **Gulf producers combined**: approximately **55–60%** of UK jet fuel imports

The UK is a **net jet fuel importer** — domestic refinery output does not cover aviation demand, and there is **no short-term substitution** available: commercial aircraft cannot switch fuels.

**Alternative supply constraints:**
- **US Gulf Coast**: Running at record jet export volumes (~200 kbd to Europe in April 2026) but cannot fully replace ~600 kbd in Middle East regional exports; US domestic aviation demand competes for this supply
- **India (Jamnagar/Nayara)**: Can produce Jet A-1; India cut ATF export duty from ₹42 to ₹33/litre effective 1 May 2026, improving economics — but logistics pipeline to deliver certified product into UK airports takes weeks to establish and EU/UK sanctions exposure on Russian crude inputs limits European buyer uptake
- **No meaningful European spare**: France, Netherlands, and Germany are all competing for the same reduced supply pool simultaneously

**UK jet fuel risk timeline:**

| Stage | Estimated timing |
|---|---|
| Prices elevated; airlines paying; surcharges applied | **Now (ongoing)** |
| ARA stocks at COVID-era lows; allocation tightening begins | **Now — imminent** |
| Airlines begin restricting cargo uplift; reducing non-essential sectors | **Early June 2026** |
| Physical rationing risk at UK airports (Heathrow, Gatwick, Manchester first) | **Early–mid June 2026** |
| Government intervention — mandatory stock drawdown, priority allocation | **July 2026** |

*This is the highest-confidence shortage risk facing the UK and carries the shortest timeline to physical impact.*

### 6.3 UK DIESEL — HIGH RISK

**UK diesel import structure (pre-crisis):**
- **United States**: ~35% (ULSD from US Gulf Coast; currently at record export levels to Europe)
- **Netherlands**: ~21% (ARA hub; liquid but competing with global demand)
- **Belgium**: ~16% (ARA catchment)
- **Kuwait / Gulf**: ~14% directly, plus additional Gulf-origin volumes cleared via ARA

Of the approximately 300,000–400,000 b/d of diesel the UK imports, roughly **14–20%** has direct Gulf/Hormuz exposure. However, the entire ARA system is under pressure because South and Southeast Asian markets are aggressively bidding for non-Gulf diesel, diverting ARA cargoes eastward. The UK competes with the world for the same marginal barrel.

**Key risk factors:**
1. **ARA hub at 8-month low**: 1.84 million tonnes represents ~9–12 days of hub distribution capacity; any further supply failure has no buffer to absorb it
2. **US ULSD supply at ceiling**: US Gulf Coast diesel exports have surged (up ~137% year-on-year to Europe) but face capacity constraints from competing Latin American demand
3. **Indian supply limited by sanctions risk**: Jamnagar's diesel exports are near record highs and India cut export duty from 1 May, but EU sanctions concerns around Russian crude inputs to Jamnagar's SEZ limit how quickly European buyers can fully utilise Indian supply
4. **Two refinery closures**: Scotland/NI now 100% import-dependent; national import deficit has grown

**UK diesel risk timeline:**

| Stage | Estimated timing |
|---|---|
| Diesel at 188–190p/litre; HGV, agricultural, construction sectors absorbing elevated costs | **Now (confirmed)** |
| Supply stress begins in Scotland, Northern Ireland, and remote rural areas | **~10–20 May 2026** |
| Wholesale availability tightening; regional distribution lead times extending | **Late May–early June 2026** |
| Physical shortage risk in Scotland, NI, and rural areas | **June–July 2026** |
| National supply constraint risk; government likely invokes emergency powers under Petroleum Act 1998 / Energy Act 2016 | **July–August 2026 if no resolution** |

### 6.4 UK PETROL — LOW RISK

The UK is a structural net exporter of petrol/gasoline. The primary Hormuz-related risks to UK petrol are indirect:
- **Naphtha feedstock**: UK petrochemical plants use naphtha as cracker feedstock; Gulf naphtha is unavailable and North Sea naphtha prices have risen in sympathy
- **Blendstock availability**: Some ethanol and specialist blendstocks have supply chain implications

**Verdict:** UK petrol supply at forecourts is not at material risk within a 3-month horizon. UK retail petrol at approximately **~157p/litre** (May 2026) reflects crude price increases, not a product-specific supply crunch.

---

### 6.5 UK LPG — LOW RISK (RESIDENTIAL/TRANSPORT)

UK LPG supply is dominated by North Sea production landing at Teesside. Residential and autogas LPG supply is physically secure. Industrial users face a 30–40% price shock on contract renewals. No residential physical shortage risk within 3 months.

| Source | Products | Incremental European capacity | Key constraint | Availability |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| US Gulf Coast | Diesel (ULSD), jet, naphtha | ~150–200 kbd diesel (near ceiling) | Terminal and logistics capacity full | Immediate |
| India (Jamnagar / Nayara) | Diesel, gasoil, jet | 200–300 kbd diesel; 50–100 kbd jet | EU sanctions risk on Russian crude input; duty cut from 1 May helps | 2–6 weeks |
| North Africa (Algeria, Egypt) | Diesel, naphtha | <100 kbd incremental | Limited refinery complexity | 1–2 weeks |
| Russia (via Turkey / India) | Gasoil | Constrained by EU sanctions | High sanctions risk; legal exposure for EU buyers | Ongoing but risky |
| ARA spot market | All products | All buyers competing simultaneously | Scarce; no surplus volume available | Immediate |
| Saudi Red Sea (Yanbu) | Crude, limited products | ~2 mb/d crude; limited products only | Petroline pipeline capacity | Immediate for crude; weeks for products |

**India export duty cuts (1 May 2026):** India has cut export duties on diesel (₹55.5 → ₹23/litre) and ATF (₹42 → ₹33/litre) in a direct policy response to the European supply crisis. This modestly improves the economics of Indian exports to Europe. However, EU buyers remain cautious about Jamnagar-processed products due to the risk of secondary EU sanctions linked to Russian crude processed at Reliance's Jamnagar complex. Until EU regulators provide clarity, Indian export capacity cannot be fully utilised.

**Bottom line — the uncoverable gap:** Available incremental European supply from all alternative sources combined (~400–600 kbd) cannot fully cover the European Gulf product import deficit (~600–800 kbd). There is a **structural uncoverable gap of approximately 100–300 kbd** that must be met by demand destruction or drawdown of strategic stocks. This gap is now compounding after approximately 10 weeks of continuous drawdown.

## 8. STRATEGIC PETROLEUM RESERVES

### IEA emergency release (as of 2 May 2026)

On **11 March 2026**, the IEA announced a coordinated emergency SPR release of **400 million barrels globally** — the largest in IEA history and the sixth ever coordinated release. All 32 IEA member countries participated unanimously.

| Entity | Authorised volume | Status as of 1 May 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| United States (SPR) | 172 MMbbl | ~17.5 MMbbl drawn; DOE RFP for next 92.5 MMbbl tranche, bids due 4 May |
| IEA members combined | 400 MMbbl | In execution; pace constrained by logistics (~2.5 MMbbl/week US draw rate) |
| **Second coordinated release** | **Not yet announced** | IEA Director Birol (14 Apr): "ready to go deeper" |

At global consumption rates of ~100 mb/d, 400 MMbbl represents approximately **4 days of global consumption** — a significant signal but not a structural solution. The US draw rate of ~17.5 MMbbl over 7 weeks (of 172 MMbbl authorised) indicates logistical constraints on the drawdown pace.

**No second coordinated IEA release has been announced as of 2 May 2026.**

### UK SPR position

- UK total strategic stocks: approximately **68 million barrels** (crude + products) — equivalent to ~60–90 days of consumption as a blended average
- **Jet-specific strategic cover: approximately 10–15 days** (down from ~15–20 days at the start of the crisis; 10 weeks of drawdown at elevated consumption rates)
- **Diesel strategic cover: approximately 25–35 days** (down from ~30–40 days pre-crisis)
- Approximately 50% of UK strategic stocks are held in ARA terminals (Netherlands, Belgium) under IEA bilateral agreements — these are simultaneously the most constrained storage locations

**Critical caveat:** The 60–90 day total IEA-compliant figure is a **blended average across all products**. Specific product categories — particularly jet fuel — have much lower cover than the aggregate implies. Strategic stocks provide a bridge, not a solution: even a full drawdown of UK jet-specific strategic reserves extends the timeline by only 10–15 days at current consumption and drawdown rates.

## 11. KEY CONCLUSIONS

1. **The ceasefire changes the shooting war, not the supply war.** No exchange of fire since 7 April, but the US naval blockade and Hormuz closure continue uninterrupted. The product supply chain disruption is functionally identical to full-war conditions. The absence of kinetic activity must not reduce the urgency of supply risk planning.

2. **Jet fuel is at crisis stock levels — the window before physical airport allocation is closing.** ARA hub stocks below 600,000 tonnes (lowest since March 2020 COVID crash) after a 40% drawdown since January, representing approximately 10–13 days of NW European hub supply. CAPA warns of European summer shortages before June's peak demand. Government slot-rule relaxation is a precautionary signal that Whitehall is preparing for allocation scenarios.

3. **UK retail diesel has broken through earlier projections.** Confirmed at 188.5–189.8p/litre — above prior analyst projections and rising. ARA gasoil hub stocks at an 8-month low represent ~9–12 days of hub distribution capacity. Scotland and Northern Ireland face supply stress within approximately 10–20 days. Plan for current prices as the floor, not the ceiling.

4. **The UK's domestic refining buffer has been severely reduced.** The permanent closure of Lindsey (April 2026) — added to Grangemouth (April 2025) — means the UK has lost ~30–35% of its peak domestic refining capacity in 13 months. The four remaining refineries (Fawley, Humber, Stanlow, Pembroke) are running North Sea and Atlantic Basin crudes insulated from Hormuz, but cannot substitute for the lost import flows. Scotland and Northern Ireland are now fully import-dependent.

5. **Alternative supply exists but cannot close the gap.** Available incremental supply from US Gulf Coast, India, and North Africa (~400–600 kbd combined) cannot fully cover the European product import deficit (~600–800 kbd). A structural uncoverable gap of 100–300 kbd must be met by demand destruction or stock drawdown — which is now in its tenth week.

6. **Hormuz will not reopen quickly even with a deal.** Baker Hughes and the Dallas Fed project August 2026 at the earliest. Mine-clearance adds 2–4 weeks. The 3,200-vessel queue requires a further 3–5 weeks to clear. Supply relief, when it comes, is **5–9 weeks behind any diplomatic breakthrough** — earliest UK supply impact: September–October 2026.

7. **India's export duty cuts (1 May) help but are not a solution.** Constructive on the margin; constrained by EU sanctions exposure on Russian crude processed at Jamnagar. Until regulatory clarity is provided, Indian capacity cannot be fully utilised as a Gulf substitute.

8. **For aviation: forward contract expiry is the critical risk event.** No current physical shortage at UK airports, but airlines operating on pre-purchased forward contracts. For carriers with Q3 2026 hedge expiry, renewing in the spot market by June will be significantly more costly and may not be fully achievable at required volumes. This is the mechanism by which the physical shortage risk crystallises.
