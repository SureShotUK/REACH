# Fixed Access Ladders (Work Access) — Standards and Applicability

**Prepared:** 2026-05-07
**Context:** Two small fixed access ladders (~2 m high), indoor, out of the elements, not in grain store area, used for **occasional work access** (not emergency escape).

---

## 1. KEY DISTINCTION FROM EMERGENCY ESCAPE LADDERS

The regulatory framework changes fundamentally when a ladder is used for work (even occasionally). The most important shift is that the **Work at Height Regulations 2005 (WAHR)** apply in full, along with **PUWER 1998** as work equipment. These do NOT apply to the emergency escape ladders discussed in `Ladder_Standards.md` in the same way — emergency escape ladders are governed primarily by the **Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005**.

This document covers the standards relevant to your **occasional work access** ladders.

---

## 2. STANDARDS AND REGULATIONS THAT APPLY

### A. Primary Legislation

#### Work at Height Regulations 2005 (WAHR)

**Relevance:** This is the **primary regulation** for your work access ladders. All three parts apply:

**Part 1 — General Requirements:**
- Regulation 3: You must avoid work at height where possible (e.g., use an extendable tool from ground level instead of climbing)
- Regulation 4: Where work at height cannot be avoided, take appropriate measures to prevent falls (guardrails, platforms, etc.)
- Regulation 5: Equipment must be suitable, inspected, and maintained
- Regulation 6: Risk assessment required for work at height

**Schedule 6 — Steep Ladders (Critical for Fixed Ladders):**
- Fixed ladders are classified as "steep ladders" under WAHR
- **Schedule 6, paragraph 3:** A steep ladder must not be used as a means of access if another means of access would be safer under all circumstances. You must be able to justify that a ladder was the safer choice compared to a stair or other means.
- **Schedule 6, paragraph 6:** The length of the climb must be taken into account — shorter climbs are less hazardous
- **Schedule 6, paragraph 7:** Where a steep ladder is used for access (not just egress), it must comply with **BS 5395-3** for design and installation
- **Note:** WAHR Schedule 6 references BS 5395-3, which has since been withdrawn and superseded by BS 4211:2005+A1:2008. In practice, BS 4211 is now the applicable design standard. WAHR Schedule 6 has not been updated to reflect this supersession.
- **Schedule 6, paragraph 8:** Rest platforms must be provided at suitable intervals for longer climbs

**Key point for your 2 m ladders:** At ~2 m, these are relatively short climbs. WAHR Schedule 6 recognises that shorter climbs are lower risk, but the justification requirement (para 3) still applies — you must be able to demonstrate that a ladder was the appropriate choice.

#### Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998 (PUWER)

**Relevance:** Your fixed ladders are "work equipment" under PUWER.

- **Regulation 5:** The ladder must be suitable for its intended purpose
- **Regulation 6:** The ladder must be maintained in a safe condition
- **Regulation 8:** Specific measures for risks arising from equipment (falls, structural failure)
- **Regulation 9:** Users must receive adequate health and safety training
- **Regulation 10:** Only people trained and authorised may use the equipment

**Inspection under PUWER:** PUWER does not prescribe a fixed inspection frequency for ladders. However, HSE guidance and industry practice recommend **6–12 monthly detailed visual inspections** for fixed access ladders used for work. This is more frequent than the 12-monthly inspection for emergency escape-only ladders because regular loading accelerates deterioration.

#### Regulatory Reform (Fire Safety) Order 2005 (RRF(SO))

**Relevance:** Even though these ladders are not part of the means of escape, RRF(SO) still applies to the premises generally:
- The ladder must not block or obstruct any fire escape route
- The ladder itself must not create a fire risk (e.g., no storage of combustibles on or around it)
- The Responsible Person must still consider the ladder in their fire risk assessment (what happens if someone is on the ladder during a fire?)

### B. British Standards

#### BS 5395-3:1997+A2:2017 — Code of Practice for Fixed Ladders (~~Withdrawn/Superseded~~)

**Relevance:** ~~Withdrawn and superseded by BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 for design and BS 7883:2019 for inspection~~. Historically this was the **primary standard** for fixed ladders used for work access. **BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 is now the primary design standard.** The lifecycle guidance formerly provided by BS 5395-3 is now split between BS 4211 (design and construction) and BS 7883:2019 (inspection methodology).

**What it covers for your use case:**
- Selection criteria: when ladders are appropriate vs. stairs/other means
- Design requirements: dimensions, clearances, loadings, safety features
- Installation guidance: fixings, supports, foundations
- Safety cages and hoops: for ladders over 3 m (your 2 m ladders may not require cages under this standard, but hoops are recommended even at 2 m)
- Rest platforms: not required for 2 m ladders
- Inspection: periodic inspection programme including frequency, methodology, defect classification
- Maintenance: corrosion protection, component replacement

**Current status:** BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 is now the primary design standard. BS 7883:2019 is the current inspection standard. BS 5395-3 formerly bridged these two as a comprehensive lifecycle code of practice but has been superseded.

#### BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 — Specification for Permanently Fixed Ladders

**Relevance:** BS 4211 provides the detailed design specification for permanently fixed ladders on buildings. If your ladders were manufactured post-2005, they should comply with BS 4211.

**For your 2 m ladders:** The dimensional and load specifications in BS 4211 are useful as a benchmark during inspection. Even if the ladders predate BS 4211:2005, the standard is the reference point for assessing structural adequacy.

#### BS 7883:2019 — Inspection of Fixed Ladders in Operation

**Relevance:** The inspection methodology in BS 7883 applies equally to work access ladders and emergency escape ladders. A competent inspector conducting an inspection to BS 7883 will assess:
- Structural condition (stiles, rungs, fixings, welds)
- Corrosion extent and remaining wall thickness
- Safety feature condition (cages/hoops, gates, platforms)
- Overall fitness for purpose

**Key difference:** For work access ladders, the inspection must also assess compliance with WAHR Schedule 6 design requirements (justification for ladder use, safety features) — not just structural condition.

#### BS EN ISO 14122-4:2016 — Safety of Machinery — Fixed Ladders

**Relevance:** Only applies if these ladders are attached to machinery, silos, or processing equipment (not just building walls). If the ladders are on building walls only, this standard is less relevant than BS 4211.

**When it applies:** If your ladders provide access to machinery, hopper tops, or processing equipment within the building, then BS EN ISO 14122-4 applies. It has overlapping design requirements with BS 4211 (which you should use for building-mounted ladders).

### C. Additional Standards Specific to Indoor Use

#### BS 5266-1:2016 — Emergency Lighting (Limited Relevance)

**Relevance:** Your work access ladders are indoor and out of the elements. If the location has adequate permanent lighting (i.e., lights on whenever the ladder is used), BS 5266-1 emergency lighting may not apply to the ladder itself. However, if the area is also a fire escape route, emergency lighting may still be required at the ladder location.

**Action:** Confirm whether the area has adequate permanent lighting sufficient for safe work access at all times the ladder might be used.

#### BS EN ISO 1461:2009 — Hot Dip Galvanizing (Limited Relevance)

**Relevance:** Your ladders are indoors and out of the elements, so corrosion risk is much lower than for the grain store ladders. However, if the indoor environment includes high humidity (e.g., grain processing areas, condensation risk), galvanizing condition should still be assessed.

**Action:** If the area is a normal indoor office or workshop environment with good ventilation and low humidity, ISO 1461 assessment is lower priority. If the area has condensation, dust, or chemical exposure, treat it similarly to the grain store ladders.

### D. WAHR Schedule 6 — Justification for Ladder Use

This is the **most important regulatory requirement** for your work access ladders and the most commonly overlooked.

**Schedule 6, paragraph 3** requires that you cannot use a steep ladder (which includes fixed ladders) as a means of access unless you have assessed the circumstances and concluded that:
1. A shorter ladder is used (your 2 m ladders meet this — they are short)
2. **The site or working conditions make the use of equipment for working at height impracticable** (e.g., space constraints, infrequent access needs)
3. **The risk can be controlled appropriately** given the local and working conditions

**In practice:** For 2 m ladders used occasionally indoors, the justification is usually straightforward:
- Space constraints make a stair impractical
- Access is infrequent (occasional maintenance/inspection)
- The height is short (2 m)
- Falls can be controlled through other measures (e.g., safe system of work, training)

**However:** You must **document this assessment**. If HSE enquires, you need to show that you considered alternatives (e.g., a small stair, a stair tower, platform access) and concluded the ladder was the safer option in the circumstances.

## 3. COMPARISON: WORK ACCESS LADDERS vs. EMERGENCY ESCAPE LADDERS

| Aspect | Work Access Ladders (Your 2 m Ladders) | Emergency Escape Ladders (Grain Store) |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary regulation** | WAHR 2005 + PUWER 1998 | RRF(SO) 2005 + PUWER 1998 |
| **Primary standard** | **BS 4211:2005+A1:2008** (design) | BS 7883:2019 (inspection) |
| **WAHR Schedule 6 applies?** | **Yes** — must justify ladder over stair | No — emergency use is not "work at height" |
| **Risk assessment required?** | **Yes** — WAHR Regulation 4 risk assessment | No — not a work activity |
| **Inspection frequency** | **6–12 months** (due to work use loading) | 12 months (no regular loading) |
| **Safety cages/hoops** | Required if over 3 m (WAHR Schedule 6) | Recommended but not mandated for emergency |
| **Rest platforms** | Required if over 6 m (WAHR) | Recommended for >9 m (accessibility) |
| **Emergency lighting** | If area has no adequate permanent lighting | Mandatory (BS 5266-1) |
| **Training for users** | **Mandatory** — users must be competent | Not required — occupants use without training |
| **Three points of contact** | **Applies** during work use | Not applicable during emergency escape |
| **Fall arrest** | May be required for tall ladders | Not practical in emergency |
| **Fire risk consideration** | Must not block escape routes | **Is** the escape route |
| **PUWER training requirement** | **Mandatory** — users must be trained | Not applicable |

## 4. WHAT TO ASK YOUR INSPECTOR FOR THESE LADDERS

For your 2 m work access ladders, the inspection should cover:

1. **WAHR Schedule 6 justification:** Does the existing ladder comply with the design requirements of Schedule 6? Can you demonstrate that a ladder was the appropriate choice?
2. **Structural condition:** Inspect to BS 7883:2019 methodology
3. **Design compliance:** Assess against BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 dimensions and load capacity
4. **Safety features:** Check hoops/cages (even at 2 m, hoops are good practice), handholds at top and bottom, gate operation
5. **Environmental factors:** Indoor corrosion assessment (less critical for off-weather locations but still important if humidity/dust present)
6. **User safety:** Are the conditions around the ladder safe for the person using it? (Clearance at base, landing space at top, lighting, no obstructions)
7. **PUWER compliance:** Is the ladder maintained in safe condition suitable for its purpose?

## 5. RECOMMENDED INSPECTION REGIME FOR WORK ACCESS LADDERS

| Inspection Type | Frequency | Responsible | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-use visual check** | Before each use | User | WAHR Regulation 4 |
| **Detailed visual inspection** | **6 monthly** (harsh indoor environment) or **12 monthly** (normal indoor) | Competent person | PUWER Regulation 6 |
| **Structural engineer survey** | Every 5 years (recommended best practice) | Structural engineer | PUWER 1998 Reg 6 + industry best practice |
| **Post-event inspection** | After any event liable to jeopardise safety | Competent person | PUWER Regulation 6(3) |

**Key difference from emergency escape ladders:** The 6-monthly inspection for work access ladders is more frequent because regular loading causes wear and deterioration that emergency escape ladders do not experience.

## 6. COMPLIANCE CHECKLIST FOR YOUR 2 M WORK ACCESS LADDERS

### Design and Installation
- [ ] WAHR Schedule 6 justification documented (why a ladder was chosen over stairs/other means)
- [ ] Ladder dimensions comply with BS 4211:2005+A1:2008 (rung spacing 225–300 mm, width >= 300 mm, clearances 200 mm behind/600 mm front)
- [ ] Load capacity adequate for intended use (BS 4211: 1.5 kN per rung)
- [ ] Top landing platform with handholds adequate
- [ ] Base area has adequate clearance and landing space
- [ ] Hoops or safety cage provided (recommended even at 2 m)
- [ ] Top and bottom gates operate correctly (if applicable)

### Legal Compliance
- [ ] WAHR Regulation 4 risk assessment completed for ladder use
- [ ] WAHR Regulation 5 — ladder suitable for purpose
- [ ] PUWER Regulation 5 — ladder maintained in safe condition
- [ ] PUWER Regulation 8 — measures to prevent falls
- [ ] PUWER Regulation 9 — users trained in safe use
- [ ] PUWER Regulation 10 — only trained/authorised users
- [ ] Ladder does not obstruct any fire escape route (RRF(SO))

### Inspection and Maintenance
- [ ] Detailed visual inspection completed (last 6–12 months)
- [ ] Inspection by competent person (see `Competent_Person_Fixed_Ladder_Inspections.md`)
- [ ] Inspection records maintained
- [ ] Defects from last inspection remedied
- [ ] Structural engineer survey completed within 5 years (recommended)
- [ ] Environmental corrosion assessed (indoor environment)

### User Safety
- [ ] Area around ladder base kept clear
- [ ] Adequate permanent lighting at ladder location
- [ ] Users competent to use ladder safely
- [ ] Safe system of work in place (including how to dismount safely at top)
- [ ] No storage of materials near ladder access or landing

## 8. KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **These 2 m work access ladders are subject to stricter regulation than your emergency escape ladders** because they are used for work. WAHR Schedule 6 is the most significant difference — you must be able to justify the ladder over a stair.

2. **The primary standard is now BS 4211:2005+A1:2008** (design and structural specification), with BS 7883:2019 as the inspection methodology. BS 5395-3 was formerly the primary lifecycle standard but has been superseded. This combination applies to both work access and emergency escape ladders, but the **regulatory driver** is different (WAHR vs. RRF(SO)).

3. **At ~2 m height, these ladders do not legally require safety cages/hoops** under WAHR Schedule 6 (which triggers at 3 m), but hoops are good practice even at this height.

4. **Inspection frequency is more frequent** (6–12 months vs. 12 months) because work use accelerates deterioration.

5. **User training is mandatory** for work access ladders (PUWER Regulation 9) — this is not required for emergency escape ladders.

6. **Your inspection contractor can use the same company** for both sets of ladders — the methodology (BS 7883) is the same. But they must apply the **different regulatory framework** (WAHR vs. RRF(SO)) during their assessment.
