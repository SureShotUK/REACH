Permit Application Overview
The grid reference SE 78168 47996 (the outflow of the drain) is in the catchment of the River Derwent. This is a highly sensitive environmental area; the Derwent is a designated Site of Special Scientific Interest (SSSI) and a Special Area of Conservation (SAC) due to its important wildlife and our site is a designated Impact Zone for the SSSI. 
This means that the level of scrutiny from the Environment Agency will be extremely high. Their primary goal will be to protect this sensitive site, so our application must be flawless. It also means that the penalties for non-compliance would be severe, a criminal prosecution in the higher courts with the potential for an unlimited fine and up to 5 years imprisonment. Recent fines for water companies have reached tens of millions of pounds. The EA can impose a stop notice requiring the company to stop the discharge immediately, failure to comply is a criminal offence and will result in further penalties. 
Itâ€™s important to understand how the EA will view the permit application. The reverse osmosis (RO) process concentrates the substances already present in the potable water (like chlorine, salts, and minerals). Discharging this high-salinity, potentially chlorinated water into a small stream is the EA's primary concern, as it can be toxic to aquatic life.
Our application will need to focus on proving we can manage these concentrated substances.

2. Precautions & Testing (Revised)
Our focus shifts from chemical management to proving we can manage the concentration and timing of the discharge.
Key Precautions (Treatment Plan)
1.	De-chlorination (Still Critical): This remains the highest priority. The RO process concentrates the chlorine from the mains water, and this is highly toxic to the aquatic life in the SSSI. We must install an effective de-chlorination system (e.g., an activated carbon filter) on the concentrate line.
2.	Buffer Tank & Controlled Release: This is now the core of the application. You cannot simply discharge the intermittent flow directly. You will need a holding tank to store the concentrate. This tank must be fitted with a system (e.g., a pump or valve on a timer) that slowly bleeds the concentrate into the stream over a 24-hour period. This ensures the stream's natural flow can dilute it, even when you are not operating.
3.	TDS / Salinity Management: This is our main challenge. The concentrate will be a high-salinity 'brine' compared to the freshwater stream. Our entire application will rest on proving that even after the controlled release (precaution #2), the final mixed concentration in the stream is safe.
Required Testing (Evidence)
We need to gather data to build a "mass balance" calculation to prove to the EA that the discharge is safe.
â€¢	Baseline Stream Analysis (Essential): We must commission a water quality survey of the stream. Critically, this must include:
o	Low Flow Data: We need the stream's flow rate during a dry period (its "Q95" flow). This is the EA's worst-case scenario for dilution.
o	Baseline Chemistry: We need to know the stream's existing (upstream) conductivity/TDS, pH, and temperature.
â€¢	Effluent Analysis (Simplified): Because you use no chemicals, this is simpler. We need:
o	A chemical analysis of the incoming mains water (from your water authority).
o	A chemical analysis of your final RO concentrate.
o	This will allow us to state "The concentrate contains only X, Y, and Z, which are present in the original mains water, at 3x concentration."
â€¢	De-chlorination Proof: We must test the water after the de-chlorination system to prove to the EA that the Total Residual Oxidant (TRO) level is below the detection limit (or at a safe level, e.g., <0.01 mg/l).

3. Key Unknowns
The success of the permit now rests on one critical unknown:
â€¢	The Stream's Dilution Capacity: This is the single biggest risk to the project. We know the discharge volume (e.g., 50,000 litres/day). But if the small stream's low-flow rate is, for example, only 100,000 litres/day, there is not enough water to dilute the concentrate safely, and the EA will almost certainly refuse the permit. If the stream's flow is 5,000,000 litres/day, we have a very strong case.
