### Determining the Number of First Aiders: UK Legislation and HSE Best Practice

In the UK, employers have a legal duty under **The Health and Safety (First-Aid) Regulations 1981** to provide "adequate and appropriate" first aid provisions for their employees. This doesn't mean a fixed number for every workplace; instead, it requires a tailored approach based on a comprehensive **first aid needs assessment**. The Health and Safety Executive (HSE) provides guidance to help employers conduct this assessment effectively.

#### The Core Principle: Needs Assessment

The cornerstone of determining first aid requirements is a thorough risk assessment process. This assessment must consider all foreseeable risks and circumstances within your workplace.

**Key Factors to Consider in the Needs Assessment:**

1.  **Nature of Work and Hazards:**
    *   **High-risk environments** (e.g., construction, manufacturing, working with hazardous machinery or chemicals) will require more extensive first aid provisions and a higher number of highly trained first aiders (e.g., First Aid at Work (FAW) qualified).
    *   **Low-risk environments** (e.g., offices, shops, libraries) may require fewer first aiders, potentially qualified in Emergency First Aid at Work (EFAW), or even just appointed persons for very small organisations.
    *   Consider specific hazards like work at height, confined spaces, electrical work, or lone working.

2.  **Number of Employees:**
    *   This is a primary factor, as a larger workforce naturally increases the potential for incidents.
    *   The HSE provides general guidelines based on employee numbers, categorised by risk level (see below).

3.  **Workplace Size and Layout:**
    *   Large sites, multi-story buildings, or dispersed work areas may necessitate more first aiders to ensure that help can reach an injured person promptly.
    *   Consider travel times within the site.

4.  **Work Patterns and Shift Systems:**
    *   Adequate first aid cover must be available during all working hours, including night shifts, weekends, and periods when staff are working alone.
    *   Consider cover for holidays and other absences of first aiders.

5.  **Location and Proximity to Emergency Services:**
    *   If your workplace is remote or far from an Accident & Emergency department, you may need more comprehensive first aid arrangements (e.g., more FAW trained first aiders, or additional equipment).

6.  **Accident History:**
    *   Reviewing your organisation's accident book and incident records can highlight recurring issues or areas where first aid is frequently required, informing future provisions.

7.  **Non-Employees:**
    *   While not a legal requirement under the 1981 Regulations for non-employees, the HSE strongly recommends that the needs of others who may be on the premises (e.g., visitors, contractors, the public, children in schools) are included in the assessment and provisions are made for them.

8.  **Employee Vulnerabilities:**
    *   Consider employees with pre-existing medical conditions or disabilities.
    *   Recent HSE guidance also highlights the importance of considering mental health in first aid needs assessments.

#### General HSE Guidelines for First Aiders

The HSE avoids prescribing exact numbers, but offers general recommendations based on workplace risk and employee count. These are indicative and your needs assessment may dictate different provisions.

**Low-Risk Workplaces (e.g., offices, shops, libraries):**

*   **Fewer than 25 employees:** At least one **Appointed Person**.
*   **25-50 employees:** At least one **Emergency First Aider at Work (EFAW)**.
*   **More than 50 employees:** At least one **First Aider at Work (FAW)** for every 100 employees (or part thereof).

**Medium to High-Risk Workplaces (e.g., factories, warehouses, light engineering, food processing):**

*   **Fewer than 5 employees:** At least one **Appointed Person**.
*   **5-50 employees:** At least one **EFAW** or **FAW** (depending on the potential injuries identified in the assessment).
*   **More than 50 employees:** At least one **FAW** for every 50 employees (or part thereof).

**Important Considerations:**

*   **Cover for Absence:** Always ensure there is adequate first aid cover during all working hours, taking into account annual leave, sick leave, and other absences of your first aiders. This often means having more than the minimum recommended number.
*   **Appointed Person vs. First Aider:**
    *   An **Appointed Person** is responsible for managing first aid equipment (e.g., restocking kits) and calling emergency services. They do **not** need formal first aid training, though basic awareness is advisable.
    *   A **First Aider** has undergone formal training (EFAW or FAW) and is qualified to administer first aid. FAW training is more comprehensive than EFAW.
*   **Training Validity:** First aid certificates are typically valid for three years. The HSE strongly recommends annual refresher training in resuscitation skills.

#### Conclusion

The process of ascertaining the number of first aiders is dynamic and specific to each organisation. It requires a diligent and ongoing first aid needs assessment that accounts for all relevant workplace factors. Employers must document their assessment and subsequent provisions, ensuring they are "adequate and appropriate" to meet their legal obligations and safeguard employee well-being.
