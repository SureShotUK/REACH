# Legionella Prevention and Control: Best Practice and Regulatory Requirements

**Site:** Noxdown Limited, York Road, Pocklington
**Site Type:** Mixed industrial (AdBlue production, warehousing), offices, and welfare facilities.
**Specific Risk Factors:** AdBlue production involves dilution of Urea in water heated to approximately 40°C. This temperature range (20°C - 45°C) is ideal for Legionella proliferation. Separate office and welfare buildings will have their own hot and cold water systems.
**Jurisdiction:** England and Wales
**Prepared:** February 2026
**Primary References:** ACoP L8 (4th Ed., 2013), HSG274 Parts 1–3, Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 (HSWA), Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH).

---

## 1. Introduction and Purpose

This document outlines the best practices and regulatory requirements for the prevention and control of Legionella bacteria at Noxdown Limited's Pocklington site. The aim is to ensure compliance with relevant health and safety legislation and guidance, and to minimise the risk of Legionnaires' disease among employees, contractors, and visitors. Given the presence of water systems, particularly those operating within the Legionella proliferation temperature range (e.g., AdBlue production), a proactive and comprehensive management approach is essential.

---

## 2. Regulatory Framework for Legionella Control

The legal responsibility for Legionella control falls on employers and those in control of premises. The primary legislation and guidance in England and Wales are:

*   **Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 (HSWA):** Requires employers to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare of their employees and others who may be affected by their work activities. This includes managing the risks from Legionella.
*   **Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH):** Legionella bacteria are classified as a hazardous substance under COSHH. This requires employers to assess the risks from exposure to Legionella, and to prevent or control that exposure.
*   **Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR):** Reinforces the requirement for risk assessments and the implementation of effective control measures.
*   **Approved Code of Practice (ACoP) L8: Legionnaires' disease. The control of Legionella bacteria in water systems:** This ACoP provides practical guidance on how to comply with the law. It has a special legal status: if you follow the advice in the ACoP, you will be doing enough to comply with the law. If you choose not to follow the ACoP, you will need to demonstrate that you have complied with the law in some other way.
*   **HSE Guidance HSG274: Legionnaires' disease. Technical guidance:** This guidance is split into three parts, providing more detailed advice on:
    *   Part 1: Evaporative cooling systems
    *   Part 2: Hot and cold water systems
    *   Part 3: Other risk systems (e.g., spa pools, process water systems)
*   **Notification of Cooling Towers and Evaporative Condensers Regulations 1992:** Requires occupiers of premises to notify the local authority of any cooling towers or evaporative condensers on site.

## 3. Responsible Persons and Duties

To comply with ACoP L8, Noxdown Limited must appoint a competent person or persons (the 'Responsible Person' or RP) to manage and oversee the Legionella control scheme.

**Key duties include:**

*   **Duty Holder (Noxdown Limited):** The ultimate legal responsibility for managing Legionella risk rests with the company. This duty cannot be delegated, though tasks can be.
*   **Responsible Person(s):** Appointed by the duty holder with sufficient authority, competence (knowledge, experience, training), and resources to carry out the Legionella control scheme. This person will manage the day-to-day operations of the control scheme.
*   **Competent Support:** Engagement of specialist water treatment companies or consultants may be necessary for tasks requiring specific expertise, such as risk assessments, complex system monitoring, or remedial works.

## 4. HSE's Five Steps to Legionella Risk Assessment (HSWA / COSHH Requirement)

A suitable and sufficient risk assessment is the foundation of effective Legionella control. It must be regularly reviewed and updated.

### Step 1: Identify and Assess Sources of Risk

Identify all water systems that could potentially harbour Legionella bacteria and create a risk of exposure. At Noxdown Limited, this includes:

*   **AdBlue Production Process:**
    *   Water storage tanks used for dilution.
    *   Pipework and associated equipment where water is heated to ~40°C.
    *   Any areas where aerosols (fine water droplets) could be generated (e.g., mixing, spraying, discharge points, ventilation from process).
    *   Infrequently used sections or dead legs within the process water system.
*   **Hot and Cold Water Systems (Offices and Welfare Units):**
    *   Showers, taps, toilets, kitchen facilities.
    *   Infrequently used outlets.
    *   Pipework, including dead legs and redundant pipework.
*   **Other Potential Systems:**
    *   Humidifiers, air conditioning units with water components (if present).
    *   Emergency showers or eye wash stations.
    *   Hose reels.
    *   IBC Wash area.

### Step 2: Prepare a Written Scheme for Preventing or Controlling the Risk

Based on the risk assessment, a comprehensive written scheme must be developed. This scheme details how the identified risks will be managed and controlled. It should include:

*   **System Description:** Detailed schematics or plans of all water systems identified as being at risk.
*   **Safe Operating Procedures:** For all relevant water systems, including the AdBlue production process.
*   **Control Measures:** Specific actions to reduce Legionella growth and dissemination (see Section 5).
*   **Monitoring Program:** Details of monitoring, inspection, and maintenance tasks, including frequencies.
*   **Roles and Responsibilities:** Clear assignment of duties to the Responsible Person and others.
*   **Record Keeping:** Procedures for maintaining accurate records.
*   **Review Process:** Schedule and triggers for reviewing the risk assessment and written scheme.

### Step 3: Implement, Manage and Monitor Precautions

The written scheme must be actively implemented and managed. This involves:

*   **Regular Inspections:** Visual inspections of water systems for condition, temperature anomalies, stagnation, and cleanliness.
*   **System Monitoring:**
    *   **Temperature Monitoring:** Regularly check and record temperatures of hot and cold water at sentinel points and outlets.
        *   Hot water: Should reach 50°C (60°C in healthcare) within one minute at outlets. Storage should be at least 60°C.
        *   Cold water: Should be below 20°C at outlets after two minutes.
        *   AdBlue Process Water: Specific monitoring of the 40°C process water for temperature stability and potential for aerosol generation is critical.
    *   **Flushing:** Infrequently used outlets (e.g., showers, taps in offices or welfare) should be flushed weekly to prevent stagnation.
    *   **Water Treatment:** If chemical treatment is used in any system (e.g., biocide in process water), chemical levels must be regularly monitored.
    *   **System Cleaning and Disinfection:** Regular cleaning and disinfection of storage tanks, calorifiers, and other system components as per the written scheme and guidance.
    *   **Sampling for Legionella:** Routine microbiological sampling for Legionella may be required for high-risk systems, or systems where control is difficult to maintain. This must be conducted by UKAS-accredited professionals and laboratories.
*   **Competent Staff and Training:** Ensure all individuals involved in the control scheme (Responsible Person, maintenance staff, contractors) are appropriately trained and competent.
*   **Communication:** Regular communication with employees about Legionella risks and control measures, especially concerning areas like the welfare unit showers.

### Step 4: Keep Records of the Precautions

Maintain comprehensive and up-to-date records for at least five years. These include:

*   Legionella risk assessments.
*   Written scheme of control.
*   Details of the Responsible Person and their training.
*   Monitoring results (temperatures, chemical levels, flushing logs).
*   Maintenance, cleaning, and disinfection records.
*   Any incidents, corrective actions, and their outcomes.
*   Records of statutory notifications (e.g., cooling towers).

### Step 5: Review the Risk Assessment

The risk assessment and written scheme are living documents and must be reviewed regularly, or whenever significant changes occur. Reviews should take place:

*   **At least every two years, or whenever there is reason to suspect it is no longer valid.**
*   **If there are changes to the water system:** e.g., modifications, extensions, mothballing.
*   **If there are changes to the use of the building:** e.g., changes in occupancy, new processes.
*   **Following any identified control failures or adverse monitoring results.**
*   **Following a confirmed case of Legionnaires' disease** linked to the premises.

## 5. Specific Control Measures for Noxdown Limited

### 5.1 AdBlue Production Process (Water Heated to ~40°C)

The process water heated to approximately 40°C represents a significant risk for Legionella growth and aerosol generation.

*   **Risk Assessment Focus:** A detailed risk assessment specific to this process is paramount, identifying all potential aerosol-generating devices and points of stagnation.
*   **Temperature Control:** While the process requires 40°C, evaluate if this temperature can be maintained consistently and rapidly cycled to higher temperatures (e.g., 60°C for disinfection) during downtime, or if alternative heating methods could reduce the risk. Avoid prolonged periods at 20-45°C without treatment.
*   **Water Quality:** Consider continuous or intermittent biocide treatment (e.g., chlorine dioxide) for the process water if temperature control alone is insufficient or impracticable. Any treatment must be compatible with the production process and end product.
*   **Minimise Stagnation:** Design and maintain the system to prevent water stagnation. This includes:
    *   Minimising dead legs and redundant pipework.
    *   Ensuring adequate flow throughout the system.
    *   Regular flushing or circulation of any standby sections.
*   **Aerosol Control:** Implement engineering controls to minimise aerosol release from the process, such as local exhaust ventilation, covers, or enclosures, especially where workers may be exposed.
*   **Regular Cleaning:** Establish a strict cleaning and disinfection regime for process tanks and pipework.

### 5.2 Hot and Cold Water Systems (Offices and Welfare Units)

These systems, though separate, still pose a risk and require careful management.

*   **Temperature Control:**
    *   Hot water storage: Maintain at least 60°C.
    *   Hot water distribution: Achieve 50°C within one minute at all outlets (60°C in healthcare).
    *   Cold water distribution: Maintain below 20°C at all outlets after two minutes flushing.
*   **Minimise Stagnation:**
    *   **Flushing:** Implement a weekly flushing regime for all infrequently used outlets (e.g., spare office taps, seldom-used showers in the welfare unit). Flush for a sufficient duration to ensure fresh water flows through.
    *   Remove redundant pipework (dead legs) and infrequently used fittings.
*   **Cleanliness:**
    *   Regularly clean and descale showerheads and spray inserts (at least quarterly) to remove biofilm and scale, which can protect Legionella.
    *   Clean cold water storage tanks annually, or as indicated by risk assessment.
*   **Water Storage:** Ensure cold water storage tanks are lidded, insulated, and free from debris and ingress.
*   **Thermostatic Mixer Valves (TMVs):** If present, TMVs should be serviced and disinfected regularly, as the internal components can create areas where Legionella can proliferate if not properly maintained.

## 6. Training and Competence

All individuals involved in Legionella control must be adequately trained and competent for their roles. This includes:

*   **Responsible Person:** Must have a thorough understanding of Legionella risk, the site's water systems, the written scheme, and relevant legislation.
*   **Maintenance Staff:** Training on routine monitoring (e.g., temperature checks, flushing), basic maintenance, and incident reporting.
*   **Contractors:** Any contractors undertaking work on water systems must demonstrate competence and adhere to the site's Legionella control procedures.
*   **General Awareness:** All employees should be made aware of the risks of Legionella and the importance of reporting concerns (e.g., unusual water temperatures, discoloured water).
