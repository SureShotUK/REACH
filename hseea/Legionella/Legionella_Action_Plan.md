# Legionella Outbreak Action Plan

**Location** Noxdown Limited, York Road, Pocklington
**Site Type:** Mixed Industrial and Office
**Jurisdiction:** England and Wales
**Prepared:** February 2026
**Primary References:** ACoP L8 (4th Ed., 2013), HSG274 Parts 1–3, UKHSA PHE Outbreak Investigation Guidance

> **This document is not a substitute for professional legal or specialist water hygiene advice.** In an actual outbreak, retain a competent water hygiene specialist and legal counsel immediately, and follow the instructions of the UK Health Security Agency (UKHSA) Health Protection Team (HPT).

---

## Regulatory Overview

Three distinct legal systems operate simultaneously in a Legionella outbreak. Each triggers separate obligations for the employer:

1. **Health and safety law** — enforced by HSE (industrial/manufacturing areas) or Local Authority Environmental Health Officers (EHOs) (office/commercial areas) under the Health and Safety (Enforcing Authority) Regulations 1998. On a mixed site, both regulators may have jurisdiction over different parts of the premises.
2. **Public health law** — the UKHSA Health Protection Team (HPT) leads the public health investigation, independent of the employer.
3. **Infectious disease notification law** — statutory obligations on registered medical practitioners and diagnostic laboratories.

## Responsible Persons and Duty Holders

### Duty Holder

The duty holder is the employer or person in control of the premises. On a mixed industrial/office site:

- Duties to employees arise under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1974/37/section/2" target="_blank">Section 2 of the Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 (HSWA)</a>.
- Duties to non-employees (visitors, contractors) arise under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1974/37/section/3" target="_blank">Section 3 HSWA 1974</a>.


### Responsible Person (RP)

ACoP L8 requires the duty holder to appoint a competent Responsible Person with:
- Sufficient authority over operational procedures
- Competence (training, skills, and knowledge of the water systems)
- Day-to-day responsibility for implementing the written control scheme

The duty holder cannot delegate the legal duty, only the operational function. The RP may be an employee or a contracted specialist.

### Enforcement Jurisdiction on a Mixed Site

| Area | Enforcing Authority |
|---|---|
| Factory / manufacturing / industrial | HSE |
| Offices, retail, catering, leisure | Local Authority EHO |
| Construction | HSE |

Both HSE and Local Authority EHOs may be involved simultaneously on a mixed site.

## Section 1: Triggering the Response

Initiate the outbreak response when:

- A confirmed case of Legionnaires' disease is identified in a person present on site during the probable exposure window (2 to 10 days before symptom onset; most commonly 5 to 6 days).
- Two or more cases have symptom onset close in time with a plausible shared exposure.
- A water sample returns a Legionella count above the relevant action threshold (see Section 4).
- The HPT, an EHO, or HSE contacts the site regarding a linked case.

**Do not wait for laboratory confirmation before initiating response.** Begin immediately upon reasonable suspicion.

## Section 2: Immediate Actions — First 24 Hours

1. **Convene the internal incident response team** immediately. Include: the Responsible Person; James Spencer, Managing Director; Steve Irwin, Operations Director; Alex Wood, Production Manager; Zak Ali, General Manager; Martin Crowther, Worknest Consultant and Competent Person.

2. **Engage a specialist water hygiene contractor** immediately. If not on a retained contract, engage one now. They should attend site to assess all water systems, take emergency samples, and advise on immediate control measures.

3. **Notify authorities immediately** — do not investigate independently first. Early HPT notification is critical (see Section 3). Attempting to investigate before notifying delays detection of additional cases.

4. **Preserve water systems for investigation** — coordinate with the HPT and enforcing authority before commencing disinfection or remediation, as premature disinfection destroys evidence. Balance this against the ongoing risk to persons on site.

5. **Identify all water systems on site**, including:
   - Cooling towers and evaporative condensers
   - Hot and cold water systems (storage, calorifiers, distribution, outlets)
   - Spa pools, decorative fountains, humidifiers, industrial process water
   - Any systems recently modified, shut down, or operating outside normal parameters

6. **Consider precautionary isolation** of suspect water systems. Where a cooling tower is strongly implicated, shut it down prior to investigation and remediation. For hot and cold water systems where shutdown is not practicable, restrict use of known risk outlets (e.g., showers).

7. **Identify potentially exposed persons** — see Section 5.

8. **Document everything** from this point forward: decisions, timings, persons involved, samples taken, communications, and actions taken.

## Section 3: Public Health Authority Involvement and Notifications

### 3.1 Statutory Notification by the Clinician

The primary statutory notification obligation falls on the **treating registered medical practitioner**, not the employer. Under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2010/659/regulation/2/made" target="_blank">Regulation 2 of the Health Protection (Notification) Regulations 2010</a>:

- The treating doctor must notify the **proper officer of the relevant local authority** (usually the Director of Public Health or a designated Health Protection Officer) as soon as reasonably practicable (orally, due to urgency), with written notification within 3 days.
- Diagnostic laboratories must separately notify UKHSA upon identification of Legionella spp.

The employer does not make this statutory notification — but should not assume it has happened. If the employer becomes aware of a case before the HPT makes contact, contact the HPT proactively.

The UKHSA HPTs lead the public health investigation in England. All 13 regional HPTs operate 24 hours a day, 7 days a week.

- Provide the written Legionella risk assessment, written control scheme, and all monitoring records

### 3.3 Incident Management Team (IMT)

Where two or more cases are epidemiologically linked, the HPT will typically convene an **Incident Management Team (IMT)** to coordinate the investigation. The IMT may include: UKHSA HPT; Local Authority EHO; HSE; NHS microbiology laboratory; UKHSA National Legionella Surveillance Team; senior employer representatives; and clinicians managing affected patients.

The employer's role in the IMT is as an information source and responsible party implementing control measures — not as an investigator of the public health response.

### 3.4 UKHSA Case Definitions

| Definition | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Confirmed case** | Clinical/radiological diagnosis of pneumonia with: positive Legionella culture from a respiratory specimen; or Legionella pneumophila antigen detected in urine; or Legionella nucleic acid detection by PCR. |
| **Probable case** | Clinical/radiological diagnosis of pneumonia without microbiological confirmation, where location and symptom onset meet outbreak-specific exposure criteria. (Applies only once an outbreak has been declared.) |
| **Cluster** | Two or more cases with symptom onset close in time and location. |
| **Outbreak** | Two or more cases sharing an exposure location with symptom onset within 28 days, plus microbiological or strong epidemiological evidence of connection. |
| **Exposure window** | 2 to 10 days before symptom onset. |

### 3.5 Local Authority Environmental Health Officers (EHOs)

EHOs enforce compliance in LA-regulated premises (offices, leisure, retail) and have independent powers including: inspecting premises; collecting water samples; examining records; issuing Improvement and Prohibition Notices. On a mixed site, coordinate with both HSE and the EHO simultaneously.

### 3.6 RIDDOR Notification to HSE

Under <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2013/1471/regulation/9/made" target="_blank">Regulation 9 of RIDDOR 2013</a>:

- When a **confirmed diagnosis** of Legionnaires' disease is received for an employee who worked on water systems likely to have been contaminated with Legionella, report to the relevant enforcing authority **without delay**.
- Report online: <a href="https://notifications.hse.gov.uk/RiddorForms/Disease" target="_blank">HSE RIDDOR Disease Report</a>
- RIDDOR applies to employees with occupational exposure only. The Health Protection (Notification) Regulations cover all cases regardless of their relationship to the premises.
- Both obligations must be fulfilled separately.

## Section 4: Water System Investigation and Remediation

### 4.1 Competent Specialist

All sampling and remediation must be carried out by a competent specialist. The contractor should be UKAS-accredited for water sampling and testing in accordance with BS EN ISO 11731:2017 (Water quality — Enumeration of Legionella). Laboratory analysis must be by a UKAS-accredited laboratory — non-accredited results will not be accepted by regulators.

### 4.2 Action Thresholds — Hot and Cold Water Systems (HSG274 Part 2, Table 2.2)

| Legionella Count (CFU/litre) | Required Action |
|---|---|
| Below 100 | No immediate action. Continue routine monitoring. |
| 100 to 1,000 | Low-level contamination. Resample to confirm. If repeated: review and implement remedial actions. |
| Greater than 1,000 | Significant contamination. Immediate action. Resample immediately. Review all controls and risk assessment. Disinfect system. Retest frequently until sustained control is achieved. |

**In an outbreak context (confirmed human cases):** Do not wait for a count above 1,000 CFU/L. Treat any positive result with urgency. The water system should be considered the likely source until excluded by investigation.

For cooling towers (HSG274 Part 1): counts above 1,000 CFU/L require immediate action; shut down, clean, disinfect, and certify safe before return to service.

### 4.3 Investigation Sampling

Before remediation (subject to HPT/enforcing authority coordination), take samples from:
- Cooling tower basin water, sump, drift eliminators
- Hot water storage cylinders/calorifiers (base and outlet)
- Cold water storage tanks (base and outlet)
- Representative hot and cold water outlets (nearest and furthest from storage)
- High-risk outlets: showers, spray taps, hose reels, industrial process water
- Dead legs, infrequently used outlets, recently modified pipework sections

Samples must arrive at the laboratory within 48 hours of collection. Request molecular strain typing (sequence-based typing or whole genome sequencing) to determine whether environmental and clinical strains match — this is the definitive evidence linking source to cases.

### 4.4 Immediate Control Measures

**Cooling towers (where implicated):**
- Shut down immediately pending investigation.
- Do not hyperchlorinate while running — shut down first to avoid aerosolising contaminated water.
- Clean and disinfect following HSG274 Part 1 (March 2024 edition).
- Do not return to service until disinfected, resampled, and cleared by a competent person.

**Hot and cold water systems:**
- Isolate dead legs, infrequently used outlets, and components showing elevated counts.
- Thermal disinfection: flush all outlets with hot water at minimum 60°C for at least one minute. Flush cold water outlets to confirmed temperatures below 20°C.
- Chemical shock disinfection (hyperchlorination at 20–50 mg/L free residual chlorine, with several hours' contact time, followed by thorough flushing) — planned and executed by a specialist contractor.
- Descale showerheads and spray components: scale and biofilm protect Legionella from disinfection; physical cleaning must accompany chemical treatment.
- Chlorine dioxide treatment may be used where temperature control is difficult to maintain, as it penetrates biofilms more effectively.

### 4.5 Post-Remediation Verification Sampling

- Resample all treated areas within 2 to 5 days of completing disinfection.
- Continue resampling at frequent intervals (at minimum monthly) until sustained results below the action threshold are achieved across multiple consecutive sample sets.
- Retain all sample results as part of the site's permanent records.

## Section 5: Identification of Potentially Exposed Persons

### 5.1 The Exposure Window

The incubation period for Legionnaires' disease is **2 to 10 days** (most commonly 5 to 6 days). For each confirmed case, count backwards 2 to 10 days from the date of symptom onset. All persons present on site during this window must be considered potentially exposed.

### 5.2 Tracing Potentially Exposed Individuals

Working with the HPT, compile:

- **Attendance and access records**: employee attendance records, access control logs, shift rotas.
- **Contractor and visitor records**: visitor books, contractor sign-in records, delivery logs.
- **Public exposure**: members of the public or customers if the site has public areas.

**Identify high-exposure areas**: the HPT and water hygiene specialists will identify the most likely aerosol source areas. Those who worked near cooling tower plumes, used showers, or worked with process water systems are considered higher-risk.

**Identify high-risk individuals** within the potentially exposed population:
- Adults aged over 45 (risk increases with age)
- Smokers and heavy drinkers
- Those with chronic respiratory, kidney, heart, or lung disease
- Diabetics
- Anyone with a compromised immune system (immunosuppressant therapy, chemotherapy, HIV)
- Healthy individuals rarely develop serious disease, but employers must consider vulnerabilities that may not have been disclosed

### 5.3 Medical Surveillance

- **Inform all potentially exposed individuals immediately** (see Section 6) and advise them to seek medical attention if they develop symptoms: high fever, chills, cough, muscle aches, headache, or shortness of breath.
- **Advise employees to inform their GP** of potential exposure so that the Legionella urinary antigen test can be requested promptly if symptoms develop.
- **Provide a letter to employees** confirming the dates of their potential exposure, to present to their GP or at A&E.
- **Facilitate access to occupational health** where available, particularly for those in at-risk groups.
- **Do not conduct your own medical interviews** or contact individual employees' GPs without consent — this is the HPT's function.
- **Cooperate with the HPT's questionnaire exercise** — the HPT will deploy standardised questionnaires to potentially exposed persons. Provide contact information in compliance with UK GDPR (see Section 5.4).

### 5.4 Data Sharing with the HPT

The Health Protection (Notification) Regulations 2010 and associated public health legislation provide a legal basis for sharing employee contact information with the HPT for disease investigation purposes. The UK GDPR exemption at Article 9(2)(i) (processing necessary for reasons of public interest in the area of public health) and the public task basis under Article 6(1)(e) apply to UKHSA's data processing. Document the legal basis for any information shared with the HPT.

## Section 6: Dissemination of Information to Employees and Interested Parties

### 6.1 Legal Obligations

The employer has specific legal duties to inform:

- <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/2002/2677/regulation/12/made" target="_blank">**COSHH Regulation 12**</a>: Employers must provide employees with the significant findings of the risk assessment, appropriate precautions, and actions they should take. If a control failure is identified, employees must be informed **immediately**.
- <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1999/3242/regulation/10/made" target="_blank">**MHSWR Regulation 10**</a>: Employers must provide comprehensible and relevant information on risks to health and safety.
- <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/uksi/1999/3242/regulation/8/made" target="_blank">**MHSWR Regulation 8**</a>: Where employees are exposed to serious and imminent danger, employers must inform them of the nature of the hazard and the protective steps being taken.

There is no exemption permitting an employer to withhold this information. The Barrow-in-Furness inquiry found that the absence of clear communication about Legionella concerns was a fundamental failure contributing to the deaths.

### 6.2 Employee Communication — Immediate (Same Working Day)

Issue a written communication (email, letter, or posted notice) covering all of the following. Verbal-only communication is insufficient as the primary medium.

**1. The situation**
State clearly that Legionnaires' disease has been identified (or is strongly suspected) and is linked to the site water systems. Do not minimise or obfuscate.

**2. What Legionnaires' disease is**
A form of severe pneumonia caused by Legionella bacteria found in water systems. It is **not passed from person to person**.

**3. Symptoms to watch for**
High temperature/fever, chills, cough (initially dry), muscle aches, headache, shortness of breath. Symptoms typically develop **2 to 10 days after exposure**.

**4. What employees must do**
If they develop any of these symptoms — particularly high fever or cough — seek urgent medical attention. Inform their doctor of potential Legionella exposure at work and provide the exposure date range. A letter confirming the dates of exposure will be provided on request.

**5. Steps being taken**
Identify which water systems are being investigated and treated. State which areas or outlets have been taken out of service.

**6. Internal contact**
Provide a named point of contact (HR, Occupational Health, or the Responsible Person) for employees with questions or wishing to report symptoms.

**7. Proportionate reassurance**
Not everyone exposed to Legionella develops disease. Most healthy adults exposed to low concentrations do not become ill. However, early diagnosis is important for successful treatment, which is why prompt reporting of symptoms is essential.

**Accessibility:** If any employees do not read English or have literacy difficulties, make appropriate arrangements for accessible communication.

### 6.3 Ongoing Communications

- Update employees regularly as the investigation progresses — do not go silent after the initial communication.
- Inform employees when water systems are returned to service and what testing confirmed they were safe.
- If additional cases are confirmed, communicate appropriately with all potentially exposed persons, coordinating with the HPT on messaging.

### 6.4 Contractors and Visitors

Contractors on site during the exposure window and visitors with significant exposure (e.g., those who used shower facilities or spent extended time near potential aerosol sources) should also be informed. The HPT will advise on the scope of this communication.

### 6.5 Media and External Communications

Coordinate with the HPT before issuing any public communications or press statements. The HPT will advise on appropriate messaging and may co-author communications. Designate a single spokesperson. Do not make statements to the media without coordinating with the HPT and taking legal advice.

## Section 7: Post-Outbreak Review

### 7.1 Legal Requirement

ACoP L8 requires a review of the Legionella risk assessment and written control scheme:
- Immediately following a suspected Legionella case linked to the site
- Whenever control measures are found to be ineffective
- Whenever there are significant changes to the water system or premises

This is a legal obligation. The review must be documented.

### 7.2 Root Cause Analysis

The review must answer: what failure allowed Legionella to reach levels sufficient to cause infection? Common root causes include:

- Failure to maintain water temperatures (hot water below 50°C, cold water above 20°C)
- Infrequently used outlets and dead legs causing water stagnation
- Failure to clean and disinfect cooling towers or water systems on schedule
- Dosing system failures not detected and acted upon
- Inadequate biocide concentrations in cooling systems
- Scale and biofilm accumulation
- Changes to water systems (modifications, extensions, mothballing) not reflected in the risk assessment

### 7.3 Management System Review

Examine whether the failures resulted from management oversight failures:
- Did the Responsible Person have sufficient authority and competence?
- Was monitoring carried out and records reviewed?
- Was contractor work being verified?
- Were concerns raised by staff or contractors acted upon?
- Was the risk assessment reviewed at the required intervals?

### 7.4 Corrective Action Plan

Produce a documented corrective action plan with, for each identified failure:
- Root cause
- Corrective action
- Person responsible
- Deadline for completion
- Evidence of completion

Implement under senior management oversight.

### 7.5 Record Retention

ACoP L8 requires Legionella-related records to be kept for **at least five years**. Records relating to a disease outbreak should be retained for the duration of any foreseeable litigation or enforcement action — potentially well beyond five years (in the Barrow case, legal proceedings concluded approximately four years post-outbreak).

Retain: all water sampling results; risk assessment and control scheme documents; maintenance and monitoring logs; communications with HPT/HSE/Local Authority and legal advisers; all employee outbreak communications; disinfection records; post-outbreak review report and corrective action plan.
