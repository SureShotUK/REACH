# Near Miss Reporting: Best Practice Guidance

*Written By Steve Irwin;*      *Created: May 2026*  

---

## HSE and IOSH Best Practise

**A business should aim for HIGH near miss reporting — not zero.**

A low near miss count does not indicate a safe workplace. It almost always indicates that staff are not reporting. The hazards are still present; they are simply hidden from management. A business that shows zero near misses is more likely to be at greater risk of a serious accident, not less.

This is the consistent position of the HSE, IOSH, and all major occupational safety bodies.

> *"Near misses should not be ignored or treated lightly, as they can provide valuable insight into how well you are managing health and safety in your workplace."*
> — <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/near-miss-book.htm" target="_blank">HSE Near-miss Book</a>

> *"A pattern of near misses provides an early warning that something needs attention."*
> — <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/near-miss-book.htm" target="_blank">HSE Near-miss Book</a>

## Why Near Misses Matter: Leading vs Lagging Indicators

Safety performance is measured by two types of indicators:

| Indicator Type | Examples | Nature |
|---|---|---|
| **Lagging** (reactive) | Accidents, injuries, RIDDOR reports | Harm has already occurred by the time you know |
| **Leading** (proactive) | Near misses, hazard observations, unsafe act reports | Early warning — allows action before harm occurs |

Near miss counts are a **leading indicator**. They tell you about the health of your safety management system *before* anyone is hurt. Accident counts tell you only that something already went wrong.

The HSE's process safety guidance explicitly supports switching emphasis toward leading indicators:

> *"Switching the emphasis in favour of leading indicators to confirm that risk controls continue to operate is an important step forward."*
> — <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/hsg254.htm" target="_blank">HSG254: Developing Process Safety Indicators</a>

## The Accident Pyramid

Two influential models show the relationship between near misses and serious accidents:

| Model | Near Misses | Minor Injuries | Major Injury/Fatality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heinrich (1931) | 300 | 29 | 1 |
| Bird (1966) | 600 | 30–10 | 1 |

**Important caveat:** The specific numerical ratios in these models are not universally applicable — the relationship between near misses and serious accidents varies significantly by industry, hazard type, and working environment. The HSE does not endorse the pyramid ratios as fixed laws.

However, the underlying principle is valid and widely accepted: near misses occur far more frequently than serious injuries and represent the largest pool of early warning data available to any organisation. Capturing and acting on them is the most cost-effective way to prevent accidents.

## Why Zero-Target Thinking Is Harmful

Setting a KPI to minimise near miss reports creates three specific failure modes:

1. **Suppression** — Staff stop reporting to avoid scrutiny, blame, or the perception that they caused a problem. The hazards remain; they become invisible to management.

2. **False assurance** — Management sees a low number and believes the workplace is safe. They stop looking for hazards. Risk accumulates undetected.

3. **Perverse management incentive** — Managers under pressure to show low numbers may actively discourage reporting, compounding the problem.

The HSE estimates that approximately **90 near misses occur for every accident**. If your reported near miss count is far below what would be expected relative to your accident rate, underreporting is almost certainly occurring.

---

## What to Measure Instead

Do not use raw near miss count as a metric to minimise. Use these instead:

| Metric | What it tells you | Healthy direction |
|---|---|---|
| **Trend** | Is the count rising or falling over time? | Rising early on = improving culture (more confidence in reporting) |
| **Closure rate** | % of near misses that received a documented response | Should be as close to 100% as possible |
| **Near miss : accident ratio** | For every accident, how many near misses were reported? | Higher is better — shows proactive identification |
| **Time to close** | How long from report to corrective action? | Should be falling over time |

A rising near miss count at the start of a reporting programme is a **positive sign** — it indicates staff are gaining confidence that reporting is safe and worthwhile.

## Blame-Free Reporting Culture

Near miss reporting will only work if staff trust that reporting will not result in blame or disciplinary action. The HSE is explicit on this in its accident investigation guidance:

> *"Laying all the blame on one or more individuals is counter-productive and runs the risk of alienating the workforce and undermining the safety culture."*
> — <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/hsg245.htm" target="_blank">HSG245: Investigating Accidents and Incidents</a>

> *"Investigations should be conducted with accident prevention in mind, not placing blame."*
> — <a href="https://www.hse.gov.uk/pubns/books/hsg245.htm" target="_blank">HSG245</a>

Disciplinary action is appropriate only for deliberate or malicious violations — not for honest mistakes, skill errors, or near misses reported in good faith.

## Practical Implementation Checklist

| Action | Notes |
|---|---|
| Make reporting easy | Form should take under 5 minutes to complete |
| Provide multiple channels | Paper form, digital, verbal to supervisor, anonymous option |
| Respond to every report | Acknowledge receipt; communicate what action was taken |
| Investigate root causes | Apply the same rigour as for accidents — what system allowed this? |
| Share learnings | Brief the team on what was found and what changed |
| Review regularly | Near miss data should be a standing agenda item in safety meetings |
| Never punish reporters | Reinforce that reporting is valued and expected |

## Legal Position

There is **no legal requirement** under UK law to record near misses (they are not RIDDOR reportable unless they meet the specific criteria for dangerous occurrences). However:

- Under the <a href="https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/1974/37/section/2" target="_blank">Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974, Section 2</a>, employers have a duty to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety and welfare of employees.
- Near miss reporting is one of the most cost-effective ways to fulfil that duty proactively.
- The HSE expects employers to investigate near misses as part of a functioning safety management system.

The absence of a near miss reporting system — or evidence that it is being suppressed — would be considered a significant management failure by an HSE inspector.
