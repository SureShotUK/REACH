## Oil & Gas — Pre-War Capabilities

**Crude oil production:** ~3.3 million barrels/day (mb/d) total output; ~1.5 mb/d exported. Over 90% of exports purchased by China at a significant discount to circumvent US sanctions. Iran is an OPEC founding member but operates outside normal quota enforcement.

**Natural gas:** World's second-largest proven reserves (32+ trillion cubic metres). **South Pars** — the largest gas field on earth, shared with Qatar's North Dome — had Iranian production of ~2 billion cubic feet/day pre-war, far below its 25–30 bcf/day capacity due to chronic under-investment caused by sanctions. Iran has **no LNG export infrastructure**; Qatar extracts ~9× more from the same reservoir.

**Kharg Island:** Iran's primary export terminal, handling ~90% of all crude oil exports. Storage capacity ~30 million barrels; can simultaneously load 10 supertankers.

Sources: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum_industry_in_Iran" target="_blank">Wikipedia — Petroleum Industry in Iran</a> · <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pars_gas_field" target="_blank">Wikipedia — South Pars Gas Field</a>

---

## Refining

Domestic refining capacity ~1.46 mb/d across six major refineries (Abadan 350k, Isfahan 284k, Bandar Abbas 232k, Tehran 220k, Arak 170k, Tabriz 100k). Despite being a large crude exporter, Iran historically imported refined gasoline due to years of under-investment. The **Mahshahr petrochemical complex** was responsible for approximately 70% of Iran's domestic gasoline supply.

---

## Strait of Hormuz

~15 mb/d of crude and refined products transit daily (2025), representing **25% of all global seaborne oil trade** and **20% of world LNG supply**. It is the only sea route from the Persian Gulf to the open ocean. Closure or disruption directly affects Kuwait, Qatar, Bahrain, UAE, Saudi Arabia, Iraq, and Iran itself.

Source: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strait_of_Hormuz" target="_blank">Wikipedia — Strait of Hormuz</a>

## Sanctions

- **1979–2015:** Progressive US, EU, and UN sanctions over hostage crisis, nuclear programme, and state sponsorship of terrorism.
- **JCPOA (2015):** Iran agreed to cap enrichment at 3.67% and limit centrifuges; sanctions suspended; frozen assets released.
- **2018:** Trump withdrew US from JCPOA; full US sanctions reimposed.
- **October 2024:** Iran placed on **FATF blacklist** (terrorist financing).
- **August–September 2025:** UK/France/Germany triggered the UN **snapback mechanism**; full UN sanctions restored 28 September 2025. China and Russia refused compliance.
- **Current status (2026):** Maximum-pressure sanctions in force across US, EU, and UN. Iran effectively isolated from SWIFT; all major financial institutions prohibited from transacting.

---

## Political Allegiances

**China:** Iran's largest trading partner; 25-year, $400 billion cooperation agreement (2021); buys >90% of Iranian crude exports at discounted prices.

**Russia:** Strategic military partner; supplied 85% of Iran's arms imports historically. Iran supplied Russia with Shahed-136 drones (~$1.75bn agreement); Russia signed $25 billion deal for four nuclear power plants (September 2025). Banking systems linked outside SWIFT.

**BRICS:** Full member since 1 January 2024.

**Axis of Resistance:** Iran funds and arms Hezbollah (Lebanon), Hamas (Gaza), Houthis (Yemen), and Iraqi Popular Mobilization Forces — collectively ~$700m/year (pre-war estimate). The fall of Assad in Syria (December 2024) severed Iran's primary land corridor to Lebanon.

---

## Nuclear Programme

Iran enriched uranium to **60%** from April 2021 (far above the 3.67% JCPOA ceiling). By May 2025, stockpile reached **408 kg of 60%-enriched uranium** — sufficient for multiple weapons if further enriched to weapons-grade (90%). In June 2025, the IAEA found Iran non-compliant with NPT safeguards for the first time since 2005; Iran suspended IAEA cooperation entirely in July 2025. The March 2026 US military campaign included strikes on Iranian nuclear sites.
