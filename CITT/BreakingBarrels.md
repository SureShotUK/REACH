# Breaking Barrels: How a Barrel of Crude Oil Becomes Refined Products

*Reference document for CITT 2026 Fuel Briefing — Portland Fuel Part 2*

---

## The Barrel

A standard barrel of crude oil contains **42 US gallons** (approximately 159 litres). When a refinery processes that barrel, it does not simply split it into equal fractions — the yield of each product depends on three key variables:

1. **The crude slate** — whether the crude is light or heavy, sweet or sour
2. **Refinery configuration** — how complex the processing units are (simple distillation vs. catalytic cracking, coking, hydrocracking)
3. **Market demand** — refineries configure output to match what the local market needs

### Light vs. Heavy Crude

Crude oil is classified as light or heavy based on its **API gravity** — a scale developed by the American Petroleum Institute where a higher number means lower density. Light crude (API above 31°) flows easily, contains a naturally higher proportion of the smaller hydrocarbon molecules that become gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel, and requires less processing. Heavy crude (API below 22°) is thick, viscous, and rich in larger molecules that tend to become fuel oil, asphalt, and other heavy products — unless the refinery invests in conversion units (crackers, cokers, hydrotreaters) to break those molecules down into lighter, more valuable fuels. Canadian oil sands bitumen is among the heaviest crude in the world (API as low as 8°), which is why it requires either upgrading before it enters the pipeline or a highly complex refinery at the other end. By contrast, Brent crude (API ~38°) and Arab Light (API ~33°) are much lighter and yield more transport fuel from the same barrel with less processing effort.

### Sweet vs. Sour Crude

This refers entirely to **sulphur content**, not flavour. Sweet crude contains less than 0.5% sulphur by weight — it was originally called sweet because low-sulphur oil has a mildly pleasant smell. Sour crude contains more than 0.5% sulphur (often 1–3%). Sulphur must be removed before refined products can be sold, because combustion releases it as sulphur dioxide — a regulated air pollutant and the cause of acid rain. Removing sulphur requires dedicated hydrotreating units, adds cost, and consumes hydrogen. A sour crude is therefore worth less than an equivalent API-gravity sweet crude, all else being equal. WCS (Canadian heavy) is both heavy *and* sour — the combination that makes it the most discounted major benchmark in the world. Brent and WTI are both light and sweet, which is why they command the premium prices used as global benchmarks.

**Processing gain:** Refined products occupy *more* volume than the crude they came from. This is because the refinery separates crude into lighter fractions that have lower density. The total volume of refined products typically exceeds the crude input by **3–6%** (the US average processing gain is ~5–6%). This is why a barrel of crude can yield slightly more than 42 gallons of products in volume terms.

**Why the three regions differ:** Canadian refineries process a heavy, oil-sands-influenced crude slate within a North American (gasoline-dominant) vehicle market. European refineries process a lighter North Sea and imported crude slate but serve a market that has historically run more diesel cars and commercial vehicles. Middle Eastern refineries process their own light-to-medium sulphurous crudes and are increasingly export-oriented, with a growing emphasis on naphtha for Asian petrochemical feedstocks.

## 1. Average Canadian Refinery

### Crude Slate

Canadian refineries process a diverse mix:
- **Synthetic crude oil (SCO)** — light product of oil sands upgrading; essentially no sulphur
- **Western Canadian Select (WCS)** — heavy, high sulphur bitumen blend
- **Light sweet crude** — Hibernia (offshore NL), some imported and US Bakken
- **Blended imported crude** — particularly in Atlantic and Quebec refineries

The mix of heavy oil sands crude and lighter synthetics creates a distinctive yield profile: a higher proportion of distillates and asphalt than a refinery processing only light crude.

### Product Yield per Barrel (42 US gallons / 159 litres)

| Product | % of Barrel | Gallons | Litres | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diesel / Distillate fuel oil | **37%** | 15.5 | 58.9 | Road diesel, home heating oil, off-road |
| Motor gasoline | **36%** | 15.1 | 57.2 | Regular and premium unleaded |
| Still gas | **8%** | 3.4 | 12.7 | Refinery fuel; not sold commercially |
| Jet fuel / Kerosene | **5%** | 2.1 | 8.0 | Jet A; some kerosene |
| Asphalt / Bitumen | **5%** | 2.1 | 8.0 | Roads and roofing; reflects heavy crude input |
| Other products | **9%** | 3.8 | 14.2 | Petrochemical feedstocks, lubes, petcoke, sulphur, LPG |
| **Total** | **100%** | **42.0** | **158.9** | |

**Key characteristics:**
- High combined diesel + gasoline output (73%) reflects the North American transport focus
- Asphalt at 5% is a direct result of processing heavy oil sands bitumen — light crude refineries produce little or no asphalt
- Still gas (8%) is consumed within the refinery as fuel; this is higher than European averages, partly reflecting the energy intensity of processing heavier oil sands crude
- Jet fuel at only 5% is notably lower than European and Middle Eastern counterparts

## 2. Average European Refinery

### Crude Slate

European refineries process:
- **North Sea light sweet crude** — Brent, Forties (UK/Norway); declining production but still significant
- **Mediterranean medium crude** — Libyan, Algerian, some Caspian via pipelines
- **Imported medium/heavy crude** — West African, Middle Eastern, some Russian (pre-2022, now largely displaced)

As of recent data: European refineries consume approximately **3.06 million b/d of sweet crude**, 1.19 million b/d of medium crude, and 0.26 million b/d of heavy crude.

### Product Yield per Barrel (42 US gallons / 159 litres)

| Product | % of Barrel | Gallons | Litres | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diesel / Gasoil | **40.2%** | 16.9 | 63.9 | Road diesel, heating gasoil; highest of three regions |
| Gasoline | **18.7%** | 7.9 | 29.7 | Relatively low vs. Canada; reflects diesel car fleet |
| Fuel oil | **10.0%** | 4.2 | 15.9 | Residual fuel; marine bunkers, power generation |
| Kerosene / Jet fuel | **7.7%** | 3.2 | 12.3 | Jet A-1; major aviation hub activity |
| Naphtha | **6.9%** | 2.9 | 11.0 | Petrochemical feedstock; exported to Asia |
| LPG | **2.5%** | 1.1 | 4.0 | Propane, butane; domestic and industrial |

**Key characteristics:**
- Diesel is the dominant product at 40.2% — Europe's historically diesel-heavy car fleet and large trucking sector drive this
- Gasoline yield is the lowest of the three regions (18.7%) — the inverse of the diesel focus
- Fuel oil at 10% reflects marine bunker demand (Rotterdam, Antwerp are major bunkering hubs) and residual power generation
- Naphtha at 6.9% serves European and Asian petrochemical complexes
- "Other" at 14% includes bitumen (roads), lubricants, and refinery own-use fuel

## 3. Average Middle Eastern Refinery

### Crude Slate

Middle Eastern refineries primarily process their own regional crudes:
- **Arab Light** (Saudi Arabia) — API ~33°, sulphur ~1.8%; the global benchmark medium crude
- **Arab Medium / Arab Heavy** (Saudi Arabia) — progressively heavier and more sulphurous
- **Murban** (Abu Dhabi) — API ~40°, low sulphur; lighter and premium
- **Kuwait Export Crude** — API ~31°, sulphur ~2.6%
- **Iranian Heavy / Iranian Light**

Middle Eastern refineries are highly diverse in configuration — from older, simpler distillation plants (high fuel oil yield) to world-scale modern conversion facilities like Kuwait's Al-Zour refinery (615,000 b/d, opened 2022) and Saudi Arabia's Jazan refinery. The figures below represent a weighted average across the regional fleet.

| Product | % of Barrel | Gallons | Litres | Notes |

| Fuel oil / Residuals | **17%** | 7.1 | 27.0 | Declining as refineries upgrade; was 30% in 2010 |
| Gasoline | **20%** | 8.4 | 31.8 | Growing domestic vehicle market in KSA, UAE, Kuwait |

**Key characteristics:**
- Diesel at 30% is the lowest of the three regions — older regional refineries are less optimised for distillate extraction
- Fuel oil at 17% remains elevated versus Europe and Canada, though declining sharply; was ~30% in 2010, ~25% by 2016, now approaching 15–20% as new conversion capacity comes online
- Naphtha at 12% is the highest of the three regions — Middle Eastern producers export significant volumes of naphtha to Asian petrochemical crackers
- LPG at 6% reflects the natural LPG content of Middle Eastern crudes and associated gas processing
- The profile will shift materially over the next decade as new petrochemical-integrated refineries (targeting 25–30% petrochemical yield) come online

## Key Takeaways for the CITT Presentation

1. **Diesel is the dominant product everywhere** — but the degree varies enormously. European refineries are the most diesel-optimised; Middle Eastern the least (though improving)

2. **The current crisis amplifies the structural shortage** — with 20% of global oil off the market via Hormuz, refineries everywhere are running harder on their diesel/gasoil units. The crack spread ($44–50/bbl) reflects this: global refinery output simply cannot cover middle distillate demand

3. **Canada's asphalt yield matters** — at 5% of every barrel, Canadian refineries are producing significant bitumen output from oil sands crude. This is infrastructure-adjacent and not interchangeable with fuel products

4. **Naphtha tells the petrochemical story** — Middle Eastern refineries are deliberately expanding naphtha yield to feed the petrochemical industry. That product competes with diesel/jet for refinery throughput — another structural pressure on transport fuel supply

5. **Still gas is the hidden cost** — Canadian refineries consume 8% of every barrel just to power the refinery itself. This is partly why oil sands refining is so energy-intensive and why Canadian fuel has a higher carbon footprint per litre than oil produced from conventional light crude
