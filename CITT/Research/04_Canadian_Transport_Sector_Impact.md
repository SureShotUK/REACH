## Fuel as a Proportion of Carrier Operating Costs

Fuel is the **single largest variable cost** in road freight operations:

| Cost Category | Share of Operating Costs (typical) |
|---|---|
| **Fuel** | **30–40%** |
| Driver wages & benefits | 35–40% |
| Equipment (depreciation, lease) | 10–15% |
| Maintenance & repair | 5–8% |
| Insurance, tolls, admin | 5–8% |

At current crisis prices (**CAD $2.35/L national average**), fuel is consuming the top end of that 30–40% range for most carriers — and in some cases pushing higher, compressing margins across the board.

**The math for a typical long-haul Canadian fleet:**
- Average fuel economy: ~35–38 litres/100 km (Class 8 tractor-trailer)
- Annual km per truck: ~160,000–200,000 km
- Annual fuel cost per truck at $2.35/L: **~CAD $134,000–$179,000/truck/year**
- At pre-crisis $1.65/L, this was: ~$94,000–$125,000/truck/year
- **The additional annual fuel cost per truck: ~$40,000–$54,000**

For a fleet of 100 trucks, the crisis has added **$4–5.4 million/year** in fuel costs that must be recovered or absorbed.

## Fuel Surcharge (FSC) Mechanisms

Fuel surcharges are the primary mechanism carriers use to pass fuel cost volatility to shippers. Understanding how FSCs work is essential for both carriers and shippers in this audience.

### How FSC is Calculated in Canada

The **Freight Carriers Association of Canada (FCAC)** publishes the reference diesel price used by most Canadian LTL carriers. The FSC percentage is calculated based on a sliding scale tied to the published diesel price.

**FCAC FSC Reference Source:** <a href="https://fcafuel.org/" target="_blank">Freight Carriers Association of Canada: Fuel Surcharge</a>

### Current FSC Rates (Early April 2026)

| Carrier/Segment | FSC Rate | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Canadian LTL (typical) | **26–30%** | Up from ~15–18% pre-crisis |
| BC (BC Container Trucking) | **~30%** | Regulated; March 2026 level |
| Loomis Express (fixed rate customers) | **25.80%** | Late Feb 2026 |
| Loomis Express (volume discount customers) | **32.80%** | Late Feb 2026; higher base rate |
| Rail (CN Rail) | Variable | Weekly adjustment; separate schedule |

**Trend:** BC FSC was 22% in January–February 2026, rose to 30% by March. Expect further adjustments as April prices flow through.

### How FSC Adjustments Lag Price Reality

A critical nuance for this audience: **FSC adjustments typically lag fuel price movements by 2–6 weeks**, depending on carrier tariff terms. This means:

- **Carriers** are temporarily absorbing price spikes before they can adjust FSC
- **Shippers** see FSC increases after the pump price has already risen
- During a rapid price run-up (as seen Feb–April 2026), carriers absorb losses first

## Rail Fuel Surcharge — CN Rail

CN Rail publishes a separate FSC schedule updated weekly based on its internal fuel cost tracking.

<a href="https://www.cn.ca/en/customer-centre/prices-tariffs-transit-times/fuel-surcharge/" target="_blank">CN Rail: Fuel Surcharge Schedule</a>

Rail FSC rates are generally lower than trucking FSC rates as a percentage of linehaul, but rail freight rates overall are lower — so the absolute fuel cost impact is similar per tonne-km.

## Margin Compression — The Carrier Reality

**The squeeze is real.** Carriers face a simultaneous triple challenge in April 2026:

1. **Fuel costs up ~43% year-on-year** — not yet fully recovered through FSC
2. **Freight demand softening** — economic uncertainty (trade war, recession fears) is reducing shipment volumes
3. **Driver wage pressure** — driver shortage has not abated; wage competition continues

**The result:** Many mid-size Canadian carriers are operating at historically thin or negative operating ratios:
- Pre-crisis (2025): typical OR 92–95%
- April 2026: operators reporting OR 96–100%+ (i.e., at or below breakeven)

**Maersk Canada** updated its ground freight fuel fee in March 2026, signalling that even international freight forwarding companies are having to pass through Canadian fuel costs.

Source: <a href="https://www.maersk.com/news/articles/2026/03/30/canada-ground-freight-fuel-fee-update" target="_blank">Maersk: Canada Ground Freight Fuel Fee Update March 2026</a>

## Shipper vs. Carrier Tension

The current environment has sharpened the long-standing tension between carriers and shippers over who absorbs fuel cost:

| Issue | Carrier Position | Shipper Position |
|---|---|---|
| FSC pass-through speed | Immediate — costs are real now | Lag is contractual — pay when terms say |
| FSC transparency | Reflects real pump price index | Distrust: FSC seen as profit centre |
| Fixed-rate contracts | Seeking renegotiation or fuel adjustment clauses | Locked in at agreed rates |
| Spot vs. contract rates | Spot rates spiking; prefer contract | Prefer contract stability; resisting spot |

**The key message for shippers:** Carriers operating at breakeven cannot sustain service quality. Shippers who squeeze FSC too hard risk losing capacity when they most need it.

**The key message for carriers:** Transparent, index-linked FSC is the credible approach. Ad hoc surcharges erode shipper trust.

## Drayage — The Most Acute Pain Point

Container drayage (port to warehouse) is the highest-surcharge segment in the supply chain. Short-haul diesel intensity, high idle time, and congestion at port terminals mean drayage operators face disproportionate fuel exposure.

Source: <a href="https://www.canaangroup.ca/drayage_surcharge/" target="_blank">Canaan Group: Drayage — The Highest Surcharge in the Chain</a>

## Presentation Talking Points

1. **Fuel is 30–40% of carrier operating costs** — the biggest lever in the entire P&L
2. **FSC rates now 26–33%** — that means for every $100 of linehaul freight, $26–$33 is fuel surcharge
3. **The lag problem is real** — carriers absorb price spikes for weeks before FSC catches up
4. **Many carriers are at or near breakeven** — this is a capacity risk for shippers
5. **Shippers: understand FSC, don't fight it** — transparent FSC is the foundation of a healthy carrier relationship during a crisis
6. **The fuel crisis is selective** — drayage and short-haul are hit hardest; long-haul has slightly more recovery mechanism via per-km FSC
