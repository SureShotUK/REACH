# Research: Hedging & Risk Strategies — April 2026

*Research file for CITT 2026 Fuel Briefing, Portland Fuel Part 2.*

## The Risk Management Imperative

In a market where diesel has moved from CAD $1.65/L to $2.35/L in weeks, and where the range of plausible outcomes spans $1.70 to $3.50/L over the next 12 months, **doing nothing is itself a risk decision**. This section covers the tools and strategies available to carriers and shippers to manage fuel price exposure.

**Key principle:** There is no single right answer. The optimal strategy depends on the organisation's:
- Size and financial capacity
- Risk appetite and balance sheet strength
- Existing contractual structure with customers and suppliers
- Operational flexibility (fleet age, routes, modes)

---

## Category 1: Financial Hedging Instruments

These instruments lock in or cap fuel prices through financial markets. Best suited to larger organisations.

### 1.1 Futures Contracts (NYMEX ULSD)

ULSD (Ultra-Low Sulphur Diesel) futures trade on NYMEX (CME Group). A fleet buys futures contracts to lock in the price of diesel for a future month.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | Buy ULSD futures → if prices rise, futures profit offsets higher pump prices |
| **Minimum practical scale** | ~50+ trucks; significant financial reserve required |
| **Basis risk** | NYMEX is USD-denominated; Canadian operators face CAD/USD currency basis risk |
| **Marking to market** | Daily margin calls required; cash flow management essential |
| **Best for** | Large fleets with treasury/finance function; sophisticated risk management |

Source: <a href="https://www.cmegroup.com/education/articles-and-reports/introduction-to-crack-spreads.html" target="_blank">CME Group: Introduction to Crack Spreads</a> | <a href="https://www.mercatusenergy.com/blog/bid/81549/a-beginners-guide-to-fuel-hedging-futures" target="_blank">Mercatus Energy: Beginner's Guide to Fuel Hedging</a>

### 1.2 Fuel Swaps

Swaps are over-the-counter (OTC) agreements to exchange a fixed price for a floating price on a specified volume of fuel.

| Aspect | Detail |
|---|---|
| **How it works** | Agree fixed price with counterparty; if market price rises above fixed, counterparty pays difference; if falls, carrier pays |
| **Settlement** | Typically against monthly average price (not daily) — reduces daily volatility noise |
| **Why swaps over futures** | No daily margin calls; settlement against average price suits fleet fuel purchasing better |
| **Counterparties** | Banks, fuel suppliers, specialist fuel hedging providers |
| **Best for** | Mid-to-large fleets wanting certainty without futures complexity |

### 1.3 Options and Collars

**Call options:** The right (not obligation) to buy fuel at a fixed price. Provides upside protection with unlimited downside benefit.
- **Cost:** Premium paid upfront (currently elevated given market volatility)
- **Benefit:** If prices fall, the option expires worthless — no loss from lower prices

**Collars:** Buy a call option (cap on cost) and sell a put option (floor below which you accept losses). Reduces premium cost; limits both upside and downside.
- **Best for:** Organisations that want cost certainty within a range; not pure speculators

Source: <a href="https://www.breakthroughfuel.com/blog/fuel-hedging-strategies-for-market-stability/" target="_blank">Breakthrough: Fuel Hedging and Risk Management Strategies</a>

## Category 2: Physical/Commercial Strategies

More accessible to smaller operators; no financial markets required.

### 2.1 Fixed-Price Supply Contracts

Negotiate a fixed diesel price with a supplier for a defined volume and period (typically 30–90 days).

| Pros | Cons |
|---|---|
| Price certainty; simple | Supplier charges premium for taking risk |
| No financial sophistication required | Miss out if market prices fall |
| Accessible to small fleets | Volume commitment — penalties for shortfall |
| Canadian suppliers offer these | Currently limited availability due to market volatility |

**Note:** In April 2026's highly volatile environment, fixed-price contracts are harder to source and carry higher premiums. Suppliers are reluctant to commit. Best strategy may be to wait for Q2 peak to pass before locking in.

### 2.2 Fuel Cards and Bulk Purchasing

- **Fuel cards** with negotiated discount programs (e.g., Petro-Canada, Shell, Esso commercial cards)
- **Bulk tank purchasing** with periodic fixed-price fills (30–60 day horizon)
- Practical for fleets of any size; no financial sophistication required
- Typical discount vs. retail pump: **2–5 cents/L**; meaningful but not a hedge

### 2.3 Fixed-Volume Procurement at Spot with Timing Strategy

- Purchase larger volumes when prices dip (tactical purchasing)
- Requires bulk storage capacity
- Effective for smaller/medium fleets without financial hedging access

## Category 3: Fuel Surcharge Design as a Risk Tool

The FSC program is the carrier's primary revenue-side hedge. A well-designed FSC transfers price risk to shippers — but only if the mechanism is well-structured.

### What Makes a Good FSC Program

| Element | Best Practice | Common Mistake |
|---|---|---|
| **Reference index** | FCAC published price; Natural Resources Canada weekly index | Using unverifiable internal cost data |
| **Adjustment frequency** | Weekly or bi-weekly | Monthly lags too far behind in volatile markets |
| **Transparency** | Published schedule freely available to shippers | Opaque; shippers assume padding |
| **Communication** | Proactive notice before increases | Surprise invoice additions |
| **Lagged vs. real-time** | Real-time (2-week lag) | 4-6 week lag — carrier absorbs spike |

**Key message for carriers:** Your FSC program is an asset. Treat it as such. Transparent, credibly-indexed FSC earns shipper trust and reduces negotiation friction.

**Key message for shippers:** Understanding your carrier's FSC methodology is not optional. If you don't know how it's calculated, you can't evaluate whether it's fair — and you risk making poor sourcing decisions.

## Category 4: Operational Efficiency as a Structural Hedge

The most durable fuel risk mitigation is simply using less fuel. In a $2.35/L environment, every litre saved has double the value of a litre saved at $1.65/L.

### Proven Operational Levers

| Strategy | Typical Fuel Saving | Investment Level |
|---|---|---|
| Aerodynamic improvement (side skirts, fairings) | 5–10% | Moderate |
| Cruise control / speed management (<100 km/h) | 8–15% | Low (policy) |
| Driver training and eco-driving programs | 5–12% | Low |
| Fleet renewal (newer engines, EV for urban) | 10–25% | High |
| Route optimisation software | 3–8% | Moderate |
| Load optimisation (cube/weight) | 5–10% | Low–Moderate |
| Idle reduction technology | 3–6% | Moderate |
| Modal shift (where feasible — road to rail) | 30–60% per tonne-km | High (logistical) |

**The compounding effect:** A carrier implementing aerodynamics + speed management + driver training can realistically achieve **15–25% fuel reduction**. At $2.35/L, that's **CAD $20,000–$45,000/truck/year** saved.

## Strategy Matrix by Fleet Size

| Fleet Size | Recommended Priority |
|---|---|
| **Small (1–25 trucks)** | 1) Fuel cards/discounts 2) Operational efficiency 3) Strong FSC program 4) Bulk purchasing |
| **Medium (26–100 trucks)** | 1) Well-structured FSC 2) Operational efficiency 3) Fixed-price contracts when available 4) Explore swaps |
| **Large (100+ trucks)** | 1) Structured hedging (swaps/options) 2) Treasury-managed program 3) Operational efficiency at fleet scale 4) Best-in-class FSC |

## For Shippers — The Other Side of the Table

Shippers often focus on negotiating down FSC rates. In a crisis market, this is counterproductive. The better approach:

1. **Understand your carriers' fuel cost exposure** — ask for their FSC methodology; it's reasonable to request
2. **Help carriers hedge** by offering longer contract terms with pricing adjustment mechanisms
3. **Evaluate your own modal mix** — is there a more fuel-efficient option for some lanes?
4. **Consider fuel cost in total landed cost** — cheap carriers with low FSC but poor reliability cost more overall
5. **Scenario plan your freight budget** — model H1 vs. H2 fuel cost at different price scenarios

## Presentation Talking Points

1. **Doing nothing is a decision** — companies that have not reviewed their fuel risk strategy since 2024 are already behind
2. **Right tool for the right size** — futures for large fleets, swaps for mid-size, operational efficiency for all
3. **FSC is a hedge, not a surcharge** — reframe this for the carrier audience; it's your primary revenue-side risk management tool
4. **The efficiency opportunity is real** — 15–25% savings available through operational discipline; pays back immediately at $2.35/L
5. **Now is not the time to lock in long-term fixed prices** — wait for Q2 peak to pass; buying insurance at the top of the crisis is expensive
6. **For shippers: work with your carriers** — carriers at breakeven are a capacity risk; FSC transparency builds the partnership you need
