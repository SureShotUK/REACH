# Research: Forecasts & Scenario Modelling — April 2026

*Research file for CITT 2026 Fuel Briefing, Portland Fuel Part 2.*

---

## The Forecast Challenge

Making fuel price forecasts in April 2026 is uniquely difficult. The Strait of Hormuz crisis has introduced the largest supply shock to global oil markets since the 1970s. Forecasting institutions are working with highly uncertain assumptions about:

1. How long the Hormuz closure/restriction persists
2. How quickly damaged Gulf energy infrastructure can be restored
3. Whether the conflict escalates, stabilises, or resolves
4. How global demand responds to higher prices and economic uncertainty

**The honest framing for the CITT audience:** No one knows with confidence. But we can model plausible scenarios with specific price implications — and that is more useful than a single-point forecast that will almost certainly be wrong.

## Three Scenario Model

### SCENARIO 1 — BULL CASE: "Prolonged Crisis"
*Higher prices; conflict persists or escalates*

**Trigger conditions:**
- Hormuz remains effectively closed beyond June 2026
- Iranian/Gulf production does not recover materially
- Conflict widens; additional infrastructure damaged
- SPR releases exhaust; OPEC+ unable to compensate with non-Gulf capacity

**Price implications:**

| Metric | Bull Case |
|---|---|
| Brent crude | $130–$150+/bbl |
| Canadian diesel pump | CAD $3.00–$3.50/L |
| ULSD crack spread | $55–$75/bbl |
| Probability (Portland Fuel estimate) | ~20–25% |

**Impact on Canadian logistics:**
- FSC rates approaching 40–45% of linehaul
- Carrier bankruptcies among undercapitalised fleets
- Freight rate spike as capacity exits market
- Shipper pain on all modal options simultaneously

---

### SCENARIO 2 — BASE CASE: "Gradual Resolution"
*EIA and Goldman Sachs consensus; prices ease through 2026*

**Trigger conditions:**
- Hormuz restrictions ease by May–June 2026 (partial reopening)
- Gulf production recovers gradually — 6.7 Mb/d lost capacity in May, near-normal by Q4
- Diplomatic progress contains conflict without further escalation
- Global demand softens modestly due to economic uncertainty

**Price implications:**

| Metric | Base Case |
|---|---|
| Brent crude | Peak ~$115 Q2, ease to $70–$85 by Q4 |
| WCS (Canada) | $55–$70/bbl by Q4 (discount of $12–$18 to WTI) |
| Canadian diesel pump | CAD $2.00–$2.20/L by Q4 2026 |
| ULSD crack spread | Normalise to $25–$35/bbl |
| Probability (Portland Fuel estimate) | ~50–55% |

**Impact on Canadian logistics:**
- Relief from current extremes, but Q2 remains very painful
- Carriers who survive Q2 see improving margins in H2 2026
- Shippers benefit from falling FSC in H2; freight rates ease

---

### SCENARIO 3 — BEAR CASE: "Swift Resolution + Demand Shock"
*Lower prices; conflict resolves quickly; recession risk materialises*

**Trigger conditions:**
- Rapid diplomatic breakthrough; Hormuz fully reopens by May
- OPEC+ unity breaks; members overproduce to recapture market share
- US tariff war deepens global recession; freight demand falls sharply
- Energy transition spending accelerates; oil demand outlook revised down

**Price implications:**

| Metric | Bear Case |
|---|---|
| Brent crude | $55–$70/bbl by H2 2026 |
| Canadian diesel pump | CAD $1.70–$1.90/L by Q4 |
| ULSD crack spread | $18–$22/bbl (near normalised) |
| Probability (Portland Fuel estimate) | ~20–25% |

**Impact on Canadian logistics:**
- Fuel cost relief — but freight volumes collapsing due to recession
- Net margin impact is mixed or negative (volume decline > fuel savings)
- Carrier revenue falls; low fuel price doesn't rescue revenue per km
- Shippers benefit on fuel cost but face own demand/revenue pressures

## Canadian-Specific Variables

These factors modify the international price scenarios for Canadian operators:

| Variable | Current State | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **CAD/USD** | 0.72 (1 USD = 1.39 CAD); CAD weak | Amplifies crude price in CAD terms; 1¢ CAD move = ~1–1.5¢/L diesel |
| **TMX Pipeline** | ~400 kb/d to Asia | Canadian oil now valued globally; WCS discount could narrow |
| **Carbon levy** | Federal + provincial layers in pump price | Adds ~$0.17–$0.22/L floor to Canadian diesel vs. US price |
| **Refinery capacity** | 17 refineries, 90% utilisation | Limited ability to run harder to increase domestic diesel supply |
| **Atlantic import dependency** | NS, NL, NB partially import-dependent | Exposed to global product spot prices |

## What to Watch

**Leading indicators** the CITT audience should track to know which scenario is materialising:

1. **Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic** — real-time tanker AIS data (MarineTraffic)
2. **EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report** — US distillate inventory level vs. 5-year average
3. **ULSD crack spread** — if falling toward $25–30, base case is playing out
4. **CAD/USD rate** — a strengthening CAD reduces domestic fuel cost independently of crude
5. **OPEC+ statements** — any formal production increase signals bear-case risk
6. **IEA Oil Market Report** — monthly; gold standard for supply/demand balance

## Presentation Talking Points

1. **Three scenarios, not one forecast** — this is the honest approach and will resonate with a sophisticated audience
2. **Base case: peak Q2, ease H2** — consistent with EIA and Goldman; CAD diesel falls from $2.35 toward $2.10–$2.20 by year end
3. **Bull case is plausible** — 20–25% chance Hormuz stays closed; this audience needs to plan for it
4. **Bear case would be a mixed blessing** — cheap fuel + recession = volume collapse; not necessarily good news for carriers
5. **Watch the indicators** — give the audience the dashboard to track scenarios themselves
