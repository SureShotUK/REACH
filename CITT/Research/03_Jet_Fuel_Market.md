## Why Jet Fuel Matters to This Audience

Jet fuel (Jet A / Jet-1 / kerosene-type) is directly relevant to logistics decision-makers for two reasons:

1. **Air cargo costs** — high-value, time-sensitive freight (pharma, electronics, perishables) ships by air. Jet fuel drives airline cargo pricing, which affects shipper costs.
2. **The fuel price premium signal** — jet fuel crack spreads are a leading indicator of broader refined product tightness. When jet fuel is expensive relative to crude, it tells the same story as diesel crack spreads: refineries cannot make enough product to meet demand.

## Jet Fuel vs. Diesel — The Crack Spread Comparison

Both diesel and jet fuel are **middle distillates** — they compete for the same refinery throughput. This is a critical structural point for the presentation:

| Product | Crack Spread (vs. crude) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ULSD Diesel | $44–$50/bbl | Historic high (crisis-driven) |
| Jet Fuel (Jet A) | Typically $3–$8/bbl above diesel | Similar crisis premium |

**Why this matters:** Refineries choose their output mix based on relative margins. When both jet fuel and diesel are commanding extreme premiums over crude, it means:
- Global refinery capacity cannot keep up with demand for middle distillates
- Neither airlines nor truck fleets can expect relief from the product side, even if crude eases

## Impact on Air Cargo Pricing

**Airline fuel surcharges** on cargo are recalculated frequently (typically weekly or monthly) based on published jet fuel price indices. With jet fuel at ~$209/bbl, shipper-facing air cargo fuel surcharges have increased substantially.

**Practical implications for logistics managers:**
- Air freight costs have increased **25–40% above pre-crisis levels** for most routes
- Shippers with time-sensitive freight are absorbing higher air surcharges
- Some shippers are modal-shifting from air to ocean/road where timelines permit
- Air cargo capacity from Middle East hubs (Dubai, Doha) is significantly disrupted

---

## Canadian Air Cargo Context

Canada's major air cargo hubs (Toronto Pearson, Vancouver, Montreal) do not face direct operational disruption but:
- Connecting flights through Middle Eastern hubs are rerouting
- International import air freight costs are elevated (pharmaceutical, electronics supply chains)
- Carriers Air Canada Cargo and WestJet Cargo are passing through fuel cost increases via surcharges

## Presentation Talking Points

1. **Jet fuel at ~$209/bbl globally** — up sharply from ~$155/bbl pre-crisis
2. **Same root cause as diesel** — middle distillate shortfall; refineries can't cover both
3. **Air cargo surcharges elevated 25–40%** — relevant to pharmaceutical, electronics, and perishables shippers in this audience
4. **Modal shift pressure** — high air costs are pushing more freight to road and ocean, which adds to diesel demand
5. **Refineries face a zero-sum choice** between making jet fuel and diesel — tight market for both is structural, not temporary
