# Canada: Crude Oil & Refined Products — East vs. West Self-Sufficiency Analysis

*Reference document for CITT 2026 Fuel Briefing. Data: 2023–2024 unless noted. See also: `Canada_Oil_Briefing.md` for upstream production, pipeline, and reserves background.*

---

## 1. The Core Insight: Two Different Energy Systems

Canada's oil and refined product supply chain splits cleanly along a geographic fault line running roughly through Ontario. West of that line, Canada is one of the most energy-secure regions on earth. East of it, the picture looks more like a mid-sized island nation dependent on seaborne supply.

| | **Western Canada** | **Eastern Canada** |
|---|---|---|
| **Provinces** | AB, SK, BC | ON, QC, NB, NS, NL, PEI |
| **Crude source** | 100% domestic pipeline | Mix: domestic pipeline + seaborne imports |
| **Seaborne crude dependency** | Zero | ~270–400 kb/d (NB: 100%; QC: partial) |
| **Product self-sufficiency** | High | Moderate to low |
| **SPR / emergency reserve** | None (commercial storage only) | None |
| **Vulnerability to maritime disruption** | Very low | High to critical |

### 2.4 Seaborne Disruption Impact: Western Canada

**Impact: Minimal.** A sustained disruption to global seaborne oil trade would:
- Have no effect on crude supply to western refineries
- Cause some tightening of BC refined product imports (37 kb/d — manageable via diversion from Alberta)
- Result in price increases (global crude price spike feeds through to WCS and WTI) but no physical supply shortage

## 3. Eastern Canada: The Seaborne-Dependent System

### 3.1 Crude Supply

Eastern Canadian refineries were built to process light, sweet crude and have limited or no pipeline connection to Alberta's heavy oil sands production. They rely on a combination of US pipeline crude and — critically — seaborne imports.

| Province | Refinery Crude Source | Volume (kb/d) | Seaborne? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ontario** | US crude via Enbridge Line 9 (reversed 2015) + some domestic | ~87 | Partial pipeline; US-origin crude, not seaborne |
| **Quebec** | Enbridge Line 9 (domestic/US mix) + marine top-ups | ~126 | Partially seaborne |
| **New Brunswick** | 100% marine imports — NO pipeline connection | ~270 | **Fully seaborne** |
| **Atlantic other** | Marine terminal imports | Small | Seaborne |
| **Eastern total** | | **~490** | **~55–60% seaborne** |

**The grade mismatch explains everything:** Alberta produces heavy, sour bitumen (API 8–20°, sulphur 3–4%). Eastern refineries were designed for light, sweet crude (API 30–38°, sulphur <0.5%). Retrofitting a single eastern refinery with a coker to handle heavy crude costs approximately CA$2 billion. No eastern refinery has made that investment, and without it they cannot use Alberta crude. The absence of an east-west pipeline compounds this — even if eastern refineries could handle oil sands crude, there is no pipeline to deliver it.

**Enbridge Line 9:** Reversed in 2015, this line carries ~300 kb/d from Sarnia, ON westward to Montreal — but in the reversed direction it moves crude eastward to Ontario and Quebec refineries. It is a partial solution for Ontario and Quebec but does not reach New Brunswick.

## 6. Strategic Petroleum Reserve

**Canada has no government-held Strategic Petroleum Reserve.** It is the only G7 member without one.

| G7 Member | Govt. SPR | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| United States | Yes | ~350 million bbl (post-2022 drawdown from 690M bbl peak) |
| Japan | Yes | ~150+ days supply |
| Germany / France / Italy / UK | Yes | IEA 90-day obligation compliant |
| **Canada** | **No** | **Zero government-held barrels** |

**Why:** The IEA 90-day stockholding obligation applies only to net *importers*. Canada, as a massive net crude exporter (~4.6 MMb/d), is exempt. Emergency response relies on the Energy Supplies Emergency Act (ESEA) — demand restraint and voluntary commercial inventory releases only.

**The gap:** Canada has no direct lever to surge supply to eastern Canada in a domestic disruption, nor to contribute barrels to allied nations during a collective IEA emergency action. Its contribution to the 2022 IEA response to Russia's Ukraine invasion was demand-side only.

Sources: <a href="https://www.iea.org/articles/canada-s-legislation-on-oil-security" target="_blank">IEA: Canada's Legislation on Oil Security</a> | <a href="https://www.biv.com/news/resources-agriculture/what-is-a-strategic-oil-reserve-and-does-canada-have-one-11988971" target="_blank">Business in Vancouver: Does Canada Have a Strategic Oil Reserve?</a> | <a href="https://www.todayville.com/canadas-embarrassing-oil-vulnerability-exposed-only-g7-nation-with-no-strategic-petroleum-reserve/" target="_blank">Todayville: Canada's Oil Vulnerability — Only G7 Nation with No SPR</a>

## 7. Seaborne Disruption Risk Analysis

### 7.1 Scenario: Sustained Disruption to Seaborne Crude Supply to Eastern Canada

The current Hormuz crisis does not directly route crude to Canada — but global maritime disruption causes **tanker market tightening, freight rate inflation, and competition for light sweet cargoes** that eastern Canadian refineries need. A more direct scenario: a prolonged disruption to US Gulf Coast or Atlantic Basin tanker operations (hurricane season, port closures, escalating conflict).

| Province / Facility | Days to Supply Crisis | Mitigation Available | Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Irving Saint John, NB** (320 kb/d) | **~7–14 days** (est. tankage at Canaport) | None — no pipeline backup; no domestic crude alternative | **Critical. No fallback.** |
| **Quebec refineries** | ~14–21 days | Partial: Enbridge Line 9 from Ontario/US; limited volume | **High risk** |
| **Ontario refineries** | ~21–30 days | US pipeline via Line 9 (US-origin crude); more resilient | **Moderate risk** |
| **Western refineries** | No timeline | Pipeline supplied; no seaborne exposure | **No risk** |

### 7.2 Irving Saint John: The Critical Single Point of Failure

Irving Oil Saint John is the **most seaborne-exposed major refinery in North America**:
- 320 kb/d capacity — Canada's single largest refinery
- **Zero pipeline connection** — Canaport marine terminal is the only crude supply route
- Serves Atlantic Canada and exports significant volumes to the US northeast
- In a seaborne disruption, it would need to reduce throughput within 1–2 weeks and cease operations within weeks unless crude inventory is sufficient

The Canaport terminal can handle Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCCs). Irving sources crude from the US Gulf, West Africa, Saudi Arabia, and North Sea on a spot and term basis — competing directly in the global market for the same light sweet grades that Asian and European refineries seek.

**During the current Hormuz crisis (April 2026):** Irving is paying elevated freight rates and competing harder for West African and US Gulf cargoes as Asian demand for alternative supply has intensified. This is a direct financial impact even though Hormuz is not an Irving supply route.

### 7.3 Refined Product Supply: Eastern Canada in a Disruption

Eastern Canada also imports ~146 kb/d of refined products nationally (2024), with Quebec the dominant importer. A disruption to seaborne product supply would:

| Product | Eastern Canada Risk | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Jet fuel** | High — 31.5% of RPP imports (est. ~46 kb/d seaborne) | Eastern airport operations threatened; YUL, YYZ particularly exposed |
| **Gasoline** | Moderate — 20.8% of RPP imports (~30 kb/d) | Pump price spikes; regional shortages in Quebec/Atlantic |
| **Diesel** | Lower — eastern Canada is a net diesel producer | Irving and Quebec refineries export diesel; disruption to their crude means diesel production falls |

### 7.4 The Circular Risk

The most insidious element: **if Irving loses crude supply and shuts down, eastern Canada loses its primary diesel and gasoline producer**. Irving's 320 kb/d output of refined products serves Atlantic Canada, Quebec (partially), and US northeast markets. A seaborne crude disruption to Irving does not just cut crude inflows — it eliminates the largest single source of refined product supply for the region. Eastern Canada would then need to import the products it previously made domestically, at precisely the moment global product markets are tightest.

### 7.5 Structural Mitigations (Current and Proposed)

| Mitigation | Status | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **East-west crude pipeline (e.g. Energy East)** | Cancelled 2017; informally under reconsideration (2025–26) | Would eliminate Irving's seaborne dependency; ~1.1 MMb/d capacity proposed; 10+ years to build if approved |
| **Irving coker investment** | No announced plans | Would allow Irving to process heavy oil sands crude if a pipeline existed |
| **Canadian SPR** | Not planned | No government proposal on the table as of April 2026 |
| **US supply via Line 9** | Operational — provides some Ontario/Quebec buffer | Does not reach NB; limited volume |
| **Commercial inventory build** | Market-driven | No mandated minimum stockholding; Irving and Quebec refiners manage their own stock levels |
| **Demand rationing (ESEA)** | Emergency authority exists | Last-resort tool; politically difficult |

## 8. Summary: Self-Sufficiency by Region

| Question | Western Canada | Eastern Canada | National |
|---|---|---|---|
| Crude self-sufficient? | Yes — massive surplus | No — ~490 kb/d seaborne imports | Net surplus but regionally split |
| Right crude type? | Yes (refineries built for heavy) | No — need light sweet; cannot use Alberta heavy | Grade mismatch is structural |
| Diesel self-sufficient? | Broadly yes | Yes — Irving/QC are large producers; seaborne crude disruption changes this | Near self-sufficient nationally |
| Gasoline self-sufficient? | Broadly yes | No — modest import dependency | Modest net importer |
| Jet fuel self-sufficient? | No (BC marine imports) | No — significant deficit | Only ~67% self-sufficient nationally |
| Seaborne disruption risk | Very low | **High to critical (Irving)** | Concentrated in NB/QC |
| Govt. emergency reserve | None | None | **Only G7 nation with no SPR** |
