# Physical Oil & Products Market — Hormuz Disruption Analysis
### Portland Fuel — Internal Reference Document
*Updated: 22 April 2026 | Scenario: Strait of Hormuz closed or severely restricted*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The partial and intermittent closure of the Strait of Hormuz since late February 2026, now compounded by a US naval blockade of Iranian ports (from 13 April), represents the most severe supply shock to global oil products markets since the 1973 Arab embargo. Total flows through the Strait have collapsed from **~20.1 million b/d** pre-war to approximately **3.8 million b/d** — a disruption of roughly 16 million b/d, equivalent to the combined output of Saudi Arabia and Iraq.

This document analyses the physical product market implications by product, by region, and with specific focus on UK exposure and shortage risk timelines. It assumes Hormuz remains shut or severely limited — i.e., no durable resolution or full passage normalisation in the near term.

**Headline conclusions:**

| Product | UK Shortage Risk | Estimated Onset |
|---|---|---|
| Jet fuel | **CRITICAL — already acute** | Now (stocks depleting) |
| Diesel/gasoil | **HIGH** | 6–10 weeks |
| LPG (residential) | Low (North Sea insulated) | >3 months |
| LPG (industrial/autogas) | Medium | 8–12 weeks |
| Petrol/gasoline | Low (UK net exporter) | Unlikely within 3 months |
| Naphtha | Medium-High (feedstock) | 4–8 weeks |
| Fuel oil/HSFO | Medium (power gen risk) | 6–10 weeks |

## 1. SCALE OF THE HORMUZ DISRUPTION

### Pre-crisis flows (Q1 2025 baseline)

| Commodity | Volume | % of Global Seaborne Trade |
|---|---|---|
| Crude oil + condensate | ~15.0 mb/d | ~34% of global crude trade |
| Refined products | ~3.3 mb/d | ~8–10% of global products trade |
| LPG | ~1.5 mb/d | ~16% of global LPG trade |
| LNG | ~35% of global LNG trade | — |
| **Total hydrocarbons** | **~20.1 mb/d equivalent** | **~25% of global seaborne oil** |

Post-blockade (April 2026 estimate): approximately **3.8 mb/d** transiting — meaning roughly **16.3 mb/d** is displaced or landlocked.

Crude export collapse from the region: from ~18.7 mb/d to ~5.9 mb/d — a **68% decline**. Refining capacity in the Gulf is simultaneously constrained; an estimated **3–4 mb/d of regional refining throughput** has been shut or curtailed due to damaged infrastructure and crude supply interruption.

### Why products are more acute than crude

Crude-on-crude substitution is relatively elastic — importers can switch to West African, Latin American, or North Sea grades. Refined products are not interchangeable in the same way: diesel specifications, cetane ratings, sulphur content and cold flow properties vary; jet fuel requires tight cert of conformance; and alternative supply chains take weeks to months to establish. The net effect is that the **product market tightness is more severe and more immediate than the crude tightness** for most Western importers.

## 2. MIDDLE EAST REFINING — WHAT HAS BEEN LOST

The Gulf is home to some of the world's largest and most complex export-oriented refineries. Much of their output transited Hormuz to Asia and, to a lesser extent, Europe.

| Refinery | Country | Capacity (b/d) | Key Products Exported |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ruwais (ADNOC) | UAE | 922,000 | Diesel, gasoil, jet, naphtha, LPG, fuel oil |
| KNPC complex (Al-Ahmadi/Mina Abdullah/Shuaiba) | Kuwait | 940,000 | Diesel, naphtha, jet, LPG |
| Ras Tanura/Jubail (Saudi Aramco) | Saudi Arabia | 550,000–1.2 mb/d combined | Diesel, naphtha, jet, LPG, petrochemicals |
| BAPCO | Bahrain | 267,000 | Diesel, naphtha |
| Sohar (OQ) | Oman | 230,000 | Diesel, fuel oil, naphtha |
| South Pars condensate refineries | Iran | ~400,000 | Condensate splits, naphtha (now struck) |

The UAE's **Ruwais refinery** is the single most important export-oriented refinery in the Gulf, producing approximately 40 million tonnes of products per year, the majority of which transited Hormuz. Kuwait's refineries — major suppliers to the UK and South Asia — are similarly constrained. Saudi export capacity at Ras Tanura and Jubail is partially reroutable via the Red Sea/Yanbu pipeline system, but volumes are limited.

**Key constraint:** Even where refineries are physically operational (Saudi, UAE, Oman), the inability of product tankers to load and clear the Strait means output is either stockpiling onshore, being flared/curtailed, or supplied to domestic markets. As of mid-April 2026, approximately **426 tankers** plus a further ~53 LNG/LPG carriers are at anchor in Gulf waters awaiting safe passage — with the total across all vessel types exceeding 800 at peak.

## 3. PRODUCT-BY-PRODUCT ANALYSIS

---

### 3.1 DIESEL / GASOIL

#### Global trade flows (pre-crisis)
Middle East diesel exports accounted for approximately **860 kb/d** of global seaborne gasoil/diesel trade. Primary recipients:
- **Asia** (India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, East Africa): ~500 kb/d
- **Europe** (via Suez): ~200 kb/d, predominantly from Kuwait and UAE
- **East Africa / Red Sea basin**: ~100 kb/d

#### Current constraints
Diesel is the most critically short product globally. The convergence of three factors has tightened the market to multi-year extremes:
1. Gulf export collapse (~860 kb/d lost from Middle East)
2. Refinery throughput cuts as crude feedstock availability is disrupted
3. Surge in demand for industrial diesels (military, logistics, power generation backup across the region)

Distillate crack spreads at NY Harbor reached **$1.42/gallon** in March 2026 — double the five-year average of $0.68/gallon and the highest since the 2022 Ukraine-driven energy crisis. European diesel premiums (ICE gasoil vs Brent) have tracked similarly.

#### Current constrained regions (diesel)
- **South Asia** (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka): Critically exposed. India imports approximately 60% of its domestic LPG/diesel needs via Hormuz; industrial diesel rationing has begun in multiple states.
- **East Africa**: Heavily dependent on Gulf product imports; Somalia, Kenya, Tanzania experiencing acute shortages.
- **Southeast Asia**: Singapore as a re-exporter is managing, but downstream markets (Myanmar, Cambodia, Philippines) are tightening.
- **Japan and South Korea**: Both have significant strategic reserves and alternative US/Indian supply access, but costs have surged.

#### Regions likely to experience shortages next
- **South Asia** is already in shortage. The next frontier is **sub-Saharan Africa** and parts of **Latin America** that relied on arbitrage cargoes from the Gulf.
- **Europe** is currently managing through US Gulf Coast and Indian imports, but these sources are approaching capacity ceilings.

#### Diesel price effects
- ICE gasoil forward curve in deep backwardation, signalling acute spot tightness
- Prompt M1 prices elevated $15–25/bbl over pre-war levels
- ULSD/HVO differential widening as biodiesel feedstock (palm, soy) supply chains also disrupted by shipping rerouting costs

---

### 3.2 JET FUEL / AVIATION KEROSENE

#### Global trade flows (pre-crisis)
The Gulf was the world's largest net exporter of jet fuel, particularly:
- **Kuwait**: Major jet exporter to Asia and Europe via long-term supply agreements
- **UAE (Ruwais)**: Significant Jet A-1 exporter
- **Saudi Arabia (Jubail SATORP)**: Jet exports to Asia

Global jet fuel seaborne trade through Hormuz: approximately **600–700 kb/d** pre-crisis.

#### Current constraints — MOST ACUTE PRODUCT
Jet fuel is the tightest market globally and the product most likely to drive visible public impacts in the UK. Key indicators:

- ARA jet fuel stocks at **6-year lows** as of March/April 2026
- Jet crack spread surged to **$80/barrel** — approximately double the pre-war level
- Benchmark Jet A-1 CIF NWE prices spiked to **nearly $1,800/tonne** (early April 2026 peak) vs ~$800/tonne pre-war
- IEA notes jet demand is particularly inelastic at short notice — airlines cannot easily reduce schedules, and jet has few substitutes

Jet fuel is geographically concentrated in its supply chain in a way that diesel is not. The US Gulf Coast does not export significant jet volumes to Europe (US domestic aviation demand absorbs most domestic jet production). India's Jamnagar and Nayara complexes can produce jet but the certification, specification, and logistics pipeline to deliver into European airports takes weeks to establish.

#### Currently constrained (jet)
- **ARA hub** (Amsterdam-Rotterdam-Antwerp): already critically tight; stocks at 6-year lows
- **Singapore hub**: constrained but managing via alternative Pacific Rim sources
- **Middle East hub airports** (Dubai, Doha, Riyadh): severe self-supply disruption; Gulf carriers operating with reduced uplift per flight and increased fuel stops

#### Regions likely to experience shortages next
- **UK** (see Section 5 below) — highly exposed via Kuwait dependency
- **Southern European airports** (Madrid, Rome, Athens) — dependent on Gulf cargoes via Suez
- **East African aviation hubs** (Nairobi, Addis Ababa)

### 3.3 PETROL / GASOLINE / NAPHTHA

#### Global trade flows
The Hormuz closure has a more muted direct effect on petrol than on middle distillates, for two reasons:
1. The Middle East is not a major petrol exporter to Europe — most Gulf gasoline went to Asia and US West Coast
2. Europe's petrol balance is structurally long; several major European refineries are net gasoline exporters

#### Naphtha — the feedstock story
The more significant issue on the light end is **naphtha**. The Gulf exports large volumes of naphtha (condensate splitter output and reformer naphtha) primarily to:
- **South Korea and Japan** (petrochemical feedstock for steam crackers)
- **India** (blending and petrochemicals)

Naphtha prices spiked from **$776/tonne** (6 March 2026) to over **$1,000/tonne** within approximately one week — a ~30% surge driven by Gulf feedstock supply collapse. The Asian petrochemical complex is severely feedstock-constrained. This has knock-on effects for plastics, fertilisers, and chemical production chains globally.

#### Petrol-specific UK risk
The UK is a **net gasoline exporter** — Fawley and Humber produce more petrol than UK demand absorbs, and export surplus to West Africa and the US. UK petrol supply is primarily sourced from domestic refinery output and NW European (ARA) traded volumes. Direct UK petrol shortage from Hormuz closure is **unlikely within a 3-month horizon** unless:
- UK refineries experience crude feedstock disruption (possible but not currently acute — UK crudes are North Sea and North American)
- Naphtha cracker feedstock shortages reduce blendstock availability

---

### 3.4 LPG (PROPANE / BUTANE)

#### Global trade flows
Hormuz is the dominant chokepoint for global LPG trade. Pre-crisis volumes: approximately **1.5 mb/d** — around **16% of global LPG seaborne trade**.

Key exporters through Hormuz:
- **Saudi Arabia (Ras Tanura / Yanbu)**: Largest LPG exporter globally; c. 400 kb/d
- **Qatar**: Major propane exporter alongside LNG
- **UAE (ADNOC)**: Significant LPG streams from Ruwais

Key importers:
- **India**: ~60% of domestic consumption from imports; 90% of imports normally via Hormuz — extreme vulnerability
- **China, Japan, South Korea**: Major LPG importers for petrochemical feedstock and residential cooking gas
- **East Africa and South Asia**: residential LPG cylinders

#### Currently constrained (LPG)
- **India**: Residential LPG cylinder shortages are already being reported in rural areas; government has suspended export allocations; subsidy bill rising sharply
- **Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka**: All acutely exposed; some industrial LPG users switching to alternatives
- **Japan/South Korea**: Managing through strategic stocks and US propane imports (US is world's largest LPG exporter via Gulf Coast/Marcus Hook)

#### Alternative supplies
Saudi Arabia exports significant propane via the **Petroline / East-West Pipeline** to Yanbu on the Red Sea — partially bypassing Hormuz. This provides some relief but the volumes are materially smaller than Ras Tanura's total LPG export capacity. Qatar's LPG/LNG exports through Hormuz are fully blocked.

US Gulf Coast propane exports to Europe and Asia are increasing rapidly. However, the VLGCs (Very Large Gas Carriers) required to move US propane volumes are in extremely high demand and the fleet cannot scale overnight.

---

### 3.5 FUEL OIL / HIGH SULPHUR FUEL OIL (HSFO) / VLSFO

#### Global trade flows
Singapore and the UAE (Fujairah) are the world's two largest bunkering hubs. Fujairah bunker volumes have effectively ceased due to the conflict. Singapore is managing but feedstock supply is constrained.

The impact is primarily on:
- **Marine bunkering costs**: HSFO and VLSFO premiums elevated; Cape rerouting is burning significantly more bunker fuel per voyage, creating demand pull simultaneously with supply disruption
- **Power generation**: Several Gulf states and South Asian economies use fuel oil for power generation backup; with the grid stressed by the conflict, demand is elevated

The Middle East also exported approximately **500–600 kb/d** of fuel oil into the global bunker market. This supply has largely been removed, tightening bunker markets globally.

For the UK, direct fuel oil exposure is limited. The UK does not rely on Gulf fuel oil for power generation. However, elevated bunker costs are feeding into shipping freight rates that affect all import costs.

## 4. TRADE FLOW REROUTING — LOGISTICS & FREIGHT

### Cape of Good Hope routing

With Hormuz effectively closed and the Red Sea also disrupted (Houthi activity), vessels rerouting from the Gulf to Northwest Europe must take the **Cape of Good Hope** route. This adds:

- **+16–32 extra days** for Aframax/LR-class product tankers vs Suez routing
- Total voyage time NW Europe → Gulf → NW Europe: **~41 days** (vs 25 days via Suez)
- Extra distance: **3,500–4,000 nautical miles** per voyage
- Additional fuel cost: approximately **$932,905 per Aframax voyage**

The effective result is that a fleet of, say, 100 LR tankers dedicated to this trade can now make roughly 60% of the voyages per year that they could previously. This alone constitutes a **~40% reduction in available freight capacity** for Middle East-to-Europe product trade, even before accounting for the loss of cargo availability from Gulf refineries.

### War risk insurance

War risk insurance premiums on Gulf of Oman / Hormuz transits escalated from a pre-crisis norm of **~0.125–0.25% of vessel value** to **1–5%** by mid-March 2026, with some underwriters quoting up to 10% for specific transits at the height of hostilities. By late March/April, premiums moderated to approximately **0.8–1%** but remain several times pre-crisis levels. Many P&I clubs and underwriters are declining cover entirely for Hormuz transits; the practical effect is that despite nominally "open" passage declarations, most commercial operators are not sending vessels through. For a VLCC (valued at ~$120m), the April 2026 rate of ~1% represents an additional $1.2m per voyage — versus ~$150,000–300,000 pre-war.

### Tanker market dynamics

| Vessel Class | Gulf Exposure | Earnings Change |
|---|---|---|
| LR2 (product tanker) | 44% from Gulf | –44% to –45% wk/wk earnings |
| LR1 (product tanker) | 33% from Gulf | –48% to –50% wk/wk earnings |
| MR (medium range) | 9% from Gulf | –25%; averaging ~$46k/day |

Paradoxically, earnings are DOWN because tankers cannot get cargoes out of the Gulf, not because demand has fallen. The vessels are trapped, at anchor, or rerouting empty. Meanwhile MR tankers are commanding very strong rates on Atlantic Basin routes (US Gulf to ARA, India to ARA) as demand for alternative supply channels surges.

LR2 Gulf-UK/Continent rates: **$91.11/mt** — significantly elevated versus the pre-war norm of ~$35–45/mt.

## 6. UK-SPECIFIC EXPOSURE

### UK refinery landscape (April 2026)

**Operating refineries:**
- **Fawley** (ExxonMobil) — 270,000 b/d; Southampton Water; major UK refinery; runs North Sea/North American crude
- **Humber / Lindsey** (Phillips 66) — ~221,000 b/d; Humberside; North Sea crude
- **Pembroke** (Valero) — ~270,000 b/d; West Wales; diverse crude slate
- **Stanlow** (Essar) — ~296,000 b/d; Merseyside; runs a variety of North Sea and Atlantic Basin crudes

**Recently closed:**
- **Grangemouth** (closed April 2025, converted to import terminal July 2025): Loss of ~200,000 b/d throughput capacity — approximately 20% of UK refining capability. Scotland now entirely dependent on imports.
- **Lindsey** (closed 2024): Further reduction in UK self-sufficiency

Combined UK refinery throughput of approximately **1.0–1.1 mb/d** against UK demand of **1.4–1.5 mb/d**, leaving a structural import deficit of approximately **300,000–400,000 b/d** which is covered by imports, primarily from ARA and direct shipment.

**Can UK refineries increase runs?** Marginally. All four remaining refineries are running at high utilisation given elevated crack spreads. The crude they run (North Sea, US, West African) is unaffected by Hormuz closure. They cannot materially offset the import deficit without additional crude availability, which is not the constraint.

### 6.1 UK JET FUEL — CRITICAL EXPOSURE

**The UK's single greatest physical product vulnerability is jet fuel.**

- Kuwait is the **single largest source of UK jet fuel imports at 38%** of total supply (~4.1 million tonnes/year pre-crisis)
- Saudi Arabia is the second largest source
- Together, Gulf producers account for approximately **55–60%** of UK jet fuel imports
- ARA hub jet stocks are at **6-year lows**
- The UK is a net jet fuel importer; domestic refinery output does not cover aviation demand
- Jet fuel has **no short-term substitution** in commercial aviation; aircraft cannot switch fuels

**Alternative sources:**
- **US Gulf Coast jet exports to Europe**: Running at record highs (~200 kbd in April 2026) but cannot fully replace ~600 kbd Middle East regional exports; US domestic aviation demand competes for this supply
- **India (Jamnagar/Nayara)**: Can produce Jet A-1 but logistics pipeline to deliver into UK airports is weeks in the making; EU/UK spec requirements and certification take time; $150–200/tonne cost premium
- **No meaningful European spare**: France, Netherlands, Germany are all competing for the same reduced supply pool

**UK jet shortage timeline:**
- **Immediate** (now): Jet available but at significantly elevated prices; airlines are paying; surcharges applied
- **4–6 weeks** (June 2026): ARA stocks reach critically low levels; jet allocation tightening; airlines begin restricting cargo and reducing non-essential sectors
- **6–8 weeks** (June–July 2026): Physical rationing risk at UK airports; Heathrow, Gatwick, Manchester exposed first due to volume; regional airports potentially face allocation squeezes earlier due to supplier prioritisation
- **8–12 weeks** (July–August 2026): If no resolution, likely scenario involves government intervention — mandatory stock drawdown, priority allocation to domestic/essential routes, suspension of non-essential aviation fuel uplift

*This is the highest-confidence shortage risk facing the UK and the one with the shortest timeline.*

### 6.2 UK DIESEL — HIGH RISK

**UK diesel import structure:**
- **Netherlands**: ~21% of imports (ARA hub; highly liquid, alternative-sourceable)
- **United States**: ~35% (ULSD from US Gulf Coast refineries via transatlantic runs; currently at record export levels)
- **Belgium**: ~16% (ARA catchment)
- **Kuwait**: ~14% directly; plus additional Gulf-origin volumes cleared via ARA

Of the approximately 300–400 kb/d of diesel the UK imports, roughly **14–20%** has direct Gulf/Hormuz exposure. The remainder is sourced from ARA, US, and European refineries that are themselves under pressure from redirected demand.

**Key risk factors:**
1. **ARA hub tightening**: As South and Southeast Asian markets bid aggressively for non-Gulf diesel, ARA cargoes that would normally flow to the UK are being bid away eastward. The UK competes with the world for the same marginal barrel
2. **US ULSD supply ceiling**: US Gulf Coast diesel exports have surged (167 → 396 kbd to Europe, +137%) but face capacity constraints due to competing Latin American demand. US exports are near or at practical ceiling
3. **Indian supply ramp-up**: Jamnagar's diesel exports to Europe hit 3 MMt in September 2025 (5-year high) and are likely running higher now. However, EU sanctions on Russian crude inputs to Jamnagar's SEZ (effective December 2025) complicate the situation and may constrain throughput
4. **Grangemouth/Lindsey closures**: Scotland is now 100% import-dependent and competes for the same reduced supply pool

**UK diesel shortage timeline:**
- **Current** (April 2026): Diesel available at elevated prices; HGV operators, agricultural users, construction sector absorbing +20–35p/litre on wholesale
- **6–8 weeks** (May–June 2026): Wholesale availability tightening; forecourts maintaining supply but wholesale lead times extending; some regional distribution stress
- **8–12 weeks** (June–July 2026): If Hormuz remains shut AND US/Indian alternatives cannot scale: physical shortage risk in Scotland, Northern Ireland, and remote areas first; then urban logistics chains
- **12–16 weeks** (July–August 2026): Rationing risk for non-priority users; government likely invokes emergency powers under Petroleum Act 1998 / Energy Act 2016 if supply falls more than 15% below normal

*Diesel shortage risk is real but more buffered than jet, given greater supply source diversity and existing US ramp-up. The 8–12 week window is the critical period.*

---

### 6.3 UK PETROL — LOW RISK

The UK is a structural **net exporter** of petrol/gasoline. Fawley and Humber refineries produce surplus gasoline relative to UK demand. The primary Hormuz-related risk to UK petrol is indirect:

- **Naphtha feedstock squeeze**: UK petrochemical plants (Grangemouth's chemicals unit, even after refinery closure, and smaller facilities) use naphtha as cracker feedstock. Gulf naphtha is now largely unavailable; North Sea-sourced naphtha prices have risen sharply in sympathy
- **Blendstock availability**: Some ethanol and specialist blendstocks have supply chain implications
- **Octane economics**: Isomerate and alkylate markets are tight globally

**Verdict**: UK petrol supply at forecourts is not at material risk within a 3-month Hormuz closure horizon.

---

### 6.4 UK LPG

UK LPG supply is dominated by **North Sea production** (primarily propane/butane from CATS, SAGE, and NSMP pipeline systems landing at Teesside and Grangemouth). This makes UK LPG supply largely **insulated from Hormuz closure** for:
- Bulk residential propane (heating oil replacements, off-grid heating)
- Autogas (LPG vehicles)
- Agricultural LPG

The risk is:
- **Import dependency for industrial / petrochemical LPG**: Some UK industrial users and terminals import propane/butane from the Gulf when North Sea supply is insufficient. These cargoes are now disrupted
- **Price**: Even with insulated physical supply, UK propane/butane prices track international benchmarks; CP (Contract Price) for propane has surged alongside global LPG disruption
- **Aerosol and refrigerant grade butane**: Specialist grades partly sourced from Gulf

**Verdict**: Residential and transport LPG physically secure. Industrial users facing price shock but not physical shortage risk in the near term.

There is a structural gap that cannot be fully covered without demand destruction or drawdown of strategic stocks.

## 8. STRATEGIC PETROLEUM RESERVES

### UK SPR holdings
- **Total**: ~68 million barrels (as of latest IEA reporting)
  - Crude: ~38 MMbbl
  - Products: ~30 MMbbl
- **Days of import cover**: ~60–90 days (68 days at 1.5 mb/d consumption)
- **Structure**: Mostly held by private companies under government obligation (NESO / DESNZ oversight); not all held in product form usable immediately

### IEA emergency release
On **11 March 2026**, the IEA announced a coordinated emergency SPR release of **400 million barrels globally** — the largest ever. UK/EU contribution: approximately 20% (~80 MMbbl equivalent). This is a significant signal but at global consumption rates of ~100 mb/d, 400 MMbbl represents only **~4 days of global consumption**.

Strategic stock releases can smooth price spikes and bridge short-term physical gaps but **cannot substitute for Hormuz access over a multi-month horizon**.

### Practical UK stock drawdown
If the UK began drawing on strategic reserves for jet and diesel at a rate sufficient to maintain current consumption, stocks would deplete at:
- **Jet fuel**: UK holds approximately 5–6 days of jet cover in strategic reserves specifically; commercial stocks add perhaps 10–15 more days of uplift
- **Diesel**: Better positioned; strategic + commercial stocks represent approximately 30–40 days of cover

The 60–90 day total IEA-compliant stock figure is a **blended average** — specific product categories (especially jet) have much lower cover than the aggregate implies.

- Diesel cracks have doubled but remain below 2022 peak (~$60+/bbl at height of Ukraine crisis)
- Naphtha has swung from negative to strongly positive — Asian steam crackers are now in severe feedstock competition with European gasoline blenders for the same barrel

### Forward curve structure
- Middle distillates (diesel, jet) in **steep backwardation** — front months commanding $8–12/bbl premium over 6-month forward — signalling acute near-term physical scarcity, not a structural long-term shift (market expects resolution)
- LPG (Mt Belvieu propane) in **strong backwardation** similarly
- Petrol market in modest contango — no acute near-term tightness

### UK retail price implications
- At current wholesale rates, UK retail diesel would expect to trade at approximately **175–185p/litre** (vs ~140p pre-war)
- Jet fuel wholesale prices imply airline fuel surcharges of **£50–120 per long-haul sector** — airlines are adding surcharges but not all have passed through full costs
- LPG bulk prices for residential users: up approximately 30–40% on contract prices

## 11. KEY CONCLUSIONS

1. **Jet fuel is the UK's most acute and immediate vulnerability.** Kuwait alone supplies 38% of UK jet imports; ARA stocks are at 6-year lows; no short-term substitute. Physical rationing risk at UK airports within 6–8 weeks if no Hormuz resolution.

2. **Diesel is the second major risk.** The UK's greater supply diversification (US, ARA, Dutch, Belgian sources) provides a buffer, but these sources are all simultaneously under pressure. Scotland and Northern Ireland — now entirely import-dependent following Grangemouth's closure — are the first-failure points. National diesel constraints could emerge within 10–14 weeks.

3. **The Grangemouth closure has materially worsened the UK's position.** The 20% reduction in domestic refining capacity removed the UK's key buffer; Scotland is now the same position vis-à-vis product supply as a landlocked state.

4. **Alternative supply exists but cannot fully cover the gap.** US and Indian exports can provide approximately 400–600 kbd additional European supply, against a deficit of 600–800 kbd. There is a structural uncoverable gap of 100–300 kbd that must be met by demand destruction or stock drawdown.

5. **Strategic stocks provide a bridge, not a solution.** UK jet-specific stocks provide approximately 15–20 days of cover. Diesel stocks are better (~35–40 days) but not infinite. IEA-coordinated release provides days of global cover, not weeks.

6. **Price shock is already here; physical shortage is 4–12 weeks away for key products.** Businesses and consumers should plan for diesel at 175–185p/litre and continued aviation fuel surcharges as the floor scenario; physical allocation risk is the tail scenario within 8–12 weeks.

7. **A ceasefire / Hormuz resolution changes the picture significantly but not instantly.** Even if Hormuz fully opened tomorrow, the 870 tankers at anchor in the Gulf would take 2–4 weeks to load, clear, and deliver product to ARA and UK terminals. The supply pipeline lag means relief is always 3–5 weeks behind any diplomatic resolution.
