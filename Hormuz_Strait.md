# Strait of Hormuz: Strategic Briefing

*As of 6 April 2026. Trade flow figures are 2024 unless noted.*

---

## What Is It?

The Strait of Hormuz is a 21-mile-wide navigable channel between the Persian Gulf and the Gulf of Oman — the **only maritime exit** for Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, Iran, Bahrain, and the UAE. There is no LNG pipeline bypass of any kind. It is the single most important energy chokepoint on earth.

## Europe's Exposure to Hormuz

Europe is **not heavily dependent on Hormuz for crude oil**, but is significantly more exposed on refined products — particularly after switching away from Russian supply following the 2022 invasion of Ukraine.

### Crude Oil

~6% of EU crude imports transit Hormuz. Europe's top crude suppliers are the US (~16%), Norway (~14%), Kazakhstan (~12%), and Saudi Arabia (~8%). A Hormuz closure would be manageable on crude alone — alternative sources exist.

### Diesel / Middle Distillates

**Highest area of post-2022 vulnerability.** As Russian diesel was sanctioned from early 2023, Europe turned to Middle Eastern refineries. Kuwait became a major supplier — reaching **14% of UK diesel imports** in 2024 (up sharply from near-zero in 2021). Across the EU, over **25% of diesel/gasoil imports** (excluding intra-EU trade) now originate from Hormuz-transiting refineries, equating to approximately **8–9% of total EU diesel consumption**.

### Jet Fuel

Europe's most acute exposure. Persian Gulf refineries — particularly in Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and UAE — are the dominant global exporters of aviation fuel. A Hormuz closure eliminates approximately **21% of global seaborne jet fuel supply** and would create an estimated **300,000 b/d shortfall** for Europe, with 247,000 b/d normally destined for Northwest Europe alone. Analysts estimate **25–38% of Europe's jet fuel** currently originates from Persian Gulf supply chains transiting Hormuz.

Source: <a href="https://www.kpler.com/blog/markets-in-motion-where-can-europe-secure-jet-fuel-to-replace-the-mideast-gulf" target="_blank">Kpler — Where can Europe secure jet fuel to replace the Middle East Gulf? (March 2026)</a>

### LNG / Natural Gas

Qatar supplies all of its LNG through Hormuz. Pre-2022, Qatar supplied ~20% of Europe's LNG. Since then, US LNG has become Europe's dominant source (~48% of EU LNG in 2023), and Qatar's share has fallen to **~10% of European LNG imports**. A Hormuz closure would therefore jeopardise approximately **10% of Europe's LNG supply** — significant but not catastrophic at the aggregate EU level.

Country-level exposure is more acute. Italy sources ~45% of its LNG from Qatar/UAE; Belgium ~38%; Poland ~38%.

Sources: <a href="https://ieefa.org/resources/strait-hormuz-disruption-would-jeopardise-10-europes-lng-imports" target="_blank">IEEFA — Hormuz disruption and Europe's LNG (2024)</a> · <a href="https://www.euronews.com/my-europe/2026/03/31/strait-of-hormuz-shutdown-what-implications-for-europe-for-how-long-and-how-high-can-price" target="_blank">Euronews — Hormuz shutdown: implications for Europe (March 2026)</a>

## Pipeline Bypass Capacity

| Pipeline | Max Capacity | Recent Throughput | Bypasses Hormuz? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Saudi East-West (Petroline)** | 7.0 mb/d | ~2.9 mb/d (Mar 2026) | Yes → Red Sea / Yanbu |
| **UAE ADCOP (Habshan–Fujairah)** | ~1.8 mb/d | ~1.6 mb/d | Yes → Gulf of Oman |
| **Iraq–Turkey (Kirkuk–Ceyhan)** | 1.6 mb/d | ~0.2 mb/d (conflict-constrained) | Yes → Mediterranean |
| **Iran Goreh–Jask** | ~0.3 mb/d | Effectively zero | Yes → Gulf of Oman |
| **LNG bypass** | None | N/A | **No bypass exists** |

Combined realistic bypass: **~4–5 mb/d** against ~20 mb/d transiting the strait — less than 25% of current flows. The Saudi East-West pipeline hit its 7 mb/d design capacity in March 2026 but is running well below that. **No LNG bypass of any kind exists under any scenario.**

## Current Status (April 2026)

The Strait remains physically open but is under acute threat. Iran has partially restricted access, blocking vessels from "hostile" nations while allowing others. Trump has threatened to strike Iranian power plants and energy infrastructure if the Strait is not fully opened by **7 April 2026, 8 PM ET**. Brent crude has risen from ~$65/barrel pre-war to a peak of ~$126, settling around $100–108/barrel as of early April 2026. The US and Israeli militaries destroyed Iran's navy (150+ vessels) and struck its primary gas infrastructure at South Pars (−12% output). Kharg Island's oil export facilities have been deliberately left intact by US forces.
